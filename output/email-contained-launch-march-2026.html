That is what we spend locking a child in a room and calling it justice. A cell, a guard, a system that sends 84% of kids straight back through the same door.

$75 a day is what works. Community programs. Real ones. Running right now, in places most policy documents have never visited.

One container. Three rooms. Thirty minutes. Touring the country.

The first room belongs to young people who have been inside the system. They decide what you see. What you feel. You walk into their version of it. Not a presentation, not a panel, not their quote buried on page 47 of a report. Theirs.

The second room is what the alternative looks like overseas. Spain's Diagrama Foundation runs therapeutic centres where reoffending drops below 14%. Family contact every week. Young people who look like children, because that is what they are.

The third room is whoever is already doing the work in that town, that community, right now. Underfunded. Unreported. Invisible to the people writing policy. Every stop, a different organisation gets the light.

We are building it while we go, because the people who need to see it cannot wait for us to get it perfect.

Across this country, communities have been solving the problem that detention never could. Quietly. Without press releases. With $75 a day and the kind of knowledge that does not come from a policy document.

JusticeHub exists to make that visible.

It is not a charity. It is not a think tank. It is infrastructure. A living platform connecting the people doing the work, documenting what is actually working, and putting that evidence in front of the people making decisions.
