## The Shift

The research is strong. It explains remand, history, bail law, First Nations overrepresentation, cost, harm, and alternatives.

But an exhibition cannot begin with a research brief.

It has to begin with a child who has not been sentenced.

Not a named child without consent. Not a trauma role-play. Not a visitor pretending to be locked up. A human pathway: one young person, one charge, one missing address, one impossible condition, one court delay, one night that becomes the first door into detention.

The visitor should understand three things before they walk through THE CONTAINED:

1. A charge is an accusation, not a finding of guilt.
2. Remand is custody before sentence.
3. In Australia, that exception has become a normal door into detention.

Then the rooms do the deeper work.

The public line is:

> CONTAINED shows what youth detention feels like. JusticeHub shows what works instead.

The sharper launch frame is:

> Remand is how a child can be locked up before sentence. THE CONTAINED shows the cost of that choice. JusticeHub shows the choices we could make instead.

## The Visitor Journey

The launch needs one clear sequence.

### 1. Before The Container: What Is Remand?

Purpose: give visitors the legal and human frame before they enter.

Visitor thought:

> I thought detention came after guilt. Now I understand a child can be locked up while the case is still unfinished.

Format:

- A short outdoor or foyer panel.
- A 90-second explainer by a facilitator.
- A pocket card with three terms: charged, bail, remand.
- QR to `/adelaide` and `/remand`.

Wall copy:

> Remand means custody before sentence.
>
> A child on remand has not been sentenced. Many have not been convicted. They are held while the court process continues.
>
> In youth justice, remand is no longer rare. Four in five young people in detention on an average day in 2024-25 were unsentenced, according to Justice Reform Initiative reporting on AIHW data.
>
> A charge is an accusation. Remand can turn accusation into a cell.

Source anchor:

- Justice Reform Initiative, 12 May 2026, citing AIHW Youth Justice in Australia 2024-25.
- ABS Prisoners in Australia 2025 for adult unsentenced context.

### 2. Room 1: The System That Detains

Purpose: show the trap, not just the cell.

Visitor thought:

> This is not one bad decision. It is a chain.

Do not make the room only about bars. Make the room about the machinery that produces the locked door:

- Police charge.
- Bail decision.
- Address required.
- Transport required.
- Adult support required.
- Curfew required.
- No-contact condition.
- Court delay.
- Breach.
- Watch house.
- Detention.

The antagonist is the paperwork. The child is not the exhibit.

Installation ideas:

- **The Conditions Wall**: a vertical stack of bail conditions, each one connected by red string to a practical need: housing, transport, phone, family support, legal help, school.
- **The Address Gap**: empty key hooks labelled "suitable address", "safe bed", "transport to court", "adult to answer the phone".
- **The Calendar**: court dates pulled forward across a long wall, showing how waiting becomes punishment.
- **The Receipt Roll**: a long thermal receipt printing the daily and yearly cost of detention.
- **The Watch House Door**: a small exterior panel naming the fact that children have been held in adult watch houses when youth detention centres are full.

Wall copy:

> Bail once asked a narrow question: will this person come back to court?
>
> Now it often asks a wider question: might this person be a risk?
>
> Risk follows poverty. Risk follows homelessness. Risk follows disability, family violence, racism, service gaps, remote geography, and the absence of someone available to stand beside a child at the right hour.
>
> When support is missing, detention starts to look like the only option. That is the design failure.

Short copy:

> If the condition is impossible, the breach is predictable.

Data tiles:

- Adult context: 19,850 of Australia's adult prisoners were unsentenced at 30 June 2025, 42 percent of the adult prisoner population. Source: ABS Prisoners in Australia 2025.
- Quarterly adult context: 19,612 of 47,295 adult prisoners were unsentenced in the December quarter 2025. Source: ABS Corrective Services, December quarter 2025.
- Youth context: four in five young people in detention on an average day in 2024-25 were unsentenced. Source: JRI citing AIHW 2024-25.
- First Nations youth context: JRI reports that 98 percent of First Nations children in detention had spent time unsentenced during 2024-25. Treat as source-checked to JRI until the AIHW data table is manually confirmed.

### 3. Between Room 1 And Room 2: The Hinge

Purpose: explain why this happened without turning the launch into a history lecture.

Visitor thought:

> The system did not accidentally drift here. Bail changed purpose.

The history brief is strongest when it names the hinge:

> Bail was redefined from a promise to return into a prediction of risk.

Use the Roman law and penal colony history as a side wall, not the main story. It adds depth, but it is not the doorway.

Installation ideas:

- **Promise / Prediction Split Panel**:
  - Left side: "Will you come back to court?"
  - Right side: "Might you be a risk?"
  - The visitor sees how the second question widens the net.
- **The Presumption Hollowed Out**:
  - A translucent copy of "presumption of innocence" overlaid by bail thresholds: unacceptable risk, show cause, compelling reason, exceptional circumstances.

Wall copy:

> The presumption of innocence has not disappeared.
>
> It has been made harder to reach.
>
> Across Australia, bail laws have become more risk-averse and more punitive. Tests such as unacceptable risk, show cause, compelling reason, and exceptional circumstances place more weight on predicted future risk before any sentence has been imposed.
>
> The result is a quiet reversal: liberty becomes something a person has to prove they deserve.

Source anchor:

- Australian Institute of Criminology, Bail and remand across Australia, 2026.

### 4. Room 2: The Supports That Would Have Changed The Path

Purpose: show that accountability requires support, not abandonment.

Visitor thought:

> The alternative is not doing nothing. The alternative is doing the right things early enough.

Room 2 should not be only "Spain works". It should translate the alternative into practical supports the visitor can understand.

Six supports to put physically in the room:

1. A lawyer before the bail decision.
2. A safe bed and address.
3. A trusted adult or cultural worker.
4. Transport to court and appointments.
5. School, training, work, or daily structure.
6. Family, healing, health, and disability support.

Installation ideas:

- **The Bail Support Table**: six objects on a table, each one linked to one support. Key, bus pass, phone, school book, court notice, family photo placeholder.
- **Move The Money**: visitors move tokens from "cell" to "support", then see what the same daily spend could hold.
- **The Open Door Diagram**: detention door on one side, support network on the other.
- **Diagrama Practice Lens**: one clean panel on education, therapy, family contact, and staffing.

Wall copy:

> The opposite of detention is not absence.
>
> It is a room full of adults who know what their job is.
>
> Bail support. Safe housing. Legal help. Cultural authority. Family work. Education. Transport. Daily structure. These are not soft options. They are the conditions that make accountability possible.

Short copy:

> A child cannot comply with a condition the system has made impossible.

Data tiles:

- NSW in 2018-19: youth detention cost about $1,413.66 per day, compared with $135.51 per day for community-based supervision. Source: NSW BOCSAR.
- NSW Bail Assistance Line: placed young people were 10.5 percentage points less likely to be in custody within six months than comparable young people who could not be placed. Source: NSW BOCSAR.
- National youth justice expenditure: detention-based supervision accounted for 66.2 percent, or $1.1 billion, of recurrent youth justice expenditure in 2024-25. Source: Productivity Commission RoGS 2026.

### 5. Room 3: The Community-Led Future

Purpose: move people from shock to obligation.

Visitor thought:

> People are already doing the work. The question is whether we back them.

Room 3 should be local at every stop. For Adelaide, the room should name the South Australian organisations, gaps, programs, and funding asks. It should also show the national JusticeHub layer that connects every stop.

Room 3 is not "hope" as mood. It is proof plus a next step.

Installation ideas:

- **Local Alternatives Wall**: South Australian organisations and services, each with source label and QR.
- **What Would Have Kept The Door Open?**: the same child pathway from Room 1, rebuilt with support at each decision point.
- **Community Ask Cards**: each partner organisation names one thing it needs funded or changed.
- **JusticeHub Search Station**: visitors search alternatives by place, issue, service type, or evidence level.
- **Story Sovereignty Note**: show how Empathy Ledger holds stories by consent, not extraction.

Wall copy:

> The cure is not hidden.

> It is underfunded, underconnected, and too often brought in after the damage is done.
>
> JusticeHub exists so the public can see the alternatives, funders can find the work, advocates can source-check their claims, and communities can hold their own stories at the centre.

## Human Story Spine

Use a composite pathway, not an invented named case.

Label it clearly:

> This pathway is a composite. It is built from documented remand drivers: bail refusal, housing, court delay, service gaps, bail conditions, breach, watch house custody, and detention. It is not one child's private story.

The narrative:

> A child is charged.
>
> The case is not finished. No sentence has been given.
>
> The court needs to know where they will sleep, who will answer the phone, how they will get to court, how they will stay away from people or places named in the bail conditions, and what support will hold the week together.
>
> But the address is unstable. The adult is working, overwhelmed, or not safe. The phone is broken. The service has a waitlist. The court date moves. The condition is breached.
>
> The system calls this risk.
>
> The child calls it no one there.

This is the line that can hold the whole experience:

> Remand is what happens when the system meets a child at the point of crisis and offers custody before care.

Public shorter version:

> Remand is custody before care has arrived.

## Wall Text Set

Use these as modular panels. Keep each one paired with a source label or QR.

### Panel A: Definition

> Remand means custody before sentence.
>
> A child on remand has not been sentenced. Many have not been convicted. They are held while the court process continues.
>
> The law still says a person is presumed innocent. The cell tells a different story.

### Panel B: The Trap

> Bail conditions can sound simple on paper.
>
> Live at this address. Be home by this time. Report to police. Stay away from this place. Answer this phone. Come back to court.
>
> For a child without stable housing, transport, a phone, family safety, health support, or a trusted adult, the condition can become the path back to custody.

### Panel C: The Hinge

> Bail was once a promise to return to court.
>
> It has become a prediction of risk.
>
> That shift matters because risk is not evenly distributed. It follows poverty, racism, homelessness, disability, family violence, remote geography, and service gaps.

### Panel D: The Cost

> Australia spends more than $1.1 billion a year on youth detention.
>
> The most expensive response is also the response that keeps failing children.
>
> The question is not whether we can afford alternatives. The question is why detention keeps getting paid first.

### Panel E: The Alternative

> The alternative is not doing nothing.
>
> It is bail support, safe housing, legal help, cultural authority, family work, education, health care, disability support, and adults who know how to stay.
>
> Accountability needs structure. A cell is not structure. It is absence with a lock on it.

### Panel F: JusticeHub

> JusticeHub is the evidence layer after the experience.
>
> Search the law. Find the programs. Follow the money. Check the sources. Share the brief. Add the missing model.
>
> Story stays sovereign. Evidence travels.

## Copy For Different Uses

### 20-Second Door Script

You are about to walk through THE CONTAINED.

The first thing to understand is remand. Remand means custody before sentence. A child can be locked up while their case is still unfinished.

This experience asks why that happens, what it costs, and what would keep children safely supported instead.

### 90-Second Facilitator Script

Before you go in, hold onto one distinction.

Charged does not mean guilty. Remand does not mean sentenced. It means a person is held in custody while the court process continues.

For children, that matters. A bail decision can depend on things a child does not control: whether there is a safe address, whether an adult can supervise, whether transport exists, whether services can respond, whether a condition is realistic, whether the court moves quickly.

When those supports are missing, the system can call the child a risk and use detention as the answer.

THE CONTAINED lets you feel the cost of that answer. JusticeHub shows the evidence, the alternatives, and the practical next step after you leave.

### Social Caption

Most people think detention comes after guilt.

Remand breaks that story.

A child can be held before sentence because bail support, housing, legal help, transport, or safe adult support is missing.

THE CONTAINED shows the cost of that choice. JusticeHub shows what works instead.

Start here: justicehub.com.au/remand

### Decision-Maker Invite

You are invited to walk through THE CONTAINED at Tandanya.

This is not a standard exhibition. It is a 30-minute public evidence experience about youth remand: how children are held before sentence, why that pathway is growing, what it costs, and what community-led alternatives already exist.

Come prepared to leave with one question: what would it take to fund the supports that stop a child reaching the cell in the first place?

## What To Change In The Current Drafts

### Keep

- The plain-English definition of remand.
- The strict separation between adult and youth data.
- The state and territory table.
- The "bail shifted from attendance to risk" argument.
- The "claims needing verification" section.
- The exhibition-safe Room 1, Room 2, Room 3 copy.

### Reframe

- Do not lead the public experience with Roman law. Use history as a depth layer.
- Do not lead with 10 statistics. Lead with one pathway, then use statistics to prove it is structural.
- Do not make "why the system is broken" a general moral claim. Make it specific: the system uses custody where support is missing.
- Do not make Room 2 only international. Translate the alternative into bail support, accommodation, legal help, cultural workers, family work, transport, education, and health care.
- Do not let the visitor leave with only shock. Give each visitor one role-based action.

### Cut Or Soften

- Avoid deficit labels for young people except when quoting historical source language.
- Avoid presenting JRI-read AIHW figures as direct AIHW figures until the underlying AIHW table is manually checked.
- Avoid using community program success percentages unless the source is named and current.
- Avoid any fictional named young person unless clearly labelled as composite and not based on a private story.
- Avoid profanity in public copy. The public phrase is "designed to detain", "failing by design", or "custody before care".
