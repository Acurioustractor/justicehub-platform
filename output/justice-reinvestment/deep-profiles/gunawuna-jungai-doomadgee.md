## 1. History

### Community and justice context

Doomadgee is a Gangalidda and Waanyi community of roughly 1,400 people in the Lower Gulf of Carpentaria. The Tim Fairfax Family Foundation profile notes that more than 70 funded organisations deliver largely fly-in fly-out services to the town, yet outcomes in health, housing, education and justice remain below community expectations. Community consultation run by Gunawuna Jungai identified lack of gainful employment as the single biggest underlying driver of contact with the justice system, alongside tension between cultural values and the Western justice system and frustration with policing. Founding chairman Barry Walden put the policing critique plainly: "We need community policing, not policing community. How can we have community safety if the police don't know the community?" (NIT, May 2024).

### From elders' vision to company (2009 to 2022)

The organisation's own history page traces its origins to 2009, when Kieran Smith (a non-Indigenous child protection practitioner, later 2012 Queensland Child Protection Award recipient) and Barry Walden (Doomadgee Traditional Owner) met and began working toward "a community-controlled company with legitimacy within both cultural and western systems." Key milestones from that account:

- **June 2010:** "Voice of Gangalidda" document produced, treated by the organisation as its seminal founding statement.
- **2010 to 2013:** Smith and Walden pioneered a culturally informed Safe House model in Doomadgee, later replicated in Aurukun, Napranum, Kowanyama and Pormpuraaw.
- **2016 to 2017:** Act for Kids became an institutional partner; Massimo Zaini joined as an apprentice; formal discussions began on transitioning Safe Houses to community control.
- **2022:** Endorsed by Doomadgee Aboriginal Shire Council and Mayor Jason Ned. Constitution adopted May 2022; registered as a company and charity August 2022. Australian Rural and Regional News described the company at launch as "15 years in the making."
- **July 2023:** Kieran Smith appointed inaugural CEO; Barry Walden elected Chairman; Massimo Zaini became first employee (COO).

The name combines Gunawuna ("child, every child, everyone" in Gangalidda) and Jungai ("authority" or "boss" in the local dialect). The task brief states that an elders' justice group carrying the Gunawuna Jungai name predates the company; the company's published history supports a long pre-incorporation gestation (2009 to 2022) anchored in elders' and Traditional Owners' authority, but no public source found in this research names a formally constituted "Gunawuna Jungai elders group" before 2022. UNVERIFIED beyond the company's own 2009 to 2022 origin narrative.

### Commonwealth and government funding milestones

- **2022:** Doomadgee selected as the first Queensland site (one of six nationally) for a Place-Based Partnership under the Joint Council on Closing the Gap, with Gunawuna Jungai as the community vehicle (TFFF).
- **October 2022 federal budget:** The Albanese Government committed $109 million to a First Nations justice package, including $79 million for up to 30 community-led justice reinvestment initiatives (the National Justice Reinvestment Program).
- **1 February 2024:** First nine National Justice Reinvestment Program grantees announced (Gunawuna Jungai not among them; Nja-marleya Cultural Leaders and Justice Group Ltd of Maningrida NT was).
- **April 2024 (reported 26 April by the North West Star; ALS and NIT coverage mid to late April):** Gunawuna Jungai announced as one of the next ten National Justice Reinvestment Program grantees, the Doomadgee site, alongside Hope Vale (Cape York Institute), Mornington Island (Jika Kangka Gununamanda Limited) and others. Attorney-General Mark Dreyfus: "Justice reinvestment empowers First Nations communities and leaders to develop local solutions to divert at risk adults and young people away from the criminal justice system." The individual grant amount for Gunawuna Jungai was not published in any source found. GAP: needs manual research (GrantConnect / Community Grants Hub grant award record for grant round 2023-2187).
- **2024:** Funded by Queensland Health to address five coronial inquest recommendations on rheumatic heart disease (TFFF).
- Philanthropic support through the Resilience stream of the Tim Fairfax Family Foundation (amount not disclosed).

## 2. Evidence and reviews

The organisation is young (incorporated August 2022, justice reinvestment funding from mid 2024) and the published evidence base is thin. That should be said plainly: there is no published independent evaluation of Gunawuna Jungai's justice reinvestment work as of June 2026.

What exists:

- **Smith, K., Onnis, L. and Walden, B. (2025), "Engaging cultural authority towards achieving sustainable operational outcomes: The Gunawuna Jungai story."** Conference poster, Developing Northern Australia Conference, Cairns, 22 to 24 July 2025; archived at James Cook University ResearchOnline; funded under the Medical Research Future Fund. Findings: the "dartboard model" of family-group governance, structured by cultural systems rather than Western organisational frameworks, enables recognition of cultural authority and produces different and more sustainable operational outcomes, with transferable lessons for other northern Australian communities. Note the authorship: two of the three authors are the organisation's founders, so this is practitioner-led documentation, not independent evaluation.
- **SPARC research project (announced April 2026):** "Shared Power for Advocating for Reform Collectively," a research partnership including Gunawuna Jungai, Yarrabah Leaders Forum and Jabu Mugay Ltd, Woorabinda Leaders Group, DIYDG (Cairns), the Queensland Health Reform Office and the Queensland Department of Women, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Partnerships and Multiculturalism, examining the four Closing the Gap Priority Reform areas (NIT, 3 April 2026). This is a forward-looking research vehicle, not a retrospective evaluation.
- **Media assessment:** National Indigenous Times (May 2024) and the North West Star (April 2024) coverage is descriptive of the funding announcement and community priorities rather than evaluative.
- The North West Star noted the funding announcement followed the Productivity Commission's February 2024 review showing shortfalls against Closing the Gap targets, which had prompted the organisation to request urgent state and federal funding.

GAP: needs manual research. The National Justice Reinvestment Program has a Commonwealth-commissioned national evaluation framework; whether Doomadgee site-level evaluation material has been produced or scheduled was not findable in public sources.

## 3. Impact model

Gunawuna Jungai describes itself as "cultural democracy in action," a backbone organisation for collective impact in Doomadgee rather than a single-service provider. Core elements:

- **Family-group governance (the "dartboard model").** Traditional Owners and elders identify Doomadgee's 18 family groups; each nominates a representative member, and each group's voice is carried by their elders and representative members. Decisions affecting the community are made "by us, for us." Elders' cultural authority is the legitimating structure, not an advisory add-on.
- **Justice reinvestment approach.** The organisation frames justice reinvestment as shifting resources from incarceration toward community-based solutions: education, housing, mental health support and employment, targeting root causes rather than punishment. Its stated aims for the Commonwealth grant are reducing justice system contact through community empowerment, system reform and economic development, including a comprehensive business plan with key stakeholders for industry development and local job creation (Kieran Smith, NIT 2024). Community policing reform is an explicit advocacy theme (Barry Walden's "community policing, not policing community").
- **Whole-of-community scope.** Beyond justice, project areas include health (including the rheumatic heart disease coronial response work), housing, early childhood development, child protection, education, data analytics and Closing the Gap implementation.
- **Reported early activity (from the organisation's justice reinvestment project page):** Justice Reinvestment Steering Committee gathered December 2024, Terms of Reference approved January 2025, Activity Workplan finalised February 2025; community engagement and data mapping; formation of the Doomadgee United rugby league football team; sports trainer courses and mediation training; discussion of a Youth Council; attendance at the "Justice Our Way: Bigger than Just Us" summit in Townsville, March 2025. Partners listed include the Justice Reinvestment Network Australia (the site names "Justice Reinvestment National Association (JRNA)"), the Queensland Department of Justice and Attorney-General, the Commonwealth Attorney-General's Department and QATSICPP.
- **Reported non-justice outcomes (first full year, per TFFF):** supported 29 residents to register ABNs for local entrepreneurship; purchased community transport and a barbecue trailer for engagement activities.

No quantified justice outcomes (diversion numbers, offending or detention trends) have been published. That is consistent with a program that finalised its workplan in February 2025.

## 4. Current status and what's next (2025 to 2026)

- **Leadership transition.** The founding pairing has handed over: Massimo Zaini is CEO (succeeding inaugural CEO Kieran Smith, who is now a board advisor and independent consultant); Athol Walden, a Gangalidda-Garawa board member and Doomadgee Aboriginal Shire Councillor, is Board Chairperson after Barry Walden transitioned from the director role in the 2025 board refresh. Berkeley Cox, former Chief Executive Partner of King & Wood Mallesons, is consulting on justice reinvestment strategic planning. Justice reinvestment cultural co-leads are Allan George and Preston Johnny; Tangala (Priscilla) Coleman coordinates community activation with a team of four community activation officers.
- **Board refresh.** Board composition reviewed and membership expanded in April to May 2025.
- **Funding.** Commonwealth National Justice Reinvestment Program funding runs on the round 2 footing (funding for that assessment cycle spans roughly 2024-25 to 2026-27 per Community Grants Hub; the Doomadgee grant's exact term and value are unpublished, GAP: needs manual research). The national grant opportunity closed to new applicants in November 2024 as the program approached full subscription. Other revenue: Queensland Health (rheumatic heart disease response), Tim Fairfax Family Foundation Resilience stream, MRFF-linked research activity and business donations.
- **What's next.** The SPARC research partnership (announced April 2026) positions Gunawuna Jungai inside a Queensland community-controlled reform coalition focused on shared decision-making, sector building, government transformation and data access. Athol Walden: "SPARC respects our cultural authority and our right to self-determination. When communities lead, the outcomes are stronger, more sustainable, and grounded in culture." Stated forward priorities include alternative housing models, sustainable business ventures for employment, reviving the local rodeo and youth-led cultural initiatives, plus maturing the justice reinvestment steering committee's workplan into delivery.
