## 1. History

### The name and the place

Tiraapendi Wodli means "protecting home" in the Kaurna language, the language of the Kaurna people of the Adelaide Plains, on whose Country Port Adelaide sits [S2, S3, S6]. Aboriginal families have lived in the Port Adelaide area across multiple generations; the Paul Ramsay Foundation describes it as a place that generations of Aboriginal people have made home [S5]. The name is sometimes written simply as "Tiraapendi" in earlier Red Cross material; "Tiraapendi Wodli" is the full and current form.

### Origins under Australian Red Cross (2017-2019)

The Port Adelaide Justice Reinvestment Project began as a partnership between Justice Reinvestment SA (JRSA) and Australian Red Cross, with early funding from the Law Foundation of SA and the SA Department for Communities and Social Inclusion [S7]. Red Cross acted as the host and backbone organisation: project management, contracts, financial management and human resources [S2, S6].

Key early milestones:

- **March 2018:** A representative, independent Port Adelaide Aboriginal Leadership Group of 9 independently elected members met for the first time, beginning a visioning and planning process to identify priorities for improving the safety, health and lives of Aboriginal families, children and young people using a justice reinvestment approach [S2, S4].
- **December 2018:** The leadership group released its inaugural Priority Action Plan 2019-21, setting out priorities, projects and key indicators across four populations: families with primary school aged children, young people, men, and adults and young people in contact with police and justice systems [S2, S4, S7].
- A community hub was established at 36 Dale Street, Port Adelaide, as the physical base for community members to design and run initiatives addressing the underlying causes of crime [S2, S4].

### Consolidation (2021-2023)

- **2021:** The leadership group established its Ways of Working documentation [S2]. The same year, Tiraapendi Wodli presented its Aboriginal Families Thrive Program (lead: Deb Moyle) to a national Indigenous justice audience, published via the Indigenous Justice Clearinghouse [S8].
- **2023:** Tiraapendi Wodli released its Theory of Change [S2].
- **October 2023:** Red Cross, Tiraapendi Wodli and JRSA formed a partnership with the Paul Ramsay Foundation to implement the Tiraapendi Wodli Understanding, Monitoring, Evaluation and Learning (UMEL) framework covering 2023-26 [S6, S7].

### Independence (2025-2026)

- **2024:** The leadership group began expanding its priorities and formally pursuing independent governance as an Aboriginal Community Controlled Organisation (ACCO) [S2].
- **4 April 2025:** Tiraapendi Wodli was formally registered as Tiraapendi Wodli Ltd (company registration). A launch event was held with the Hon Susan Close MP, outgoing Member for Port Adelaide, alongside CEO Deb Moyle and incoming Member for Port Adelaide Cheyne Rich [S2, S9].
- **November 2025:** Tiraapendi Wodli celebrated becoming an independent ACCO for justice reinvestment [S6].
- **3 December 2025:** ABN active; registered with the ACNC as a charity; endorsed as a Public Benevolent Institution with DGR Item 1 status, GST concession, income tax exemption and FBT exemption, all effective from this date [S1].
- **12 February 2026:** GST registration effective [S1].
- **1 July 2026:** Target date for Tiraapendi Wodli Ltd to commence fully independent business and operational systems (finance, HR, contracts), completing the handover from Red Cross [S2, S6].

### The Red Cross handover: when, why, what changed

**When.** The transition was staged rather than abrupt: company registration April 2025, ACCO independence celebrated November 2025, charity and DGR status December 2025, and full operational independence targeted for 1 July 2026. As of mid 2026 some program staff contact details still sit on redcross.org.au email addresses, consistent with a transition still completing [S10].

**Why.** Independence was the design intent of the justice reinvestment model from the start: community control, with the backbone organisation working itself out of the host role. The UMEL evaluation framework was explicitly designed to "build the capacity of the Aboriginal leadership group towards Tiraapendi Wodli independence" [S6, S7]. CEO Deb Moyle gave the working rationale: "As an ACCO, we can deliver stronger arguments, accountability of change [and access] increased funding opportunities, with a strong economic foundation for reinvestment into Aboriginal communities" [S6]. Moyle also described the Red Cross posture during the hosted years: Red Cross "sat respectively on the edge", walking Aboriginal leaders through challenges with cultural nuance [S6].

**What changed.** Tiraapendi Wodli moved from receiving backbone support (administration, financial management, HR) from Red Cross to operating as an independent legal entity that can hold its own contracts, employ its own staff, receive tax-deductible donations directly (DGR from December 2025), and apply for funding in its own name. Red Cross has stated it "continues to proudly walk alongside Tiraapendi Wodli", shifting from host to ally with a continuing role in supporting systemic change [S6].

A program-design document mapping the systems Aboriginal families navigate (prisons, courts, police, housing, health, education, employment, NGOs, ACCOs); largely visual, no outcome figures in the text layer [S8].

It is designed to establish an Aboriginal data ecosystem and data sovereignty approach for evaluative practice and local decision making, and to build leadership group capacity towards independence [S6, S7].

3. **Tiraapendi Wodli: Keeping strong as we grow (first stage evaluation report).** First report of results from stage one of the Tiraapendi Wodli evaluation. 4. **Tiraapendi Wodli: Our Past, Our Future (monograph, May 2025).** Listed on the Tiraapendi Wodli website with a PDF link [S4]. Contents not reviewed for this profile. GAP: needs manual research.

PRF estimated its JR portfolio partners prevented 73 children and young people from returning to detention, and noted that justice reinvestment is cheaper and more effective at reducing reoffending than youth detention, which costs more than $1 million per year per child, with positive impact typically emerging after the third year of a site's operation [S4]. Context statistic used by the organisation: Aboriginal young people in South Australia are 23 times more likely to be placed in detention than non-Aboriginal young people [S4].

## 3. Impact model

### Justice reinvestment in a metropolitan setting

Most Australian justice reinvestment sites are remote or regional (Bourke NSW, Katherine NT, Halls Creek WA, Central Australia). Tiraapendi Wodli is one of the few metropolitan justice reinvestment sites in the country, operating in Adelaide's inner north-west. The urban setting changes the model in several ways:

- **Service density, not service absence.** The Port Adelaide problem is not a lack of services but Aboriginal families having to navigate a dense, fragmented system (prisons, courts, police, housing, health, education, employment, NGOs) that was not built for them. The 2021 Aboriginal Families Thrive material maps exactly this systems web [S8]. The hub's core function is navigation and brokerage: one trusted, culturally safe front door at 36 Dale Street.
- **Community definition by relationship, not geography alone.** Port Adelaide's Aboriginal community spans the western metropolitan area, so the leadership group was independently elected to be representative rather than drawn from a single discrete township [S2, S4].
- **Hub-based delivery.** A physical community hub anchors the work, with drop-in individual support alongside scheduled group programs [S2, S10].

### The model's components

- **Community leadership first.** The elected Aboriginal Leadership Group sets priorities (the 2018 Action Plan, 2021 Ways of Working, 2023 Theory of Change), and the backbone organisation executes [S2].
- **Backbone support.** Red Cross provided project management, contracts, finance and HR during the hosted years; that function transfers to Tiraapendi Wodli Ltd itself by 1 July 2026 [S2, S6].
- **Four priority populations:** families with school aged children; young people; men and mothers; and adults and young people in contact with police and justice systems [S2, S4].
- **Case work and individual support:** the Aboriginal Families Thrive Program offers tailored, flexible support through drop-in or scheduled appointments at the hub; more than 400 support instances per month, 650+ people and families per year [S6, S10].
- **Safety as the precondition.** Community Elder Uncle Alex: "People feel safe here, for a start. And when they feel safe, they'll open up and talk about their problems" [S6].
- **Data sovereignty.** The UMEL framework builds an Aboriginal data ecosystem so the community owns the evidence about itself and uses it for local decision making [S6, S7].

### Current programs (as listed on the organisation's website, 2026)

- **Women's Gatherings** (Tuesdays 10am-1pm): visiting speakers, workshops, yarning circles, craft; genealogy research, financial literacy, budget cooking, healing circles [S10].
- **Men's Gatherings** (Fridays 10am-12pm): IT literacy, budgeting, personal development, NDIS access, community outings [S10].
- **20 Steps Post Release Support:** a 90-day intensive program for people recently released from custody covering ID, housing, Centrelink access, health checks, budgeting and cultural connection; partners are the SA Department for Correctional Services and the Paul Ramsay Foundation [S10].
- **Aboriginal Families Thrive Program, individual support:** drop-in and appointment-based case work at the hub [S10].
- **Community events:** NAIDOC weeks, school holiday programs and significant occasions [S10].
- Red Cross's 2026 story also notes mental health counselling, NDIS access support and culturally safe community space as ongoing offerings [S6].

Directors at registration (April 2025): Edward Martin, Neva Wilson, Alicia Papertalk, with Samantha Jackson as Public Officer [S2]. Program staff include Joelene Walker (Aboriginal Families Thrive Support Officer) and Simon Talanskas (Justice Reinvestment Program Coordinator), both still on Red Cross email addresses during the transition [S10].

**Legal and tax position.** Independent public company limited by guarantee; ACNC charity, PBI, DGR Item 1, income tax exempt, FBT exempt, all from 3 December 2025; GST registered from 12 February 2026 [S1].
