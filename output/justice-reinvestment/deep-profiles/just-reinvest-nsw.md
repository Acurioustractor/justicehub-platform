## 1. History

**Founding.** Just Reinvest NSW began in 2011 as a strategic initiative of the Aboriginal Legal Service (NSW/ACT) Limited (ALS), formed as a coalition of more than 20 organisations seeking to keep Aboriginal young people out of custody by investing in community-led solutions. Sarah Hopkins, then Managing Solicitor Justice Projects at the ALS, was the long-serving Chair and led the justice projects work, including in Bourke from 2012. (Sources: ALS justice reinvestment page; Sydney Criminal Lawyers interview with Sarah Hopkins.)

**Relationship to Maranguka and Bourke.** Just Reinvest NSW partnered with the community of Bourke and the local organisation Maranguka from 2012. In 2013 Bourke became the first major site in Australia to implement an Aboriginal-led, place-based model of justice reinvestment, through a collaboration between Maranguka, the Bourke Tribal Council and Just Reinvest NSW, with Alistair Ferguson the founding community leader. Maranguka is community-led and Bourke-owned; Just Reinvest NSW provided the backbone support, legal and policy capacity, data brokerage and government-relations work that the community drew on.

**Key milestones.**
- 2011: launched as ALS strategic initiative (ALS media release).
- 2012: partnership with Bourke community and Maranguka begins (ALS; justreinvest.org.au).
- 2013: Bourke becomes Australia's first major Aboriginal-led place-based justice reinvestment site (justreinvest.org.au Bourke page).
- 9 February 2015: registered with the ACNC; PBI and DGR Item 1 endorsements take effect from the same date (ABN Lookup).
- 2015: National Rural Law and Justice Award (justreinvest.org.au About page).
- September 2016: KPMG preliminary assessment "Unlocking the Future" delivered.
- 27 November 2018: KPMG Impact Assessment of the Maranguka Justice Reinvestment Project published, the first report of its kind in Australia.
- 2019: Australian Human Rights Commission Community Organisation Award and HESTA community award (justreinvest.org.au; National Indigenous Times).
- Expansion to Mount Druitt and Moree following the Bourke results (Just Futures case study, Australian Community Philanthropy).
- 5 April 2022: Just Reinvest NSW and Maranguka both become independent organisations, separating from the ALS. At independence the Just Reinvest NSW CEO was Geoffrey Winters and the Maranguka CEO was Matt Davidson (ALS media release, 5 April 2022).
- June 2023: NSW Department of Communities and Justice funds Just Reinvest NSW $2.5 million to expand justice reinvestment to Kempsey and Nowra, June 2023 to June 2027 (DCJ justice reinvestment page).
- November 2024: submission to the Senate inquiry into Australia's youth justice and incarceration system.
- August 2025: publishes the Discussion Paper on Designing a Justice Reinvestment Mechanism for New South Wales.
- 2025: launches a formal Research Agenda for "future reinvestment" (justreinvest.org.au).

## 2. Evidence and reviews

**KPMG, "Unlocking the Future: Maranguka Justice Reinvestment Project in Bourke", preliminary assessment, 2016.** A point-in-time progress assessment as of June 2016, before full implementation. Documented the baseline problem: in 2013, 121 Aboriginal young people under 26 from the Bourke LGA were released from sentenced custody; within 12 months of release, 90 per cent of those under 18 and 66 per cent of those aged 18 to 25 had a new proven court conviction. KPMG noted the project was unique in being community-led and using collective impact methodology, and intended the report to inform refinement and government engagement. (PDF hosted as a NSW Parliament inquiry submission attachment.)

**KPMG, "Maranguka Justice Reinvestment Project: Impact Assessment", 27 November 2018.** The headline evaluation. Findings for calendar year 2017 against the 2016 baseline:
- 23 per cent reduction in police-recorded incidence of domestic violence (family strength domain).
- 31 per cent increase in Year 12 student retention (youth development domain).
- 38 per cent reduction in charges across the top five juvenile offence categories.
- 42 per cent reduction in days spent in custody (adult empowerment domain).
- Gross economic impact of $3.1 million in 2017 against operational costs of $0.6 million, roughly five times operating cost; about two thirds of the impact accrued to the justice system and one third was broader regional economic impact.
- Forecast: if Bourke sustained just half of the 2017 results, an additional gross impact of $7 million could be delivered over the following five years.
- Important caveats stated by KPMG: this is an impact assessment, not an outcomes evaluation; it covers a single year of change and includes no statistical attribution or contribution analysis isolating the Maranguka effect. (KPMG report PDF via Indigenous Justice Clearinghouse; KPMG Newsroom; APO record.)

**UNSW Social Policy Research Centre study (chief investigators Bingqin Li and Miri Raven).** Examined Maranguka as a community-led initiative improving social outcomes for Aboriginal families through justice reinvestment. Surfaced in search results via the UNSW Human Rights Defender publication; full findings UNVERIFIED in this pass. GAP: needs manual research for publication year and headline findings.

**ANZSOG, "Maranguka: A Study Based on Publicly Available Evidence", case study, 2025.** An Australia and New Zealand School of Government teaching case reviewing the publicly available evidence base for Maranguka. (PDF on anzsog.edu.au.) Detailed conclusions not extracted in this pass; GAP: needs manual research.

**"Building a Pathway to Justice: Designing a Reinvestment Mechanism with Aboriginal Communities" (research with Aboriginal communities including Moree, published via Just Reinvest NSW with university partners, 2023).** Community research on how a reinvestment funding mechanism should work, summarised publicly in The Conversation piece "Too much money is spent on jails and policing: what Aboriginal communities told us about funding justice reinvestment to keep people out of prison" (2023, republished by UTS). Core finding: communities want resources shifted from prisons and policing into prevention strategies designed by communities themselves, with long-term, flexible, community-controlled funding. Exact author list UNVERIFIED in this pass.

**Discussion Paper on Designing a Justice Reinvestment Mechanism for New South Wales, Just Reinvest NSW, August 2025.** Sets out practical design considerations for a future policy mechanism that reinvests resources from the criminal justice system into community-led prevention. (justreinvest.org.au research pages.)

**Forthcoming: "The Cost of the NSW Criminal Justice System" (Just Reinvest NSW research agenda, 2025).** Will quantify the cost of Aboriginal people's movement through the NSW justice system (police contact, remand, court, incarceration, adults and young people) to calculate an "Aboriginal criminal justice cost gap" for NSW. Not yet published as of this profile.

## 3. Impact model

**Theory of change.** Just Reinvest NSW describes justice reinvestment as shifting money and resources away from prisons and policing and into prevention strategies designed by communities themselves. Its role is backbone support: it does not run programs over communities, it resources Aboriginal communities to develop their own solutions, with the stated goal of reducing Aboriginal people's interactions with the criminal justice system.

**Five-phase place-based approach** (from justreinvest.org.au/about):
1. Exploration.
2. Engagement, data collection and identification of justice "circuit breakers".
3. Development of a community strategy.
4. Implementation of the community-led strategy.
5. Incorporation of a local entity with ongoing support.

The model combines community-led decision making and governance support, capability building, and data-driven strategy. Dedicated data officers sit inside community backbone teams in Bourke, Mount Druitt, Moree, Kempsey and Nowra; in Kempsey a Community Research and Data Officer gathers local information so the community can understand what drives justice system contact. Mount Druitt's mature expression of the model is the standalone Aboriginal organisation MAYCS (Mounty Aboriginal Youth and Community Services), created out of the Mounty Yarns youth-led storytelling project. Just Reinvest NSW is a member of the Justice Reinvestment Network Australia.

**Concrete reported outcomes with figures.**
- Bourke 2017 (KPMG 2018): 23 per cent fall in police-recorded domestic violence; 31 per cent rise in Year 12 retention; 38 per cent fall in charges across the top five juvenile offence categories; 42 per cent fall in days spent in custody; $3.1 million gross economic impact against $0.6 million operating cost.
- Mount Druitt: MAYCS secured $3.3 million in government grants, comprising $2.3 million to continue justice reinvestment work over three years and almost $1 million for a Youth on Track early intervention program; the MAYCS team grew from 5 to 12 full-time local Aboriginal staff plus four youth ambassadors (Dusseldorp Forum, "7 Snapshots of Success" and "Rise of Young Leaders in Mt Druitt"). Mount Druitt program strands include OzTag, Mounty Yarns creative advocacy, a Learner Driver Mentor Program, youth support from police custody, a bail project, a community fines action plan and community access to data.
- Moree: BOCSAR data shows legal actions against young people in Moree falling over the two years to September 2025, down from a 2022-23 peak; in regional NSW overall, young people proceeded against to court declined 12.3 per cent in the two years to June 2025. Attribution to justice reinvestment specifically is not established; the NSW Government has run parallel interventions in Moree since April 2024 (Operation Soteria, bail law changes, bail accommodation service). Mark the Moree causal claim UNVERIFIED.

## 4. Current status and what's next (2025-2026)

**Leadership** (verified from justreinvest.org.au/about/our-people, fetched 2026-06-11):
- CEO: Geoff Scott. CFO: Paul O'Brien.
- Board Chair: Blake Alan Cansdale (Darkinjung). Directors: Jack Beetson, Gino Vumbaca (independent), Paul Wright (independent), Karlie Stewart (Yuin), Paul Morris (Dunghutti, Kempsey).
- Sarah Hopkins was the long-serving Chair during the ALS era; Geoffrey Winters was CEO at independence in April 2022. Dates of the transitions to the current leadership are UNVERIFIED.
- Staff of roughly 14 listed, with offices in Redfern (Sydney), Moree and Mount Druitt, including community managers in Moree (Mekayla Cochrane) and Mount Druitt (Daniel Daylight) and a Manager of Research and Data (Talia Hagerty).

**Communities.** Five active sites: Bourke (with independent partner Maranguka), Mount Druitt (MAYCS), Moree, Kempsey (embedded in "Learning the Macleay") and Nowra.

**Funding sources.** NSW Government, including the DCJ $2.5 million grant for Kempsey and Nowra expansion (June 2023 to June 2027); Paul Ramsay Foundation (partnership framed as scaling justice reinvestment through expansion of the model; a specific dollar amount was not found in this pass and is marked GAP: needs manual research); Dusseldorp Forum; the Aboriginal Legal Service NSW/ACT as a continuing partner; Vincent Fairfax Family Foundation funds Maranguka in Bourke; pro bono support from law firms including Herbert Smith Freehills, Ashurst and Gilbert + Tobin; university partners UNSW and the UTS Jumbunna Institute. Financial figures from the ACNC Annual Information Statement could not be retrieved (ACNC pages timed out twice). GAP: needs manual research for revenue, expenses and staff counts from the ACNC register.

**Policy environment.** The Commonwealth's October 2022 Budget committed $69 million over four years to a National Justice Reinvestment Program supporting up to 30 community-led initiatives, with ongoing funding of $20 million per year from 2026-27, administered by the Attorney-General's Department. This federal program drew directly on the NSW evidence base that Just Reinvest NSW and Maranguka built. Just Reinvest NSW continues to advocate federally, including its November 2024 submission to the Senate Legal and Constitutional Affairs Committee inquiry into Australia's youth justice and incarceration system, urging investment in justice reinvestment as prevention, delivery of basic human services, wraparound and diversionary services, and centring Aboriginal voices.

**Strategy.** The 2025 strategy looks to "the next iteration of place-based, community-led and data-driven justice reinvestment": proving that reinvestment works and that moving resources from the criminal justice system into communities reduces Aboriginal incarceration. The 2025 Research Agenda asks three remaining questions: what is required to reduce resources in the carceral system; what is the economic opportunity cost of imprisonment in labour terms; and what is the minimum legislative change needed to shift incarceration levels. The August 2025 discussion paper on a NSW reinvestment mechanism is the first concrete design artefact; the forthcoming cost-of-system paper will supply the fiscal case.
