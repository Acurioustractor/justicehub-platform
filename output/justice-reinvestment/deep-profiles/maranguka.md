# Maranguka Limited: Deep Organisational Profile

**ABN 82 646 525 135 | ACN 646 525 135 | 41B Mitchell Street, Bourke NSW 2840 | maranguka.org.au**

Maranguka (meaning "caring for others" in Ngemba language) is Australia's first major community-led justice reinvestment site, operating on Ngemba land in Bourke, north-west NSW. It is governed by the cultural authority of the Bourke Tribal Council and led by founder and Executive Director Alister Ferguson (commonly spelled Alistair in media; the organisation's own annual report and newsletter use "Alister"). Profile compiled 11 June 2026.

---

## 1. History

### Origins with the Bourke Tribal Council

Bourke is a remote town on the Darling River roughly 800 km north-west of Sydney, with a population of about 2,500, roughly 30 per cent of whom are First Nations people (ANZSOG/UNSW case study, 2025). Bourke has long been one of the most socio-economically disadvantaged areas in NSW, with Aboriginal young people heavily over-represented in detention.

The Bourke Tribal Council is the overarching community governance body representing the more than 20 clan and family groups of the Aboriginal community of Bourke. Maranguka describes the Tribal Council as "a treaty in itself, a coming together of distinct Indigenous Tribes and families in our area" (maranguka.org.au). Maranguka began as a grassroots vision of the Bourke Aboriginal community, championed by Alister Ferguson, for "improving outcomes and creating better coordinated support for vulnerable families and children through the true empowerment of the local Aboriginal community" (Maranguka Limited Annual Report 2024, Note 1).

### Alister Ferguson's role

Ferguson is the founder and has been Executive Director throughout. In his words: "We developed the Maranguka proposal with a clear focus on creating better coordinated support to vulnerable families and children in Bourke through community-led teams working in partnership with existing service providers" (quoted in the ANZSOG case study and on the Just Reinvest NSW site). He has framed Maranguka's success as the result of the Bourke Tribal Council "applying and asserting its cultural authority across the whole system". Ferguson has received a Medal of the Order of Australia (OAM) for service to the Aboriginal community (Philanthropy Australia profile; year of award UNVERIFIED against a second source).

## 2. Evidence and reviews

### KPMG preliminary assessment (2016)

"Unlocking the Future: Maranguka Justice Reinvestment Project in Bourke", KPMG, 2016. A preliminary feasibility and design assessment, later tabled as an attachment to NSW parliamentary inquiry submissions. It scoped the cost of the status quo in Bourke and the potential of a justice reinvestment approach.
Source: https://www.parliament.nsw.gov.au/ladocs/submissions/59833/Attachment%20-%20KPMG%20Preliminary%20Assessment%20Maranguka%20Justice%20Reinvestment%20Project.pdf

### KPMG Impact Assessment (27 November 2018) - the anchor evaluation

"Maranguka Justice Reinvestment Project: Impact Assessment", KPMG, 27 November 2018. Launched in NSW Parliament. The first report of its kind in Australia to quantify the economic impact of justice reinvestment. Headline findings:

- **Gross economic impact of $3.1 million in 2017**, against operational costs of $0.6 million, an impact five times the operating cost. About two thirds of the impact related to the justice system and one third was broader economic impact to the region.
- Changes observed in Bourke 2015 to 2017:
  - 23 per cent reduction in police-recorded rates of domestic violence (family strength measure)
  - 18 per cent reduction in major offences reported
  - 39 per cent reduction in the number of domestic violence related assaults
  - 34 per cent reduction in non-domestic violence related assaults
  - 43 per cent reduction in domestic-related assault by young people up to 25 years
  - 38 per cent reduction in charges across the top five juvenile offence categories
  - 31 per cent increase in Year 12 student retention
  - 14 per cent reduction in bail breaches (adults)
  - 42 per cent reduction in days spent in custody (adults)
- KPMG projected an **additional $7 million in benefit over five years** if just half the 2017 results were sustained (cited via Just Reinvest NSW).

Sources: KPMG newsroom https://newsroom.kpmg.com.au/maranguka-justice-reinvestment-bourke-landmark-report-demonstrates-economic-impact-3-1-million/ ; full report PDF https://www.indigenousjustice.gov.au/wp-content/uploads/mp/files/resources/files/maranguka-justice-reinvestment-project-kpmg-impact-assessment-final-report.pdf ; APO record https://apo.org.au/node/206461

Note on figure hygiene: the 23/38/42 per cent figures are restated in later secondary literature (e.g. Haysom 2024, cited in a 2024-25 Australian Parliament youth justice inquiry submission) and all trace back to the single KPMG 2018 assessment. The KPMG assessment itself notes attribution limits: it measured changes "corresponding to" the operation of the project, not causally isolated effects.

### ANZSOG / UNSW Social Policy Research Centre case study (September 2025)

Li, B., Katz, I., and Raven, M., "Maranguka: A Study Based on Publicly Available Evidence", ANZSOG Research Insights No. 50, 12 September 2025 (DOI: 10.54810/TQNJ7689). A co-governance case study co-sponsored by the NSW Government, synthesising public material 2014-2024, developed with AH&MRC ethics approval and signed off by Ferguson. Key findings: Maranguka rebalanced power toward community-led decision-making; the Cross Sector Leadership Group broke down agency silos and unlocked data sharing; cultural authority of the Bourke Tribal Council is the governance anchor; outcomes include reduced youth crime and strengthened local leadership. It also flags limits: reliance on external funding and political support creates sustainability vulnerabilities, and early government resistance to data sharing was a major barrier.
Source: https://anzsog.edu.au/app/uploads/2025/09/CASE-STUDY-Maranguka-ANZSOG-version.pdf

## 3. Impact model

### The architecture

Maranguka operates a **community hub model** in Bourke with a small **backbone team** of around five local staff (ANZSOG case study) coordinating, not duplicating, existing services. The structure:

- **Bourke Tribal Council** sits at the top as the cultural authority and decision-making body; the Maranguka Board governs the company. Government procurement contracts for services delivered in Bourke now embed Maranguka's community protocols, a formal recognition of Tribal Council authority (Annual Report 2024).
- **Cross Sector Leadership Group** (CSLG, now renamed the Maranguka Cross Sector Government Implementation Group) brings government and community partners together to unblock systemic barriers, align resources with community priorities and improve data sharing (ANZSOG; Annual Report 2024).
- **Working groups** organised around the strategy's life stages and roles: Early Childhood and Parenting; Young People aged 8 to 18; the Role of Women; the Role of Men. Each includes government, NGO, service provider and community members (October 2025 newsletter).
- **Daily Check-In** with Bourke Police, running since 2017: a daily operational meeting between Maranguka and local service providers/police that coordinates responses to overnight incidents and wraps support around families, credited with reducing youth re-offending and enabling a "more coordinated and compassionate response" (Annual Report 2024).
- **Palimaa Data Platform**: community-owned data platform built on First Nations Data Sovereignty principles, enabling community-led, data-driven decisions; interactive public version released August 2024 (Annual Report 2024; NIT Oct 2024).

### Growing our Kids up Safe, Smart and Strong

The Bourke Tribal Council's strategy, with goals developed in 2015 from community conversations around data on the life of an Aboriginal young person in Bourke. It is the organising framework for everything Maranguka does (the website strapline is "Growing our kids up SAFE, SMART and STRONG"). Maranguka implements it using a collective impact framework, changing how services are delivered to the community rather than delivering new parallel services.

### How change happens

The theory of change runs: cultural authority asserted by the Tribal Council, plus a backbone team coordinating cross-sector effort, plus shared data, produces earlier and better-coordinated support for families, which reduces contact with the justice system, which frees resources that can be reinvested in prevention. The KPMG 2018 assessment is the quantified expression of that loop.

### Concrete reported outcomes

- The KPMG 2018 figures above (the anchor outcomes set).
- More than 300 community members supported to obtain driver's licences, removing a major criminalisation pathway in remote NSW (Annual Report 2024).
- Dozens of school holiday, parenting and cultural programs; in the September-October 2025 holidays Maranguka partnered with 12 local services on a youth activities program (October 2025 newsletter).
- Early childhood programs (Welcome Baby to Country, Connected Beginnings, Healthy Kids Bus Stop) with increased participation and improved school readiness indicators in 2024; TRYP Bootcamps and Maranguka Youth Hub for 8 to 18s (Annual Report 2024).
- Policy influence: Maranguka was the first place-based community-led initiative in Australia and is one of 10 sites under the federal Stronger Places, Stronger People initiative; its model helped shape the Commonwealth's national adoption of justice reinvestment (Annual Report 2024).

## 4. Current status and what's next (2025-2026)

### Leadership and governance

Alister Ferguson OAM remains Executive Director and Founder. Governance sits with the Bourke Tribal Council and the Maranguka Board. 2024 was described as a year of reflection and governance refresh: renewing working group memberships, strengthening the authorising environment for government-community partnerships, and developing a refreshed Community Accountability Framework (Annual Report 2024).

### Finances (FY2024, audited)

From the Annual Report 2024 (financials by Accounting For Good, audited by Stewart Brown, approved by the Board 30 April 2025):

- Revenue $1,727,104 (government grants $1,176,276; other grants $488,936; donations and other revenue $61,892)
- Expenses $1,743,589 (employee costs $1,032,800; program costs $375,137)
- Small deficit of $16,485; net assets $392,777
- Cash at bank $3,366,983, offset by $2,842,900 income received in advance, indicating multi-year grant funding banked ahead of delivery

### Funding

- The NSW Government and the federal Department of Social Services co-fund the backbone team and have committed funding for another five years (Annual Report 2024; committed as of that report).
- Stronger Places, Stronger People: Bourke/Maranguka is one of 10 nationally funded place-based sites.
- Philanthropic partners include the Dusseldorp Forum (a long-term partner since the project's early years), the Paul Ramsay Foundation (including part-funding the Palimaa Data Platform) and the Vincent Fairfax Family Foundation (VFFF case study). In September 2025 staff from the Dusseldorp Forum, UTS Impact Studios and the Paul Ramsay Foundation visited Bourke to deepen the partnership (October 2025 newsletter).
- October 2024: a federal government-philanthropic partnership for place-based work was announced ($19.3 million over five years matched by philanthropic partners including PRF), with Maranguka cited as the exemplar of the approach (National Indigenous Times). Maranguka's exact share UNVERIFIED.
- New: funding awarded under the NSW Government's Expansion and Enhancement Program to develop an Aboriginal Child and Family Centre at the Yanmali site in Bourke, building on Bourke and District Children's Services (October 2025 newsletter).

### Strategy and new work

- Next strategic plan in development through 2025 community sessions (styled 10-Year Strategic Plan 2025-2035 in the annual report; Strategic Plan 2025-2030 in the October 2025 newsletter).
- **Nexus Centre**: a national hub for place-based collaboration born out of the Bourke learning, positioned to support other communities, funders and government walking the same path. This is Maranguka's main replication vehicle (Annual Report 2024).
- Community Safety Project (2025): home security upgrades (lights, cameras) for vulnerable Aboriginal and non-Aboriginal households targeted by youth crime over the prior 18 months, paired with youth support to reduce home invasions and the likelihood of youth incarceration. This signals candour that youth crime pressures re-emerged in Bourke in 2024-25 (October 2025 newsletter).
- Men's Hub upgrade on Meek Street (new shed for cultural and hands-on programs) in collaboration with CatholicCare Wilcannia-Forbes (October 2025 newsletter).
- Replication context: Just Reinvest NSW, Maranguka's original partner, now runs justice reinvestment projects in Moree and Mount Druitt with further NSW expansion planned, and justice reinvestment has been adopted as national policy with a Commonwealth program, both directly downstream of the Bourke proof of concept.

### Risks flagged in the literature

The ANZSOG case study names long-term sustainability as the open question: dependence on external funding cycles and political goodwill, with social bonds and deeper cross-sector partnerships explored as stabilisers.
