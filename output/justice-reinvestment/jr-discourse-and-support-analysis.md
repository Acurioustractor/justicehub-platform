# Justice Reinvestment: How Sites Describe Their Work, What Limits Them, and What Data and Story Support Should Look Like

Prepared for JusticeHub. Date: 2026-06-10. All quotes and claims carry a URL. Plain prose, no em dashes.

---

## 1. Their own words

How justice reinvestment (JR) sites describe their own work. Short direct quotes pulled from the org's own site or a primary report, with the source beside each.

**Maranguka / Bourke (NSW).** The community frames this as self-governance and data sovereignty, not a program delivered to them. From their own homepage:

> "We are demonstrating what can be achieved when community is in the drivers seat."
> "Operationalising self-determination by levelling the playing field with the use of data and ensuring attributable sovereignty."
> "Greater community agency and use of existing service data where the community has a say in managing better service outcomes informed by this data."

Source: https://maranguka.org.au/ . Their custom platform Palimaa is described as the vehicle for this ("Maranguka has championed the custom built Palimaa Interactive Platform to support analytics, data evaluation and research to drive local decision making", same URL). Just Reinvest NSW describes Maranguka as "a model of Indigenous self-governance which empowers the Aboriginal community to coordinate the right mix and timing of services": https://www.justreinvest.org.au/community/bourke-maranguka/

**Olabud Doogethu / Halls Creek (WA).** "Olabud Doogethu" is Kriol for "all of us together". The community's framing is blunt about who has failed and who must lead:

> "The traditional judicial system has failed us and continues to let us down. We must manage this problem ourselves. The Aboriginal way."
> "By making a commitment to diverting our mob away from the overcrowded prison system, we can invest in our future, our children."

Source: https://olabuddoogethu.org.au/about-us/ . They describe the model as having "Indigenous sovereignty and empowerment at the heart" and redesigning justice around "local Aboriginal law and culture, community-led solutions": https://olabuddoogethu.org.au/about-us/

**Tiraapendi Wodli / Port Adelaide (SA).** Name means "protecting home" in Kaurna. Governed by an independently elected Aboriginal leadership group:

> A "representative and independent Port Adelaide Aboriginal Leadership Group comprising 9 independently elected members came together ... to start a visioning and planning process to identify priority issues and opportunities."

Their hub is "a place for community members to design and implement initiatives to address the underlying causes of crime". Source (org-aligned project page): https://www.justicereinvestmentsa.org/projectssa and the site's own about page https://www.tiraapendiwodli.org.au/aboutjr (Squarespace, text confirmed via project descriptions; marked as secondary-confirmed because the about page renders client-side).

**Queensland sites.** Three community-controlled entities now hold JR funding: Gunawuna Jungai Limited (Doomadgee), Cape York Institute (Hope Vale), and Jika Kangka Gununamanda Limited (Mornington Island). The framing: "Justice reinvestment empowers First Nations communities and leaders to develop local solutions to divert at risk adults and young people away from the criminal justice system." Doomadgee's own initiative reconnects young people "to country, community, and culture". Source: https://www.als.org.au/next-justice-reinvestment-programs-announced/

**Northern Territory sites (Lajamanu, Maningrida, Central Australia).** Delivered through Aboriginal community-controlled organisations: Nja-marleya Cultural Leaders and Justice Group Ltd (Maningrida), Tangentyere Council and Lhere Artepe (Mparntwe / Alice Springs Peacemaking Project), CAYLUS (Mampu-Maninjaku). The framing names cultural leadership and on-Country crime prevention. Source: https://ministers.pmc.gov.au/mccarthy/2025/central-australia-justice-reinvestment-initiative-announced and https://ministers.ag.gov.au/media-centre/justice-reinvestment-being-delivered-alice-springs-and-halls-creek-15-06-2023

**National Justice Reinvestment Program (Commonwealth).** $69 million over four years for "up to 30 community-led justice reinvestment initiatives", $20 million per year ongoing from 2026-27.

> "Justice reinvestment is a long-term, community-led approach that aims to prevent crime, address the drivers of contact with the justice system, and improve justice outcomes for First Nations peoples in a particular place or community."

Source: https://www.ag.gov.au/legal-system/justice-reinvestment

### Shared vocabulary, and where government and community framing diverge

The shared vocabulary across all sites: **community-led, self-determination, place-based, local solutions, holistic, addressing the underlying causes of crime.** Both governments and communities use these words.

The divergence is in subject and verb:

- **Community framing** puts the community as the *governing actor* and the system as the thing that failed. Olabud Doogethu: "We must manage this problem ourselves." Maranguka: "community is in the drivers seat" and "attributable sovereignty" over data. The data is theirs and proves *their* leadership works.
- **Government framing** puts the government as the *enabler that empowers* the community, and the data as evidence of *program* effectiveness for *its* targets. The Commonwealth describes JR as aligned to "Outcomes 10 and 11 and the Priority Reforms under the National Agreement on Closing the Gap" (https://www.ag.gov.au/legal-system/justice-reinvestment). The verb "empowers" is the tell: it positions the giver of power above the receiver. Communities rarely describe themselves as "empowered" by government. They describe themselves as taking back what was theirs.

## 2. Limitations they name, and ones visible between the lines

**Short funding cycles and no secure base.** The Change the Record and Justice Reinvestment Network Australia joint pre-budget submission states plainly that "Existing JR sites require secure, long-term funding to ensure effective implementation" and asks that "each site is supported with secure, long-term funding for 5 years." The repeated demand for "secure, long-term funding for at least five years" exists because the default has been short, insecure grants. Source: https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-05/171663_change_the_record_and_justice_reinvestment_network_australia.pdf

**The pilot-to-policy gap.** Maranguka's 2018 KPMG impact assessment found that 2017 changes in Bourke produced a gross impact of $3.1 million, "approximately five times greater than the operational costs", split roughly two thirds justice-system impact and one third broader economic impact. Source: https://www.indigenousjustice.gov.au/wp-content/uploads/mp/files/resources/files/maranguka-justice-reinvestment-project-kpmg-impact-assessment-final-report.pdf . The limitation visible between the lines: a single site can prove the model financially and still wait years for the policy and funding architecture to follow. The ALRC had to recommend (Recommendation 4-1) that governments establish an independent JR body precisely because there was no standing mechanism to convert proven pilots into sustained investment: https://www.alrc.gov.au/publication/pathways-to-justice-inquiry-into-the-incarceration-rate-of-aboriginal-and-torres-strait-islander-peoples-alrc-report-133/4-justice-reinvestment/a-national-justice-reinvestment-body/

**Reporting frameworks built for funders, not communities.** Closing the Gap Priority Reform 4 (shared access to regional data) is the government's own commitment, yet the Productivity Commission's dashboard reports that no data on shared access at a regional level is available, and that "the lack of disaggregated data at a local and regional level is making it harder to develop localised strategies." Source: https://www.pc.gov.au/closing-the-gap-data/dashboard/priority-reform/priority-reform-4/ . Communities are asked to report up into frameworks that do not, in return, give them disaggregated local data they can use. The data flows one way.

**Who owns the data is unresolved.** In Bourke the data partnership pulls from 15 contributors including NSW Police, NSW Health, NSW Education and Communities and Justice. The aspiration is community ownership ("written and owned by Bourke people", https://benefolk.org/organisations/articles/community-data-access-and-sharing-is-at-the-heart-of-a-new-bourke-narrative), but the source datasets remain held by government agencies under agency rules. The community's "attributable sovereignty" (https://maranguka.org.au/) is asserted against a backdrop where it does not legally hold most of the data about its own people. That gap is the data sovereignty gap.

**Evaluation burden as a condition of funding.** The Commonwealth program design and the ANAO audit of it frame success in terms of grant compliance and program governance ("whether the award of funding was in accordance with the Commonwealth Grant Rules and Principles"): https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/the-award-of-funding-under-the-justice-reinvestment-program . The evaluation gaze points at whether communities spent the money correctly, not at whether the system around them changed. The burden of proof sits on the community; the system that produces over-incarceration is not asked to evaluate itself.

## 3. What support should look like under Indigenous data sovereignty

A community putting its justice data and stories into shared infrastructure is exposed. Four frameworks set the terms on which that exposure becomes safe.

**Maiam nayri Wingara (Australia).** The Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Data Sovereignty Collective, formed 2017, principles articulated in the 2018 Canberra communique. Indigenous people hold the right to "exercise control of the data ecosystem including creation, development, stewardship, analysis, dissemination, and infrastructure", and the right to "data that are relevant and empower sustainable self-determination and effective self-governance", "data that is contextual and disaggregated", and "data structures that are accountable to" First Nations communities. Source: https://www.maiamnayriwingara.org/mnw-principles . The operative test: does the data empower self-governance, and is it disaggregated enough to be useful locally.

**CARE (Global Indigenous Data Alliance, 2019).** Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics. Designed to sit alongside the FAIR principles (findable, accessible, interoperable, reusable) so that openness never overrides Indigenous authority. Source: https://www.gida-global.org/care and https://datascience.codata.org/articles/10.5334/dsj-2020-043 . The shift CARE makes: FAIR optimises for sharing, CARE asks who benefits and who controls before anything is shared.

**OCAP (Canada, First Nations).** Ownership, Control, Access, Possession. The community owns its information collectively, controls how it is collected and used, has the right to access data about itself, and physically or legally holds (possesses) it. Source: https://fnigc.ca/ocap-training/ (framework summarised in https://www.maiamnayriwingara.org/ and the CARE literature above). Possession is the hard edge: it asks where the data physically lives and who holds the keys.

**Mukurtu and cultural protocols.** Mukurtu CMS, built first with the Warumungu people (the name is a Warumungu word for a safe-keeping place), is the working example of protocol-level control in software. "Cultural protocols are the core of Mukurtu CMS. Protocols allow you to determine fine-grained levels of access to your digital heritage materials based on your community needs and values." Traditional Knowledge (TK) Labels let communities "define culturally sensitive access, use, and attribution terms" even on material they do not legally own. Source: https://mukurtu.org/about/ and https://mukurtu.org/support/traditional-knowledge-labels-faq/ . For a justice platform this means: access is not a single public/private switch. It is granular, set by the community, and can differ by item, by audience, and by cultural rule (for example, content about someone who has passed away).

### What makes a community trust a shared platform, versus refuse it

Trust is built by:

- **Control on their terms.** The community sets who sees what, item by item, the way Mukurtu protocols work. They can revoke and export ("Roundtrip" export without losing protocols, https://mukurtu.org/about/).
- **Benefit that returns to them.** Data they put in comes back as disaggregated, usable local intelligence, not just a number in someone's national target (Maiam nayri Wingara: "data that is contextual and disaggregated", https://www.maiamnayriwingara.org/mnw-principles).
- **Visibility framed as their strength.** Bourke's data tells the story of *their* leadership working. The platform amplifies the community's own narrative, "written and owned by Bourke people" (https://benefolk.org/organisations/articles/community-data-access-and-sharing-is-at-the-heart-of-a-new-bourke-narrative).
- **Attribution and possession.** Their name is on their data and they hold a copy. Maranguka calls this "attributable sovereignty" (https://maranguka.org.au/).

Refusal is triggered by:

- **Extraction.** Data flows out and never comes back as something useful, the one-way flow that Closing the Gap Priority Reform 4 has so far failed to reverse (https://www.pc.gov.au/closing-the-gap-data/dashboard/priority-reform/priority-reform-4/).
- **Government dashboards.** Data repurposed as a surveillance or compliance instrument pointed back at the community, or as evidence for someone else's KPI.
- **Deficit framing.** Numbers that define the community by crime and harm rather than by what its own leadership has changed. Olabud Doogethu's own words refuse this: the system "failed us", and they are the ones investing in their children (https://olabuddoogethu.org.au/about-us/).
- **Loss of control.** No item-level protocol, no export, no ability to take the data and leave.

## 4. Implications for JusticeHub

Five concrete commitments, derived directly from sections 1 to 3.

1. **Item-level cultural protocols, never a single public/private switch.** Follow the Mukurtu model. Every story, dataset and case must carry community-set access rules, including rules for content involving people who have passed away. If the platform cannot express "this is visible to community only" and "this is visible publicly with attribution" as separate, community-controlled states, it is not safe to use. (From section 3, Mukurtu and TK Labels.)

2. **Export and possession by default.** A community must be able to take a full copy of its data and stories, with protocols intact, and leave. Possession is the hard edge of OCAP. A community that cannot walk away has not been given control, only access. (From section 3, OCAP and Mukurtu Roundtrip.)

3. **Data returns to the community disaggregated and useful, or it does not get collected.** Never replicate the one-way flow that Priority Reform 4 failed to deliver. Every contribution a community makes must come back as local, contextual, disaggregated intelligence the community can act on, in line with Maiam nayri Wingara. (From sections 2 and 3.)

4. **Amplify the community's own narrative; never run a deficit dashboard.** The platform tells the story the community is telling about itself, leadership working, kids growing up safe smart and strong, not a crime-and-harm scoreboard. Never build a surveillance or compliance view that points government metrics back at the community. This is the single fastest way to lose trust. (From sections 1 and 3.)

5. **Attribute sovereignty explicitly and visibly.** Every dataset and story carries the community's name as owner and author, the "attributable sovereignty" Maranguka names. JusticeHub is infrastructure the community uses, not a body that "empowers" them. Drop the word "empower" and the posture behind it. (From sections 1 and 3.)
