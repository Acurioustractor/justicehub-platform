## Post Content

I asked a 16-year-old in Alice Springs why young people get in trouble.

She didn't pause.

"Oppression."

She's been to Darwin youth detention. 1,500km from her family. Twelve-minute phone calls. Two-hour waits between calls.

She didn't say "boredom." She didn't say "bad choices." She said the word that every inquiry has circled around for decades without landing on.

She's 16.

After my last post about Diagrama in Spain, a lot of people asked: does anything like this exist here?

There is a version of that answer told through numbers. 1,076 community-led alternatives documented. 824 with cost data. Average cost: a fraction of the $1.3 million we spend per child per year in detention.

But numbers are a translation. Something always gets lost in the translation.

So let me tell you the fuller version.

In Alice Springs, an organisation called Oonchiumpa has been doing this work for years. 100% Aboriginal community-controlled. Founded by Kristy Bloomfield, a Central Arrernte woman whose grandfather was a stolen generation man taken to the Bungalow in Alice Springs. He went to extraordinary lengths to keep his own children from the same fate.

That history doesn't sit in a filing cabinet. It walks through the door every morning.

Tanya Turner, co-founder, graduated from UWA law school, worked as an Associate at the Supreme Court of Victoria — and came home. Because these are her kids. Her Country. Her responsibility.

Together they built something the system has never managed to build: trust.

Fred Campbell, one of their case workers, told me about a young man named Xavier. Other services didn't share his disability information. When the relationship got hard, they "distanced."

Oonchiumpa stayed. For years.

"He trusts us. We earned that trust."

When Xavier got the chance to build a bed from recycled plastic — a Stretch Bed designed for remote communities — he led the work. Despite reports about his "ability to understand," he knew exactly what he was doing.

He got paid. Bought shoes. Couldn't stop smiling.

That's not a program outcome. That's a young person standing over something he built with his own hands. Proud. Wanting to teach others.

Jackquann is 14. Lives with his grandfather. Been to detention. When I asked what he'd tell politicians, he said one word:

"Programs."

When I asked what would stop him getting in trouble:

"Looking after my family."

Oonchiumpa's numbers, for those who need them:
→ 95% diversion success
→ 72% of disengaged youth back in education
→ 97.6% cheaper than detention
→ Operation Luna: 21 high-risk young people on the NT Police list. By December, one remained.

But the number that matters most is the one you can't measure. The years Fred spent earning Xavier's trust. The midnight phone calls Kristy answers. The rides to school that nobody sees.

This is what Room 3 of CONTAINED is for.

Room 1 is the reality of detention. Room 2 is Diagrama — proof it can be different. Room 3 changes at every stop because the alternative isn't one model. It's hundreds of organisations in every community already doing the work.

Oonchiumpa will fill Room 3 when CONTAINED reaches Alice Springs. In Mount Druitt, it's Mounty Yarns. On Palm Island, it's PICC — 197 staff, 78% diversion. In each city, the local community tells its own story.

Room 3 is theirs.

It's what happens when community leads.
