# Inside a Basecamp: How Oonchiumpa Is Rewriting Youth Justice in Central Australia

*A JusticeHub feature — March 2026*

---

Australia spends $548,000 per year to detain one young person. In Victoria, it's $2.67 million. The reoffending rate sits above 80%.

In Alice Springs, a small Aboriginal community-controlled organisation called Oonchiumpa does things differently. Their results — 95% diversion success, 72% school re-engagement, at 97.6% less cost than detention — are not new. What's new is that we can finally see them in context.

Oonchiumpa is the founding Basecamp in the [ALMA Network](/alma) — a decentralised network of community organisations across Australia proving that alternatives to youth detention exist, work better, and cost less. This is their story.

---

## "That's Not My Home"

Jackquann is 14. He lives with his grandfather at Upper Camp in Alice Springs. He's been in stolen cars. Been to the Alice Springs Detention Centre. Has court dates he treats as routine.

When we asked what detention is like, he said: *"At six o'clock you get locked down. You wait till tomorrow."*

When we asked what would stop him getting in trouble: *"Looking after my family."*

When we asked what he'd tell politicians: *"Programs."*

Jackquann doesn't need a risk assessment. He needs what every 14-year-old needs — family, purpose, something to do. Detention takes away the one thing he says matters most: home and grandfather.

---

## "I Don't Like Going to Darwin Cause I Have No Family There"

Laquisha is 16. She lives with her auntie in camp near Alice Springs — overcrowded, drinking around her. She manages it herself. Goes to school at St Joseph's. Wants her driver's licence. Wants to travel.

She's been to Darwin youth detention — 1,500km from home. Twelve-minute phone calls. Two-hour waits between calls. Lockdowns because "not enough staff."

When we asked why young people get in trouble, she didn't hesitate.

*"Oppression."*

No qualifier. No hedging. A 16-year-old naming the root cause in one word, with more precision than most policy documents.

## "He Trusts Us. We Earned That Trust."

Fred Campbell is a youth case worker at Oonchiumpa. He married into the Bloomfield family. He guides younger employees, does case planning, runs care team meetings.

He told us about Xavier — one of Oonchiumpa's first clients, 3-4 years ago. Xavier has a disability. Other service providers didn't share that information. When the relationship got hard, those services "distanced" from him.

Oonchiumpa didn't.

*"He trusts us. We earned that trust."*

Xavier was released from detention at the start of the year. He hasn't been back in trouble. When Oonchiumpa gave him the chance to build a [Stretch Bed](https://www.goodsoncountry.com/) — a bed made from recycled plastic designed for remote communities — Xavier led the work.

*"He just was so proud showing them that he can build it. After that he felt so happy about himself... He's quite capable of building that on his own and sharing that onto other kids."*

Fred sees immediate community application for the beds: ceremony business, bush trips, sleeping off the ground (snakes, scabies from dogs on mattresses), preventing rheumatic heart disease. Families move away from a place after a death (sorry business) — portable beds are perfect for this cultural practice.

Xavier's transformation didn't come from a structured intervention. It came from being trusted with real work.

## What Oonchiumpa Actually Does

Oonchiumpa was founded by **Kristy Bloomfield** — a Central Arrernte, Eastern Arrernte and Alyawarra woman, Traditional Owner of Mparntwe — and **Tanya Turner** — an Eastern Arrernte woman who graduated from UWA law school and worked as an Associate at the Supreme Court of Victoria before coming home.

Together they built something that is not another program designed by outsiders:

- **Youth Mentorship & Cultural Healing** — case workers like Fred walking alongside young people for years, not weeks
- **True Justice: Deep Listening on Country** — partnership with Professor Helen Milroy and ANU, training the next generation of lawyers and magistrates
- **Atnarpa Homestead On-Country Experiences** — cultural camps on Max Bloomfield's family country, east of Alice Springs
- **Cultural Brokerage & Service Navigation** — connecting young people across 7 language groups with the 32+ partner organisations that can help

The model isn't complicated. It's relationship-based. Fred picks up Nigel (14, wants to be a footy player) and takes him to school. Takes him to the cinema. Shows up consistently. Nigel has a clear goal — footy → school → job → house. What was missing wasn't motivation. It was someone to walk the path with him.

Kristy says: *"These aren't 'bad kids', they're children without basics many Australians take for granted. The system responds with detention that costs millions and changes nothing. We respond with cultural connection, practical support, and the authority that comes from being their Elders, their Traditional Owners, their family."*

## The Cross-Cutting Pattern

Across every young person we spoke with, seven themes emerged:

**1. Family connection is everything.** Every young person names family as what detention takes away. Not freedom — family.

**2. Home is complex but still home.** Camp life involves drinking and overcrowding. But young people don't say "I need a new home." They say "that's home." The need isn't removal from community — it's making community safer and more supportive.

**3. Boredom leads directly to trouble.** Stealing cars "to cruise around." Fighting because "they got nothing to do at home." The need is meaningful things to do — basketball, footy, youth centres, building beds, going to cinema, bush tucker. Not programs-as-punishment.

**4. Goals exist but pathways don't.** Nigel wants to play footy. Jackquann wants "just job." Laquisha wants her licence. Xavier wanted to contribute. These are concrete goals. What's missing is someone to walk the path with them.

**5. Culture is wanted but not yet accessed.** Jackquann wants to learn culture. Fred sees ceremony and bush trips as central. The sites exist — Lobsters Creek, Atnarpa Homestead. The access doesn't yet.

**6. The system is felt as punishment, not support.** Nobody describes any part of the justice system as helpful. Court is "scary" or routine. Detention is boring or violent. Fred's counter-model: *"We earned that trust."*

**7. Digital life is real life.** Fights start on Instagram. Status, reputation, conflict — all mediated through social media. Largely invisible to the adults and services around them.

## What a Basecamp Is

Oonchiumpa is the founding Basecamp in the [ALMA Network](/alma) — a new model for how community organisations can connect, share evidence, and build collective power.

A Basecamp is a state-level coordinator. The infrastructure. Not a head office — a hub. Basecamps:

- Coordinate local organisations in their state
- Run [CONTAINED](/contained) screenings and community events
- Feed stories, data, and evidence into the platform
- Host learning trips for other network members
- Represent the network to funders and government

Oonchiumpa is already doing all of this. In June 2026, they're hosting a learning trip to South East Queensland — visiting MMEIC on Minjerribah, Adapt Mentorship in Toowoomba, and the Goods on Country manufacturing facility at Witta Farm. Eight staff, six days, sharing models across state lines.

The other current Basecamps:
- [**PICC**](/alma/basecamps/picc) — Palm Island, QLD. 197 staff, 78% diversion rate. Culture is protection.
- [**Mounty Yarns**](/alma/basecamps/mounty-yarns) — Mount Druitt, NSW. Youth-led storytelling. 30% pursuing media careers.
- [**BQ Fit**](/alma/basecamps/bq-fit) — Inala, QLD. Boxing, fitness, wellbeing.

Growing to one Basecamp per state.

## The Point

Oonchiumpa is not an exception. It's a pattern. The same thing is happening on Palm Island, in Mount Druitt, in Inala, in communities across the country. Aboriginal-led. Community-controlled. Relationship-based. Cheaper, more effective, more humane.

What Oonchiumpa proves is what Fred already knows: the system fails by distancing. By writing off capability. By not sharing information. By not trusting.

The alternative succeeds by staying. By earning trust over years. By treating young people as capable. By being family.

Xavier built a bed. Jackquann wants to look after his grandfather. Nigel wants to play footy. Laquisha named the root cause in one word.

The cure already exists. It lives in community.
