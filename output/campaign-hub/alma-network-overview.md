# The ALMA Network

**Alternative Local Models of Australia**

A decentralised network built on one premise: the communities closest to the problem already have the answer. ALMA connects, documents, and amplifies the organisations proving that alternatives to youth detention exist, work better, and cost a fraction of what we spend locking children up.

---

## The Model — From the Ground Up

ALMA didn't start with a framework. It started with relationships.

In Alice Springs, **Oonchiumpa** — a 100% Aboriginal community-controlled organisation — has been doing the work for years. Founded by **Kristy Bloomfield** (Central Arrernte, Eastern Arrernte and Alyawarra woman, Traditional Owner of Mparntwe) and **Tanya Turner** (Eastern Arrernte, UWA law graduate, former Supreme Court Associate), Oonchiumpa works across 7 language groups within a 150km radius of Alice Springs, partnering with 32+ organisations.

Their results: 95% diversion success. 72% school re-engagement. 97.6% cheaper than detention.

But the numbers only tell part of it. The model is built on something the system doesn't measure: **trust earned over years**.

---

## What Trust Looks Like in Practice

**Fred Campbell**, a youth case worker at Oonchiumpa, describes working with a young man named Xavier — one of their first clients 3-4 years ago. Other service providers didn't share his disability information. Those services "distanced" from him when the relationship got hard.

Oonchiumpa stayed.

> *"He trusts us. We earned that trust."*

Xavier was released from detention at the start of the year. Hasn't been back in trouble. When given the chance to build a Stretch Bed from recycled plastic, Xavier led the work — despite reports about his "ability to understand."

> *"He just was so proud showing them that he can build it. After that he felt so happy about himself... He's quite capable of building that on his own and sharing that onto other kids."*

Fred's insight is the one the system keeps missing: **the transformation came from being trusted with real work, not from programs designed for him.**

## What Young People Actually Say

We sat down with young people connected to Oonchiumpa. Not to interview them for a report. To listen.

**Jackquann** (14, Upper Camp, Alice Springs) lives with his grandfather. He's been in stolen cars, been to detention, been through court. When asked what would stop him getting in trouble: *"Looking after my family."* When asked what he'd tell politicians: *"Programs."*

His core need: connection to family, belonging, purpose through work. Detention takes him away from the one thing that matters — home and grandfather.

**Nigel** (14, Double Camp station) likes school, wants to be a footy player, knows he needs to "go to school every day" to get there. Oonchiumpa picks him up, takes him to school, takes him to cinema. He values this practical, consistent presence.

His core need: someone to walk the path with him. He has the goal. What's missing is the support to stay on it.

**Laquisha** (16, camps near Alice Springs) lives with her auntie. She manages a complex living situation — overcrowding, drinking — with more maturity than most adults. She's been to Darwin youth detention: sent 1,500km from home, 12-minute phone calls, 2-hour waits between calls.

When asked why young people get in trouble, she said one word without hesitation: *"Oppression."*

Her core need: safety at home, family connection, independence — and being seen. She names the root cause. Not personal failing.

**Every young person names family as the primary thing detention takes away. Not freedom — family.**

## How ALMA Works

The network is built on three roles, drawn from how decentralised systems actually function:

### Basecamps — State-Level Coordinators

Established community organisations that anchor the network in each state. They connect local organisations, tell stories, build relationships with funders and government, and keep everything grounded in community authority.

**Current Basecamps:**
- **Oonchiumpa** — NT (Mparntwe/Alice Springs). The founding Basecamp. Indigenous-led, 7 language groups, 32 partner orgs. Kristy Bloomfield, Tanya Turner, Fred Campbell, Aunty Bev & Uncle Terry, Max Bloomfield (Traditional Owner, Atnarpa Station), Professor Helen Milroy (True Justice, ANU).
- **Mounty Yarns** — NSW (Mount Druitt, Western Sydney). Youth-led storytelling. 30% of participants now pursuing media careers. Shayle and Leah leading.
- **BQ Fit** — QLD (Inala, Brisbane). Boxing, fitness, and wellbeing for young people.
- **PICC** — QLD (Palm Island). Palm Island Community Company. 197 staff, 78% diversion rate. Culture is protection.

**What Basecamps do:**
- Coordinate local organisations in their state
- Run CONTAINED screenings and community events
- Feed stories, data, and evidence back into the platform
- Host learning trips for other network members (Oonchiumpa hosting SEQ trip, June 2026)
- Represent the network to funders and government

**Growing to:** WA, TAS, VIC, SA, ACT — one per state.

### Evidence Contributors — Community Organisations Doing the Work

The 1,076+ community organisations running youth programs across Australia. They contribute stories, outcomes data, cost data, and lived experience. Every program they run generates proof that alternatives work.

**What they contribute:**
- Program models — mentoring, arts, sport, cultural healing, diversion, family support
- Costs, outcomes, and stories through the platform
- Peer connections and shared learning
- Community verification of what actually works

### Community Verifiers — The Trust Layer

Community members, researchers, Elders, and practitioners who confirm that what organisations claim is actually happening. This isn't academic peer review — it's community accountability. An Elder who knows the program. A youth worker who's seen the outcomes. A researcher who's studied the model.

**Evidence levels (honest labelling):**
- **Proven** (4) — RCT or quasi-experimental, replicated results
- **Effective** (32) — Strong evaluation, positive outcomes
- **Promising** (405) — Community-endorsed, emerging evidence
- **Indigenous-led** (23) — Culturally grounded, community authority
- **Untested** (536) — Real programs, just not yet evaluated

536 untested programs is an argument for evaluation funding, not a weakness.

## The Oonchiumpa Model — Why It Works

Oonchiumpa's approach isn't a program you can replicate from a manual. It's a set of principles embedded in relationship:

1. **Stay when others distance.** Other services wrote off Xavier. Oonchiumpa built trust over years.
2. **Treat young people as capable.** Xavier led the bed-building. Jackquann wants to look after his family. Laquisha manages a complex life with clarity. These aren't deficits — they're strengths.
3. **Family is the unit, not the individual.** Fred: *"We say to you guys, you know, your family now."*
4. **Practical support, not just programs.** Rides to school. Cinema trips. Showing up consistently. Nigel values this more than any structured intervention.
5. **Culture is wanted and needed.** Jackquann wants to learn culture. Fred sees ceremony, bush trips, and sorry business as central. The Lobsters Creek property and Atnarpa Homestead are sites for this — real places, not program settings.
6. **The system fails by not sharing, not listening, and not trusting.** Fred's core insight: *"The system fails young people by not sharing information, by distancing when relationships get hard, by writing off capability."*

This is the Basecamp model. Oonchiumpa is the proof of concept. PICC on Palm Island is the same pattern — culture as protection, community doing what community has always done. Mounty Yarns in Mount Druitt is the same again — young people leading their own narrative.

## What Makes It Different

1. **It started with people, not data.** Kristy, Tanya, Fred, Xavier, Jackquann, Nigel, Laquisha — the network exists because of relationships, not because of a platform.

2. **Community-owned data.** Organisations own their stories and data. The platform aggregates, it doesn't extract.

3. **Honest evidence.** Half the models are untested. We say so. That's more credible than inflating everything to "evidence-based."

4. **The cost argument is irrefutable.** $548K vs $170K. 3x cheaper. Better outcomes.

5. **Indigenous-led where it matters.** Basecamps in NT and QLD are Indigenous organisations. The network centres community authority, not institutional authority.

6. **CONTAINED is the front door.** The documentary creates the emotional case. The platform provides the evidence. The young people's voices hold it together.

7. **Young people's words are unedited.** We don't clean them up, interpret them, or soften them. Laquisha said "oppression." That's the word.
