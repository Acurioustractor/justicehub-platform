## Post Content

A 16-year-old girl in Alice Springs was sent to Darwin youth detention — 1,500km from her family.

She got 12-minute phone calls. Then waited 2 hours to call again.

When we asked why young people get in trouble, she said one word: "Oppression."

She didn't need a program. She needed to be with her family.

---

This is what CONTAINED is about. Not statistics — recognition. What does it actually feel like to be a child in Australia's justice system?

But CONTAINED doesn't stop at showing the problem. At every tour stop, local community organisations fill the third room with their proof that something better already exists.

In Alice Springs, that's Oonchiumpa.

Fred Campbell, a youth case worker there, told us about a young man named Xavier. Other services wrote him off — didn't share his disability information, then "distanced" when the relationship got hard.

Oonchiumpa stayed. For years. They earned his trust.

When Xavier got the chance to build a bed from recycled plastic, he led the work. Despite reports about his "ability to understand," he knew exactly what he was doing. He got paid. Bought shoes. Couldn't stop smiling.

Fred's insight: "The system fails young people by not sharing information, by distancing when relationships get hard, by writing off capability."

Oonchiumpa succeeds by doing the opposite. Staying. Trusting young people with real work. Treating them as capable.

95% diversion success. 72% school re-engagement. 97.6% cheaper than detention.

But the real measure is Xavier standing over something he built with his own hands, proud, wanting to teach others.

That's not a pilot program. That's a proven model — already being studied by Flinders University and running ANU's True Justice initiative.

The alternative to detention isn't theoretical. It's Fred driving a kid to school at 7am. It's Kristy Bloomfield answering the phone at midnight. It's a young person building beds instead of sitting in a cell.

CONTAINED launches April 20. Come sit in the room. Then tell us who else needs to.
