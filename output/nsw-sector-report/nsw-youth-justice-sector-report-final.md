## 1. Executive Summary

New South Wales spends **$327 million per year** on youth justice (ROGS 2024-25). Two-thirds of that money -- $217 million -- goes to detention. On any given night, 184 young people are locked in one of six youth justice centres. Sixty per cent are Aboriginal. Seventy per cent have not been convicted of anything.

In March 2024, the NSW Government introduced new bail laws targeting 14-18 year olds. Since then, youth detention has surged 34 per cent. Eighty-five to ninety per cent of those refused bail under the new laws are First Nations children. Five hundred legal practitioners, sixty organisations, and the Aboriginal Legal Service called the laws a failure. The government extended them for three more years.

Meanwhile, Aboriginal community-controlled organisations receive zero dollars from NSW's direct "Breaking the Cycle of Reoffending" grants ($9.9 million across 41 grants -- every dollar to non-Indigenous intermediaries). The broader DCJ/FACS grant pool sends $5 billion through 2,675 organisations; only 3.2 per cent are Indigenous-controlled. The first ACCO appears at rank 19 in the recipient list.

Philanthropic funders have taken a structurally different approach. The Paul Ramsay Foundation, Dusseldorp Forum, NIAA, and others have invested $115 million in NSW -- 70.9 per cent directed through Indigenous-led organisations. They fund Australia's only KPMG-validated justice reinvestment model (Maranguka, Bourke: 5:1 return on investment), youth-led advocacy (Mounty Yarns, Mt Druitt), and six active justice reinvestment sites across the state.

NSW has 154 mapped youth justice programs, including 4 with Proven evidence (RCT or quasi-experimental replication) and 16 rated Effective. The cheapest Effective program costs $1,708 per young person per year. Detention costs $939,000. The ratio is 550:1.

The NSW Select Committee on Youth Justice reports in December 2026. The evidence is already in.

**Key findings:**

- **$1.33 million** national average detention cost per young person per year (ROGS 2024-25); NSW detention cost: **$939,000/year** ($2,573/day)
- **234 young people** in NSW custody as of June 2025, up **34%** since the 2024 bail laws (BOCSAR)
- **60%** of detained youth are Aboriginal, despite being **4.5-8%** of the youth population (BOCSAR)
- **79.8%** of Aboriginal youth in detention are on remand -- not convicted (BOCSAR)
- **82%** of children aged 10-13 in court had prior child protection reports; **53%** of charges withdrawn (BOCSAR BB171)
- **81%** reconviction rate within 10 years of release (BOCSAR BB162)
- **3.2%** of government-funded organisations are Indigenous-controlled vs **70.9%** of philanthropic-funded organisations
- **$0** in direct "reoffending" grants to Aboriginal community-controlled organisations
- **1,081** verified community-based interventions mapped nationally; **154** in NSW
- **4 Proven** and **16 Effective** programs in NSW -- more than any other Australian state

### The bail crisis

In March 2024, the NSW Government passed the Bail and Crimes Amendment Act 2024, introducing a new section 22C bail test imposing a "high degree of confidence" threshold for 14-18 year olds accused of repeat serious offences. Since then:

- Youth detention **up 34%** -- from 175 (June 2023) to 234 (June 2025) (BOCSAR)
- Break-and-enter custody **up 229%** in two years (BOCSAR)
- **85-90%** of those refused bail under the new test are First Nations children (Justice and Equity Centre; ALHR)
- **79.8%** of Aboriginal youth in detention are on remand -- not convicted (BOCSAR)
- **80%** of young people are refused bail overall under the amended provisions (NSW Greens, Budget Estimates)
- The laws were extended to April 2028 in February 2025, with a **$6M+ support package** (DCJ)
- **500+ legal practitioners** signed an open letter opposing the laws in March 2024 (SBS NITV)
- **60 organisations** wrote separately to Premier Minns opposing the legislation (SBS NITV)

The NSW Law Society called the "high degree of confidence" test "unprecedented" -- a novel standard in NSW law that reverses the presumption of innocence for children (Law Society Journal). The NSW Bar Association President, Dr Ruth Higgins SC, warned the laws would lead to "greater incarceration of vulnerable children" (National Indigenous Times).

ALS CEO Karly Warner: *"These bail laws have put another generation of young children into an express training program for a life of crime."*

This is a remand crisis driven by legislation, not a crime wave. Youth offending nationally has fallen 28 per cent over the past decade (ABS 2023-24). Regional NSW youth court proceedings fell 13.7 per cent to December 2025 (BOCSAR). In Dubbo, youth crime halved (BOCSAR).

### First Nations over-representation

Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander young people are 4.5 per cent of the NSW youth population. They are:

- **60%** of the youth detention population (BOCSAR June 2025)
- Detained at **22.1 times** the rate of non-Indigenous young people (ROGS 17A.7)
- Supervised in the community at **63.9 per 10,000** vs 2.9 per 10,000 for non-Indigenous youth (ROGS 17A.8)
- Indigenous children in NSW detention have increased **86% since 2020-21** (SBS NITV, February 2026)

In out-of-home care -- the upstream pipeline to youth justice -- Aboriginal children in NSW are placed at **45.1 per 1,000** (SNAICC, June 2025). NSW has the lowest restoration rate in Australia at **15.2%** (SNAICC). Of 126 recommendations from the Family is Culture review, only **12 have been completed** as of February 2024 (AbSec).

## 4. Community Alternatives

NSW has **154 mapped youth justice programs**, including 4 with Proven evidence and 16 rated Effective -- the strongest evidence base of any Australian state. Ten of the sixteen Effective programs are Indigenous-led.

### Evidence distribution

| Evidence Level | Count | % |
|---------------|-------|---|

| Effective (strong evaluation, positive outcomes) | **16** | 10% |

| Indigenous-led (culturally grounded, community authority) | **13** | 8% |
| Untested (theory/pilot stage) | **49** | 32% |

*Source: JusticeHub evidence classification, March 2026*

### Maranguka Justice Reinvestment (Bourke)

Australia's first and most rigorously evaluated justice reinvestment initiative, led by the Ngemba community and co-seeded by the Dusseldorp Forum and Vincent Fairfax Family Foundation from 2013.

**KPMG-validated outcomes:**
- **5:1 economic return** ($3.1M gross impact on $0.6M operational cost)
- **42% reduction** in custody days
- **23% drop** in domestic violence
- **38% reduction** in juvenile offences
- **31% rise** in Year 12 retention

Maranguka and Just Reinvest NSW together have 22 programs mapped in the JusticeHub database. Programs include Coordinated Youth Engagement, Driver Licensing, Bail Support, Community Safety Patrols, Warrant Clinics, Cultural Programs, and the Tribal Connect Youth Hub. The model has expanded to **six JR sites** across NSW: Bourke, Mt Druitt, Moree, Nowra, Cowra, and Kempsey.

*Sources: KPMG Preliminary Assessment (2018); JustReinvest NSW; JusticeHub DB (22 programs, 16 rated Effective)*

### Youth Koori Court

Operating since 2015 in Parramatta, 2019 in Surry Hills, and 2023 in Dubbo. Evaluation findings:

- **40% less likely** to receive a custodial sentence than mainstream court
- **No adverse impact** on reoffending rates
- 150+ young people supported over 10 years

*Source: NSW Children's Court evaluation; National Indigenous Times*

### Yuwaya Ngarra-li (Walgett)

A 23-year partnership between the Dharriwaa Elders Group and the Development Effectiveness Group (UNSW). Their place-based model -- Baulaarr Bagay Warruwi Burranba-li-gu (Two River Pathway to Change) -- has reduced court appearances to **7.8%**. Won an APPI grant in November 2025 and submitted to the NSW Select Committee in March 2026.

*Sources: Law Society Journal; UNSW Social Determinants of Justice Hub; Dharriwaa Elders Group*

### Mounty Yarns / MAYCS (Mt Druitt)

From one person with a camera to a 20-person team. $3.3 million raised in grants. NSW's first Community-led Youth Justice Roundtable at UNSW (May 2025). Documentary with 100K+ views screened at TEDxSydney, the NSW Children's Court Conference, and the PRF 2024 keynote. The Backyard Campus -- yarning circle, gym container, basketball court, Aboriginal flag ground mural, community kitchen -- was designed by young people, endorsed by six Elders.

Seven programs: Backyard Campus ($8K/youth), Justice Reinvestment Advocacy Workshops ($3.5K), Youth on Track ($5K), OzTag/Native Son's ($5K), Girls Program ($5K), Music Program ($5K), Documentary and Storytelling ($5K). All Indigenous-led. The Empathy Ledger holds 14 completed transcripts from Mounty Yarns young people.

Mounty Yarns receives $1M+ in philanthropic funding from three sources -- Paul Ramsay Foundation ($600K), Dusseldorp Forum ($250K), Bill and Patricia Ritchie Foundation ($150K). It receives **zero dollars** from the DCJ "Breaking the Cycle of Reoffending" grants.

*Sources: Empathy Ledger v2 (14 transcripts, 1 published story); JusticeHub DB (7 programs); PRF portfolio; Dusseldorp YIR 2025*

### Dubbo SAY Program

Youth crime in Dubbo **almost halved** -- from 317 incidents (2024) to 159 (2025). The Oyster Tribe delivers the SAY (Sustainable, Accessible Youth services) program with place-based community safety approaches.

*Source: BOCSAR Recorded Crime Statistics December 2025; DCJ*

### Moree -- most heavily funded regional hotspot

$13.4M + $2M additional government investment. Three local ACCOs -- Miyay Birray Youth Aboriginal Corporation, SHAE Academy, Pius X Aboriginal Medical Service -- forming a consortium for bail accommodation. Ninety young people per night in after-hours activities.

*Source: DCJ media releases; Law Society Journal*

## 5. The Accountability Gap

### What government promised vs what happened

**Five First Nations strategies introduced -- none tracked spending or outcomes.** The NSW Auditor-General found the strategies "had little to no impact" on reoffending. Fifty-six per cent of youth in custody are Indigenous despite being 3.4 per cent of the population. The Auditor-General concluded that strategies existed on paper but not in practice.

*Source: NSW Auditor-General, First Nations Audit*

**Reintegration is failing.** A separate Auditor-General performance audit found less than 50 per cent of young people were working or studying on release, and only 33 per cent were engaged in community activities post-release. Transition planning was inadequate.

*Source: NSW Auditor-General, Reintegrating Young Offenders*

**Family is Culture recommendations stalled.** Of 126 recommendations from the Family is Culture review of Aboriginal child protection, only **12 have been completed** as of February 2024. Only **5.5%** of child protection funding goes to ACCOs (AbSec).

*Source: AbSec, February 2024*

**Strip searches of children found "oppressive."** The NSW Ombudsman investigated strip searches of three children at Frank Baxter Youth Justice Centre (November 2019) and found them "unreasonable," "oppressive," and "wrong." Corrective Services officers (adult prison staff) conducted the searches. Children were strip-searched naked in front of CCTV cameras. The Ombudsman recommended legislative prohibition of routine strip searches of children. The government refused to implement the safeguard.

*Source: NSW Ombudsman, Report to Parliament: Strip Searches at Frank Baxter YJC*

**Discriminatory bail enforcement.** The Justice and Equity Centre (formerly PIAC) exposed that NSW Police conducted 150+ bail compliance checks on two Aboriginal brothers in 20 months and filed racial discrimination complaints. A secret NSW Police policy harmful to young people was also exposed.

*Source: Justice and Equity Centre*

**Inspector of Custodial Services: 51 recommendations.** The 2022 inspection of all six youth justice centres found holding rooms substandard at Frank Baxter, Acmena, and Orana. Staff vacancies stood at 51, with an 18 per cent burnout rate. The 2025 inspection has been completed; the report is pending.

*Source: NSW Inspector of Custodial Services, 2022 Report; DCJ Annual Report*

**Disability Royal Commission recommendations partially accepted.** Recommendation 8.3 (prohibit solitary confinement in youth detention) was accepted only "in part." Recommendation 8.22 (raise the age of criminal responsibility) has not been actioned.

*Source: Disability Royal Commission; NSW Government response*

### International condemnation

- **Human Rights Watch** (May 2025): ~700 children detained nationally on any given day; called for raising the age to 14 and ending child incarceration
- **HRW World Report 2025 and 2026**: NSW bail crackdown singled out; 60% First Nations
- **AHRC 'Help Way Earlier!' Report** (August 2024): 24 recommendations for transforming child justice; described children as experiencing "the most egregious breaches of human rights"; NSW case study: Yuwaya Ngarra-li (Walgett)
- **Federal Senate Inquiry** interim report (February 2025): "significant and disturbing evidence"; "near universal agreement the system is damaging children"

*Sources: HRW reports 2025-2026; AHRC Help Way Earlier! August 2024; Senate Standing Committee on Legal and Constitutional Affairs, interim report February 2025*

### Oversight recommendations: the pattern

Across the NSW Auditor-General, Inspector of Custodial Services, Ombudsman, AHRC, Disability Royal Commission, and Senate Inquiry, the pattern is consistent: recommendations are made, partially accepted, and not implemented. Five strategies with no outcome tracking. Fifty-one facility recommendations. A legislative prohibition recommended and refused. Twenty-four AHRC recommendations. Twelve of 126 Family is Culture recommendations completed. The system generates oversight -- but does not act on it.

## 6. Civic Intelligence Findings

JusticeHub's civic intelligence layer, CivicScope, cross-references parliamentary Hansard transcripts, ministerial diary disclosures, procurement data, and oversight body recommendations to identify structural accountability gaps.

### Parliamentary discourse: detention vs alternatives

Analysis of Hansard transcripts containing youth justice references reveals a significant imbalance. Nationally, **47 parliamentary speeches** mention detention, while only **10 mention alternatives** -- a ratio of approximately 5:1 (CivicScope analysis). The policy discourse recorded in Hansard does not reflect the evidence base. Proven programs with KPMG-validated returns receive a fraction of the airtime that enforcement measures command.

In NSW specifically, the dominant legislative activity has been the Bail and Crimes Amendment Act 2024, its extension, and the Children (Criminal Proceedings) Amendment Bill 2025 (which codified doli incapax). The NSW Select Committee on Youth Justice (established November 2025, reporting December 2026) represents the first dedicated parliamentary examination of evidence-based alternatives.

*Source: CivicScope Hansard analysis; parliament.nsw.gov.au*

### The ministerial access gap

Of the **678 organisations** that deliver youth justice programs in the JusticeHub database nationally, and the organisations recorded in ministerial diary disclosures, the overlap is **10 organisations -- 1.5 per cent**. The minister met with **192 organisations** during the period analysed. Community-based service providers, particularly Aboriginal community-controlled organisations, are largely absent from the ministerial consultation record.

*Source: CivicScope ministerial diary analysis; JusticeHub organisation database*

### Consultancy spending vs community investment

Nationally, **$1.85 million** was paid to **18 consulting firms** for youth justice strategy and policy advice, while the median community-based program costs **$77,000 per young person per year** (JusticeHub, 305 programs with cost data). In NSW, youth justice-specific consultancy spending is **not separately reported** -- buried in DCJ-wide aggregate financials. By contrast, Queensland reports "NIL" youth justice consultancy spending, which masks $1.85 million classified as "professional services."

Both jurisdictions lack transparency on advisory spending relative to direct program investment.

*Source: CivicScope procurement analysis; JusticeHub program cost database; QLD/NSW DCJ annual reports*

### Procurement transparency

NSW's GIPA (Government Information Public Access) disclosure log is chronological and untagged by topic -- worse than Queensland's categorised RTI log. Class 3 contracts ($5M+) are published under the GIPA Act, but there is no central searchable register equivalent to Queensland's Contract Disclosure.

## 7. Recommendations

The following recommendations are drawn from the data in this report. Each is actionable, specific, and supported by verified evidence.

### 1. Independently evaluate the bail laws before the 2028 sunset

The 2024 bail amendments produced a 34 per cent detention surge. Eighty-five to ninety per cent of bail refusals are First Nations children. Seventy per cent of detained youth are unconvicted. The laws should be independently evaluated -- by BOCSAR, not the department that introduced them -- before any further extension. The NSW Law Society, Bar Association, ALS, and 500+ legal practitioners have called for this.

*Evidence: BOCSAR Custody Statistics June 2025; JEC bail check data; 500+ practitioner open letter*

### 2. Fund Aboriginal community-controlled organisations directly

Zero ACCOs receive "Breaking the Cycle of Reoffending" grants. Zero appear in the top 20 of "Targeted Earlier Intervention" funding. The first ACCO in the overall DCJ/FACS grant list appears at rank 19. Philanthropic funders send 70.9 per cent of their money through Indigenous organisations. Government sends 3.2 per cent. Close this gap. Mandate minimum ACCO participation in all youth justice grant categories.

*Evidence: DCJ/FACS NGO Grant Data (6,095 grants, 2,675 orgs); JusticeHub philanthropic analysis*

### 3. Scale what works

These are not pilots. They are proven models with verified outcomes:

| Youth Koori Court | Independent evaluation | Marginal | 40% less custody, no reoffending increase |
| Yuwaya Ngarra-li (Walgett) | 23 years of data | Low | Court appearances down to 7.8% |
| Mounty Yarns (Mt Druitt) | Community-endorsed | $3.5-8K/year | 20-person youth-led team, 100K+ documentary views |
| Dubbo SAY | BOCSAR-verified | -- | Youth crime halved in one year |

Government should co-invest alongside philanthropy at these sites, not duplicate intermediary contracts. The PRF has shown the model: $40M+ through community-controlled organisations with evaluation built in.

*Evidence: KPMG Maranguka Assessment; Youth Koori Court evaluation; BOCSAR December 2025; Empathy Ledger (14 transcripts)*

### 4. Publish program-level outcome data

The NSW Auditor-General found five First Nations strategies with no outcome tracking. One hundred and fifty-four programs are mapped in the JusticeHub database. Attach outcomes to them. No program should receive public funding without published outcome tracking.

*Evidence: NSW Auditor-General, First Nations Audit; JusticeHub DB (154 NSW programs)*

### 5. Tag DCJ grants by youth justice

$5 billion flows through DCJ/FACS with no way to isolate youth justice spending. NSW's eTendering portal cannot filter by youth justice. Add a youth justice tag to grant data and procurement categories so the public, the Select Committee, and the Auditor-General can see where money goes.

*Evidence: DCJ/FACS NGO Grant Data; buy.nsw.gov.au analysis*

### 6. Map Burrun Dalai Aboriginal Corporation

$59 million in government funding. Zero programs mapped. This is the largest government-funded ACCO in NSW. Either the programs exist and should be documented, or the funding is not reaching frontline delivery. The Select Committee should request a full account.

*Evidence: JusticeHub DB; DCJ/FACS NGO Grant Data*

### 7. Raise the age of criminal responsibility

The ACT has done it. Scotland did it decades ago. BOCSAR's own data shows 82 per cent of 10-13 year olds in court had child protection reports and 53 per cent of their charges were withdrawn. These children are being processed through the justice system, disrupted, and released without conviction. This is expensive, harmful, and achieves nothing. A coalition of 130+ organisations and $2.3 million in donated media supports this change.

*Evidence: BOCSAR BB171; Raise the Age NSW (130+ orgs); AHRC Help Way Earlier! (24 recommendations)*

### 8. Address the cross-system pipeline

Eighty-two per cent of children aged 10-13 in court had prior child protection reports. Sixty-five per cent of youth justice supervised had prior child protection contact. The pipeline is identifiable years in advance. Invest in the child protection system -- not the criminal justice system -- for these children. NSW's $1.2 billion child protection package (2025-26 Budget) is a start, but only 5.5 per cent of child protection funding currently goes to ACCOs.

*Evidence: BOCSAR BB171; Legal Aid NSW Submission; AbSec; NSW Budget 2025-26*
