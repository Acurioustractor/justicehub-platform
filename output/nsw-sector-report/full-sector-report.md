## Executive Summary

New South Wales spends **$327 million per year** on youth justice (ROGS 2024-25). On any given night, **184 young people** are in NSW detention. **60% are Aboriginal** — despite Aboriginal young people being 8% of the youth population. **70% are unsentenced**, held on remand before trial.

In March 2024, the NSW Government introduced new bail laws targeting 14-18 year olds. Since then, youth detention has surged **34%** — from 175 to 234 young people in custody. **85-90% of those refused bail under the new laws are First Nations children.** 500 legal practitioners, 60 organisations, and the Aboriginal Legal Service have called the laws a failure. The government extended them for three more years.

The state's direct youth justice NGO grants — "Breaking the Cycle of Reoffending" — total **$9.9 million across 41 grants**. Every dollar goes to non-Indigenous intermediaries. **Zero Aboriginal community-controlled organisations receive reoffending grants.** Meanwhile, the broader DCJ/FACS grant pool sends $5 billion through 2,675 organisations — only **3.2% are Indigenous**.

Philanthropic funders have taken a different approach. The Paul Ramsay Foundation, Dusseldorp Forum, and others have invested **$115 million in NSW** — **71% through Indigenous-led organisations**. They fund Australia's only KPMG-validated justice reinvestment model (Maranguka, Bourke: 5:1 return), youth-led advocacy (Mounty Yarns, Mt Druitt: $1M+ from three funders), and six active JR sites across the state.

NSW has **154 mapped programs** — including **4 with Proven evidence** and **16 rated Effective**. Ten of the sixteen Effective programs are Indigenous-led. The evidence base is stronger than any other Australian state. The cheapest Effective program costs **$1,708 per young person per year**. Detention costs **$939,000**.

The NSW Select Committee on Youth Justice reports in December 2026. The evidence is already in.

### The Bail Crisis

Since the Bail and Crimes Amendment Act 2024 (March 2024):

- Youth detention **up 34%** — from 175 (June 2023) to 234 (June 2025)
- Break-and-enter custody **up 229%** in two years

- **79.8%** of Aboriginal youth in detention are on remand — not convicted
- The laws were extended for three more years in May 2025, with a **$6M+ support package**

This is a **remand crisis driven by legislation**, not a crime wave.

*Sources: BOCSAR Custody Statistics June 2025, December 2024; SBS NITV, February 2025*

## 6. What the Sector Is Saying

### Four Political Camps

**Labor (Premier Minns):** Dual approach — tough bail laws plus investment in diversion. Has ruled out raising the age of criminal responsibility. $86.9M over 4 years announced ($66.9M diversion + $20M tech platform).

**Coalition (Henskens/Saunders):** Wants harder measures — electronic monitoring, mandatory curfews, expanding bail offences to all serious indictable crimes. Bill voted down by Labor.

**Greens (Boyd):** Raise the age of criminal responsibility to 16, detention age to 18, close all youth detention centres.

**Aboriginal Legal Service / Legal Sector:** Fiercely opposed to bail crackdown. ALS CEO Karly Warner: *"These bail laws have put another generation of young children into an express training program for a life of crime."*

## 7. Transparency and Accountability

### BOCSAR — NSW Bureau of Crime Statistics

Unlike QLD, NSW has BOCSAR — an independent statistical agency that publishes detailed youth justice data. Key publications:

- **BB171:** Young People Aged 10-13 — found 82% had child protection reports, 53% of charges withdrawn
- **BB162:** Long-term reoffending — 81% reconvicted within 10 years
- **Quarterly custody statistics** with demographic breakdowns

### Inspector of Custodial Services

- 6 youth justice centres inspected (2022 report: 51 recommendations)
- Holding rooms substandard at **Frank Baxter, Acmena, Orana**
- 2025 inspection completed — report pending

### NSW Ombudsman — Strip Searches

The Ombudsman investigated strip searches of three children at Frank Baxter Youth Justice Centre (November 2019) and found them **"unreasonable," "oppressive," and "wrong."**

- Corrective Services officers (adult prison staff) conducted the searches
- Children were fully strip-searched naked in front of CCTV cameras
- The Ombudsman recommended **legislative prohibition of routine strip searches** of children
- Body scanners are being rolled out as an alternative

*Source: NSW Ombudsman, Report to Parliament: Strip Searches at Frank Baxter YJC*

### Auditor-General

Two critical audits:

1. **Reintegrating Young Offenders:** Less than 50% working or studying on release. Only 33% in community activities.
2. **First Nations Audit:** Five strategies introduced, **none tracked spending or outcomes**. "Little to no impact" on reoffending. 56%+ of youth in custody are Indigenous despite being 3.4% of population.

### GIPA (Government Information Public Access)

- Youth Justice records available from 1991
- Disclosure log is **chronological and untagged by topic** — worse than QLD's categorised RTI log
- No easy way to determine refusal rates for YJ-specific requests

### Consultancy Spending

YJ-specific consultancy spend is **not separately reported** — buried in DCJ-wide aggregate financials. This is a transparency gap.

### Mount Druitt — Mounty Yarns (MAYCS)

**Mounty Yarns** began as one person with a camera in Western Sydney. It is now a 20-person team of young Aboriginal people who have raised **$3.3 million in grants**, hosted NSW's first Community-led Youth Justice Roundtable at UNSW (May 2025), and produced a documentary with **100K+ views** — screened at TEDxSydney, the NSW Children's Court Conference, and the RPC 2024 keynote.

The Backyard Campus in Mt Druitt — a yarning circle, gym container, basketball court, Aboriginal flag ground mural, and community kitchen — is the physical base. It was designed by young people, endorsed by six Elders: Aunty Zona Wilkinson, Aunty Jenny Ebsworth, Aunty Sandra Hickey, Aunty Elaine Gordon, Uncle Tony Hunter, and Aunty Lizzie May.

Mounty Yarns runs **7 programs**: Backyard Campus ($8K/youth), Justice Reinvestment Advocacy Workshops ($3.5K), Youth on Track ($5K), OzTag/Native Son's ($5K), Girls Program ($5K), Music Program ($5K), and Documentary & Storytelling ($5K). All are Indigenous-led.

The Empathy Ledger holds **14 completed transcripts** from Mounty Yarns — including the full documentary and individual interviews with young people:

**Youth Justice Interviews:** Shayle McKellar, Amelia, Leah, Adam, Taleigha, Tyrese
**Backyard Activation:** Isaiah, Taj, Isabella, Archie Darcy, Polly, Jaylee

These are young people speaking in their own words about justice, community, and what works. Their stories are not academic evidence — they are the evidence that community-controlled programs create something no government contract can purchase.

Mounty Yarns receives **$1M+ in philanthropic funding** from three sources — Paul Ramsay Foundation ($600K), Dusseldorp Forum ($250K), and the Bill & Patricia Ritchie Foundation ($150K). It receives **zero dollars** from the DCJ "Breaking the Cycle of Reoffending" grants.

*Source: Empathy Ledger v2, Mounty Yarns organisation (14 transcripts, 1 published story, 4 media items). JusticeHub: 7 programs mapped. Philanthropic funding: PRF portfolio, Dusseldorp YIR 2025, JusticeHub DB.*

### Bourke — Maranguka / Just Reinvest NSW

**Maranguka** is Australia's first and most rigorously evaluated justice reinvestment initiative. Run by the Ngemba community in Bourke, it was co-seeded by the Dusseldorp Forum and Vincent Fairfax Family Foundation from 2013.

**KPMG-validated outcomes:**
- **5:1 economic return** ($3.1M gross impact on $0.6M operational cost)
- **42% reduction in custody days**
- **23% drop in domestic violence**
- **38% reduction in juvenile offences**
- **31% rise in Year 12 retention**

Maranguka and Just Reinvest NSW together have **22 programs** in the JusticeHub database — the most of any NSW organisation pair. Programs include Coordinated Youth Engagement, Driver Licensing, Bail Support, Community Safety Patrols, Warrant Clinics, Cultural Programs, Tribal Connect Youth Hub, and the Men's Space.

The model has expanded to **six JR sites** across NSW: Bourke, Mt Druitt, Moree, Nowra, Cowra, and Kempsey (Learning the Macleay). The NSW JR Program received **$9.8M** through the PRF Justice Reinvestment portfolio review.

*Sources: JustReinvest NSW; KPMG Preliminary Assessment; JusticeHub DB (22 programs, 16 rated Effective)*

---

### Moree — Most Heavily Funded Regional Hotspot

Moree has received **$13.4M + $2M+ additional** government investment for youth justice, making it the most heavily funded regional site in NSW. Three local Aboriginal organisations — **Miyay Birray Youth Aboriginal Corporation**, **SHAE Academy**, and **Pius X Aboriginal Medical Service** — are forming a consortium for bail accommodation.

**90 young people per night** participate in after-hours activities through the programs.

*Source: DCJ media releases; Law Society Journal*

---

### Walgett — Yuwaya Ngarra-li

**Yuwaya Ngarra-li** (meaning "we all walk together") is a 23-year partnership between the Walgett community and the Development Effectiveness Group. Their place-based approach — **Baulaarr Bagay Warruwi Burranba-li-gu** (Two River Pathway to Change) — has reduced court appearances to **7.8%**.

Yuwaya Ngarra-li won an **APPI grant in November 2025** for "addressing social determinants of justice for criminalised children" and submitted to the NSW Select Committee in March 2026.

*Source: Law Society Journal; UNSW Social Determinants of Justice Hub*

---

### Dubbo — Measurable Results

Youth crime in Dubbo **almost halved** — from 317 incidents (2024) to 159 (2025). The Oyster Tribe delivers the SAY (Sustainable, Accessible Youth services) program with place-based community safety approaches.

*Source: Regional media; DCJ reporting*

## 9. The Cross-System Pipeline

| Crossover pathway | NSW Rate | Source |
|-------------------|---------|--------|
| 10-13yo in court with CP reports | **82%** | BOCSAR BB171 |
| Youth justice supervised with prior CP contact | **65%+** | Legal Aid NSW |
| 10-13yo court charges withdrawn | **53%** | BOCSAR BB171 |
| Reconviction within 10 years | **81%** | BOCSAR BB162 |

The pipeline is identifiable years in advance. **82% of children aged 10-13 who appear in court already had child protection reports.** More than half of their charges are withdrawn — meaning the system processes them, disrupts their lives, and releases them without conviction.

Professor Eileen Baldry (UNSW) has documented the crossover between disability, child protection, and youth justice — showing that cognitive disability, mental health, and out-of-home care are the strongest predictors of justice system contact, not criminal intent.

The NSW Auditor-General found that five First Nations strategies existed on paper but **none tracked spending or outcomes** and had **"little to no impact"** on the pipeline.

*Sources: BOCSAR BB171; BOCSAR BB162; Legal Aid NSW Submission; NSW Auditor-General; Baldry (UNSW)*

## 10. What Works — Proven Alternatives in NSW

### Maranguka Justice Reinvestment (Bourke)
**5:1 return.** 42% reduction in custody days. 23% drop in DV. KPMG-validated. Aboriginal-led. $5K/young person.

**40% less custody** than mainstream court. **No increase in reoffending.** Culturally appropriate diversionary mechanism operating in several NSW locations.

### Yuwaya Ngarra-li (Walgett)
Court appearances down to **7.8%**. 23-year community partnership. Won APPI grant 2025.

### Dubbo SAY Program
Youth crime **halved** in one year (317 → 159 incidents). Place-based, community-led.

### BackTrack Youth Works (Armidale)
Indigenous-led youth engagement through animal care, agriculture, and mentoring. Evidence base emerging.

## 14. Recommendations

### 1. Publish program-level outcome data
The NSW Auditor-General recommended this. No program should receive public funding without published outcome tracking. NSW has 154 mapped programs — attach outcomes to them.

### 2. Tag DCJ grants by youth justice
$5 billion flows through DCJ/FACS with no way to isolate youth justice spending. Add a youth justice tag to grant data so the public can see where money goes.

### 3. Fund Aboriginal community-controlled organisations directly
Zero ACCOs receive "Breaking the Cycle of Reoffending" grants. Zero appear in the top 20 of "Targeted Earlier Intervention" funding. Philanthropic funders send 71% of their money through Indigenous organisations. Government sends 3.2%.

### 4. Evaluate the bail laws
The 2024 bail amendments produced a 34% detention surge. 85-90% of refusals are First Nations children. The laws should be independently evaluated before the 2026 sunset extension.

### 5. Scale what works
Maranguka delivers a 5:1 return. Youth Koori Court delivers 40% less custody. Dubbo halved youth crime. Mounty Yarns built a 20-person team on $1M. These are not pilots — they are proven models waiting for government investment at scale.

### 6. Map Burrun Dalai
$59M in government funding, zero programs mapped. Either the programs exist and should be documented, or the funding is not reaching frontline delivery.

### 7. Raise the age of criminal responsibility
The ACT has done it. Scotland did it decades ago. BOCSAR's own data shows 82% of 10-13 year olds in court had child protection reports and 53% of their charges were withdrawn. Criminalising these children is expensive, harmful, and pointless.

### 8. Align government funding with philanthropic evidence
Philanthropic funders have built the evidence base: 16 Effective programs, 4 Proven, KPMG-validated returns. Government should co-invest alongside philanthropy, not duplicate intermediary contracts.
