**Prepared by:** JusticeHub Intelligence

**Data sources:** ROGS 2025, QAO Report 15, QFCC, QLD Contract Disclosure, CivicScope, AIHW, ministerial diaries

## Executive Summary

Queensland spends **$536 million per year** on youth justice (ROGS 2024-25). Spending has more than doubled in a decade — from ~$215M in 2014-15 — while reoffending has **increased from 64% to 69%** within 12 months (QAO Report 15, 2024). Post-detention reoffending reaches **84-96%** (QFCC).

The system costs **$2,714/day per young person** in detention vs **$493/day** in community supervision. Indigenous young people are **26.3x overrepresented** in detention.

Intermediary organisations receive **27x more funding** than Aboriginal Community-Controlled Organisations in top contracts ($963M vs $36M). The government's "$560M early intervention" package is **57% detention infrastructure** (Woodford $224M + Wacol $94M).

The ACT provides the counter-example: raising the age of criminal responsibility to 14 produced a **50% reduction in youth arrests** with no increase in community crime.

**Bottom line:** QLD is paying premium prices for the worst possible outcomes while the evidence for alternatives is overwhelming.

### 451 QLD Programs by Evidence Level

| Evidence Level | Count | % |
|---------------|-------|---|
| Proven (RCT/quasi-experimental, replicated) | **0** | 0% |
| Effective (strong evaluation, positive outcomes) | **6** | 1% |
| Promising (community-endorsed, emerging evidence) | **137** | 30% |
| Indigenous-led (culturally grounded, community authority) | **13** | 3% |
| Untested (theory/pilot stage) | **295** | 65% |

**Key finding:** Zero Proven programs in QLD. 65% of programs have never been evaluated. Only 6 have strong evaluation evidence. The state is spending $536M/year with almost no evidence base.

### QAO Report 15 Findings (2024)

The Queensland Audit Office's audit of youth justice found:
- **18% of sampled case files** had no documented evidence that rehabilitation was delivered
- The **co-responder program received $104.6M with no documented rationale** for the spending
- Reoffending within 12 months: **69%** (up from 64%)
- Recommendation: department should measure outcomes, not just outputs

## 6. QLD vs ACT Comparison

| Measure | QLD | ACT |
|---------|-----|-----|
| **Philosophy** | Punitive (breach & detain) | Rehabilitative (raise age, divert) |
| **Min age criminal responsibility** | 10 years | **14 years** (raised 2024) |
| **Youth arrest rates post-reform** | Increased | **50% reduction** |
| **Community crime increase** | N/A | **None** |
| **Detention cost/day** | $2,714 | ~$2,200 |
| **Community control emphasis** | Low (27x intermediary gap) | High (Ngunnawal-led) |
| **Indigenous overrepresentation** | 26.3x | ~16x (declining) |
| **Key legislation** | Breach of bail expansion | Raising age, therapeutic orders |

**Key finding:** QLD and ACT represent a natural experiment. ACT raised the age and invested in community control; youth arrests dropped 50% with no crime increase. QLD expanded detention and punitive measures; reoffending increased. The evidence is unambiguous.

## 9. Cross-System Pipeline

| Crossover | Rate | Source |
|-----------|------|--------|
| Child protection → youth justice | **68%** | QLD data linkage studies |
| Mental health comorbidity | **45%** | Clinical assessments |
| Indigenous in detention | **52%** | ROGS 2025 |
| Cognitive disability (estimated) | **~40%** | National studies |

### Lifetime Cost

For each young person who progresses through the full pipeline (child protection → youth justice → adult corrections), the estimated lifetime cost to government is **~$1.8M**.

Early intervention at the child protection stage (0-5 years) would cost a fraction of this. The most cost-effective intervention point is before first contact with the justice system — not after.

**Key finding:** The cross-system pipeline is predictable and preventable. 68% of young people in youth justice have child protection histories. Intervening earlier, through community-controlled family support services, would prevent the pipeline rather than managing its consequences at $2,714/day.

## 10. Data Gaps

These gaps are not accidental — they protect the status quo from accountability.

| Gap | Impact | Why It Matters |
|-----|--------|---------------|
| Program-level recidivism | Cannot measure which programs reduce reoffending | $536M spent with no outcome measurement by program |
| Cost-benefit by program | ~30% of programs have cost data | Full economic evaluation impossible |
| Disability crossover | No linked NDIS data | Up to 40% of detained youth have cognitive disabilities |
| Education outcomes | No linked data | Cannot track school engagement before/after programs |
| Child protection linkage | No linked DCSSDS data | Know 68% have CP histories but cannot track pathways |
| Cultural safety metrics | No standardised measure | Cannot compare cultural safety across programs |
| ACCO vs intermediary outcomes | No systematic comparison | Cannot prove ACCOs deliver better outcomes (which would threaten intermediary funding) |
| Intermediary overhead | No public data | Unknown what % of intermediary funding reaches frontline delivery |

## Recommendations

1. **Redirect detention spending to community control** — Even a 10% shift ($54M/year) would more than double ACCO funding
2. **Mandate program-level outcome measurement** — No program should receive public funding without documented outcome tracking
3. **Publish intermediary overhead rates** — Require all funded organisations to disclose what percentage reaches frontline service delivery
4. **Raise the age** — The ACT evidence is unambiguous; QLD should follow
5. **Link cross-system data** — Child protection + youth justice + education + health data must be linked to enable evidence-based early intervention
6. **Fund community-controlled evaluation** — ACCOs should measure outcomes on their own terms, using culturally appropriate metrics
7. **Independent audit of "early intervention" claims** — The $560M figure needs independent verification of what constitutes actual early intervention vs detention infrastructure
