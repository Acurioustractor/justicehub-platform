## Executive Summary

Queensland spends **$536 million per year** on youth justice (ROGS 2024-25). Spending has more than doubled in a decade — from ~$215M in 2014-15 — while reoffending has **increased from 64% to 69%** within 12 months (QAO Report 15, 2024). Post-detention reoffending reaches **84-96%** (QFCC, Exiting Youth Detention, June 2024).

On any given night, **300 young people** are in QLD detention — the highest count nationally. **90% are unsentenced** — held on remand, not convicted (AIHW 2023-24). Indigenous young people are **26.3x overrepresented** in detention (ROGS 2025). The system costs **$2,714/day** per detained young person.

The government's "$560M early intervention" package is **57% detention infrastructure** — Woodford ($224M) and Wacol ($94M) relabelled as prevention. Intermediary organisations receive **27x more** than Aboriginal Community-Controlled Organisations in top QLD contracts ($963M vs $36M).

Meanwhile, **72.9%** of children in youth justice had prior child protection contact (QFCC Crossover Cohort Data Insights, 2024). For First Nations children, the figure is **81.2%**. The pipeline is predictable, preventable, and expensive.

Two UN Special Rapporteurs, the Australian Human Rights Commission, Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, the Law Council, and multiple peak Aboriginal legal services have all called for fundamental reform. The Senate inquiry into youth justice reports in June 2026.

Communities like **Palm Island**, **Mount Isa**, and **Minjerribah** are already running the alternatives. Their stories are in this report.

### Trends

- **Detention numbers up 50%** over five years (AIHW 2023-24)
- **Community supervision down 18%** over five years (AIHW 2023-24)

The shift is structural: QLD is moving young people *from* community supervision *into* detention, not reducing offending. The 90% unsentenced figure means this is overwhelmingly a **remand crisis** — children held before trial, not after conviction.

### What the QLD Auditor-General Found

QAO Report 15 (2024) — *Delivering Youth Justice Services*:

- **18% of sampled case files** had no documented evidence that rehabilitation was delivered
- The **co-responder program received $104.6M with no documented rationale** for the spending level
- Reoffending within 12 months: **69%** (up from 64%)
- The department measures *outputs* (service hours, contacts) not *outcomes* (reoffending, wellbeing)
- Recommendation: "The department should develop and implement a framework for measuring outcomes"

*Source: QAO Report 15: Delivering Youth Justice Services, 2024*

### Recidivism

| Metric | Rate | Source |
|--------|------|--------|
| General reoffending (12 months) | **69%** | QAO Report 15, 2024 |
| Previous general reoffending | 64% | QAO Report 15, ~2020 baseline |
| Post-detention reoffending (12 months) | **84-96%** | QFCC Exiting Youth Detention, June 2024 |
| Change over time | **+5pp increase** | QAO 2020-2024 |

The QFCC study was based on interviews with **66 young people and 44 workers** inside the system. It concluded that detention-based rehabilitation is fundamentally ineffective. Young people reported that programs inside detention were experienced as compliance requirements, not genuine support.

More spending has produced worse outcomes. The 5 percentage point increase in reoffending (64% to 69%) occurred during a period when spending rose from ~$340M to $536M.

*Sources: QAO Report 15 (2024); QFCC Exiting Youth Detention Report, June 2024*

## 6. What the Sector Is Saying

### United Nations

In May 2025, **two UN Special Rapporteurs** — on the rights of Indigenous peoples and on torture — issued a joint statement describing youth justice systems across Australia as "in crisis" and QLD laws as **"incompatible with basic child rights."**

They specifically cited:
- Detention of children as young as 10
- Breach of bail laws that criminalise non-compliance
- Conditions of confinement (solitary, watch houses)

*Source: OHCHR Media Advisory, May 2025*

### Human Rights Watch

In May 2025, Human Rights Watch documented QLD-specific conditions:
- Children held in **concrete watch house cells with no natural light** (Cairns)
- **No outdoor exercise** provided at Murgon
- Use of solitary confinement for behavioural management
- Extended watch house stays exceeding legal time limits

*Source: HRW, "Children Suffering Under Criminal Legal System," May 2025*

### Australian Human Rights Commission

The AHRC's **"Help Way Earlier!"** report called for transforming child justice toward safety and wellbeing, away from punitive models. It identified QLD's approach as particularly concerning given the scale of Indigenous overrepresentation.

*Source: AHRC, Help Way Earlier! Transforming Child Justice for Safety and Wellbeing, 2024-25*

### Senate Inquiry

The **Senate Standing Committee on Legal and Constitutional Affairs** is conducting its second inquiry into Australia's youth justice and incarceration system. It reports **30 June 2026**.

Major submissions from:
- **ATSILS** — detailed analysis of QLD-specific failures in diversion and bail
- **Law Council of Australia** — calling for national minimum standards
- **VACCA** — First Nations self-determination in youth justice design
- **NAPCAN** — early intervention evidence review

Media coverage of the inquiry hearings (March 2026) described the system as "in crisis" with witnesses calling for national reform.

*Source: Senate Inquiry Page, APH; NIT, 16 March 2026*

### Amnesty International

Amnesty has specifically targeted QLD's "Adult Crime, Adult Time" legislation as a **violation of the Convention on the Rights of the Child**, noting that no other developed nation applies adult sentencing regimes to children under 14.

*Source: Amnesty International Australia, 2025*

### Academic Research

**Dickson (2025)** published a peer-reviewed analysis in the *Alternative Law Journal* describing the Making Queensland Safer Act as **"punitive populism"** enacted against overwhelming criminological evidence. The paper documents how QLD policy departed from evidence precisely as the evidence base strengthened.

*Source: Dickson, "Punitive Populism and Youth Justice in Queensland," Alternative Law Journal, 2025*

### QFCC — Electronic Monitoring

The Principal Commissioner submitted that electronic monitoring of children **"does not reduce reoffending and creates false community expectations of safety"** while subjecting children to surveillance that impedes rehabilitation.

*Source: QFCC Submission on Youth Justice (Electronic Monitoring) Amendment Bill, January 2026*

**Palm Island Community Company** runs **21 programs** covering justice, health, education, culture, and employment — all governed by Aboriginal community members. It is the closest thing to a complete community-controlled service system in Queensland.

**Uncle Alan**, a Traditional Owner and Director for the Manbarra people, is a master storyteller of the Gabul (snake) dreaming story. His skin name is Nakawaraka, his totem Kurenja (stingray). He trained at Cairns TAFE and RMIT, and now leads the Mingna Mingna Rangers on Palm Island.

Uncle Alan's story is recorded in a full interview transcript in the Empathy Ledger.

**Iris**, born on Palm Island:

> *"My name is Iris. My parents were born here on Palm Island."*
> — Iris, in "The Little Boat Called Ivy May"

**Henry Doyle**, a local Palm Island man:

> *"I'm a local on Palm Island. I'm Aboriginal."*

The **Men's Group** on Palm Island — whose story is titled "Finding a Reason to Get Out of Bed" — represents exactly the kind of program that falls outside standard service categories. ### Minjerribah (Stradbroke Island) — MMEIC

**Minjerribah Moorgumpin Elders-in-Council** operates on Quandamooka Country — North Stradbroke Island, South East Queensland. MMEIC has been running Indigenous-led justice and healing for decades, predating the current wave of government interest in "justice reinvestment."

The organisation is led by Elders including **Aunty Evie**, **Uncle Dale**, and **Aunty Maureen**, supported by a new generation including Tegan Burns, Chelsea Rolfe, and Benjamin Moss.

MMEIC runs the **Quandamooka Justice and Healing Strategy** — a community-designed approach to justice that centres Elders, culture, and Country rather than courts and compliance.

**Shaun Fisher**, a First Nations community activist and oyster farmer connected to MMEIC and Stradbroke Island, has a full recorded interview in the Empathy Ledger.

MMEIC's stories — "After the Flood," "The Morning Tide," "Grading Day" — are quieter than the crisis narratives that dominate youth justice coverage. They describe community life, not system failure. That is precisely the point: justice reinvestment succeeds when community is strong enough that the justice system becomes unnecessary.

*Source: Empathy Ledger v2, MMEIC organisation (9 members, 3 stories, 2 transcripts, 2 projects). JusticeHub trip stop: "MMEIC proves Indigenous-led justice reinvestment works at scale over decades."*

---

### What the stories show

These three communities — Mount Isa, Palm Island, Minjerribah — share three things:

1. **Cultural authority that cannot be outsourced.** Uncle George, Uncle Alan, Aunty Evie are not service providers. They are knowledge holders. Their presence is what transforms a program from a service into a relationship.

2. **Programs that don't fit government categories.** A men's group. A bush camp. An oyster farm. A fitness session. These are not "interventions" in the departmental sense. They are community life. They work because they are real.

3. **Persistence without funding.** BG-FIT runs on almost nothing. MMEIC has operated for decades. PICC delivers 21 programs. None of them receive funding proportional to what intermediaries receive for comparable work. They exist because communities built them, not because governments funded them.

The $536M question is not whether these approaches work — the people in these stories are the evidence that they do. The question is why the system sends $963M to intermediaries and $36M to the communities doing the work.

## 9. The Cross-System Pipeline

### How children enter the system

| Crossover pathway | Rate | Source |
|-------------------|------|--------|
| Child protection contact before youth justice | **72.9%** | QFCC Crossover Cohort Data Insights, 2024 |
| First Nations children with CP contact | **81.2%** | QFCC Crossover Cohort Data Insights, 2024 |
| Mental health comorbidity | ~45% | Clinical assessment literature |
| Cognitive disability (estimated) | ~40% | National studies (no QLD-specific data) |

The QFCC crossover study is definitive: **nearly three-quarters** of children who end up in youth justice were already known to child protection. For First Nations children, it is **four in five**.

This means the pipeline is **identifiable years in advance**. The children who will enter youth justice at 14 are, in most cases, already known to the state at 4.

### What early intervention costs

| Intervention point | Cost | Outcome |
|-------------------|------|---------|
| Community family support (0-5 years) | ~$3-8K/year | Prevention |
| Community program (youth) | ~$5K/year (median) | Diversion |
| Intensive community program | ~$15-25K/year | High-risk diversion |
| Community supervision | ~$180K/year | Monitoring |
| Detention | **~$991K/year** | 84-96% reoffending |

Every dollar spent at the top of this table prevents multiples at the bottom. The system currently concentrates spending at the bottom.

*Sources: QFCC Crossover Cohort Data Insights, Nov 2024; ROGS 2025; JusticeHub program cost analysis*

### QLD in national context

| Measure | QLD | National | Source |

| Detention rate per 10,000 | **5.1** | 3.0 | AIHW 2023-24 |
| % unsentenced | **90%** | 72% | AIHW 2023-24 |

| Reoffending (12mo) | **69%** | ~55% | QAO; ROGS |
| Min age criminal responsibility | 10 | 10-14 (varies) | Legislation |

QLD has the highest raw detention numbers, the highest unsentenced proportion, the highest Indigenous overrepresentation in detention, and among the highest reoffending rates nationally.

### ACT Reform

The ACT raised the minimum age of criminal responsibility to **14 years** in **July 2025**. It is the first Australian jurisdiction to do so.

The reform is too recent for outcome data — a statutory review is scheduled for 2028. However, the ACT's approach is fundamentally different from QLD: therapeutic orders replace criminal prosecution for children under 14, and community-controlled services (Ngunnawal-led) are prioritised over institutional responses.

International evidence from Scotland's "Kilbrandon" model — which raised the age and shifted to welfare-based responses decades ago — shows that raising the age does not increase youth crime. It reduces system contact and improves long-term outcomes.

QLD has moved in the opposite direction: lowering the effective threshold through breach-of-bail laws, expanding electronic monitoring, and building new detention infrastructure.

*Sources: ACT Government, Raising the Age (July 2025); ROGS 2025; Scottish Government evaluation data*

## 11. What Works Globally

QLD's approach contradicts 40 years of international evidence. Here is what other jurisdictions have done instead.

### Scotland — GIRFEC / Children's Hearings
**Outcome:** 92% reduction in youth prosecutions, 97% reduction in youth custody sentences (2008-2022). The Children (Care and Justice) Act 2024 ended youth imprisonment entirely from August 2024. Welfare-based responses for all under-18s.

*Source: Scottish Government, Justice for Children 2024-26*

### New Zealand — Rangatahi Courts & Iwi Panels
**Outcome:** Rangatahi Court participants are 15% less likely to reoffend. 64% reduction in young Māori offending over 10 years. Over 80% of iwi panel participants complete their plans. Family Group Conferencing has been the primary diversion mechanism since 1989. However, Māori still comprise 66% of Youth Court appearances despite being 16% of population.

*Sources: Youth Court of NZ; Walton (2020), Iwi panels and reoffending*

### Canada — Youth Criminal Justice Act
**Outcome:** Youth incarceration dropped 50% within 6 years of the 2003 Act. Over 90% long-term reduction. No increase in youth crime. Replaced prison-based responses with community alternatives and extrajudicial measures.

*Source: Justice Canada, YCJA Evaluation Findings, 2021*

### Spain — Diagrama Foundation
**Outcome:** 13.6% recidivism over 6 years (vs 30-50% in traditional detention). 70%+ transition to education or employment within 6 months. €3.80 return per €1 invested. Therapeutic community model with individualised rehabilitation plans.

*Source: Diagrama Foundation evaluation data*

### USA (Missouri) — Missouri Model
**Outcome:** 24% reincarceration within 3 years (vs 43% Texas, 52% Arizona). Fewer than 8% go on to adult prison. 85% of exiting youth positively engaged in community. Small group homes near community replace large youth prisons. No extra cost to taxpayers.

*Source: Annie E. Casey Foundation, The Missouri Model*

### Australia (NSW) — Maranguka Justice Reinvestment
**Outcome:** 42% reduction in custody days. 23% drop in domestic violence. 31% rise in Year 12 retention. $3.1M gross impact on $0.6M operational cost — KPMG-validated 5:1 return. Aboriginal-led in Bourke, NSW. The closest Australian proof of concept for community-controlled justice reinvestment.

*Sources: JustReinvest NSW; KPMG Preliminary Assessment*

### The meta-analytic consensus

Across **48 meta-analyses** covering 40 years of research: rehabilitation works (OR 1.73 for CBT-based programs), while deterrence-based approaches are **slightly harmful** (OR 0.85). This is settled science. QLD's current approach — more detention, harsher bail, breach-based enforcement — contradicts the entire evidence base.

*Source: PMC systematic review, "The 40-year debate"*

## 12. Closing the Gap

**Target 11:** Reduce the rate of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander young people in detention by at least 30% by 2031.

QLD's First Nations youth detention rate is **46 per 10,000** — more than double the national target of 22.3. The target is assessed nationally as "no change from baseline," but QLD is actively going backwards. First Nations detention rate has risen from 33 to 40 per 10,000 over four years (AIHW 2025).

**QATSICPP** (Queensland Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Child Protection Peak) was appointed as the QLD Youth Justice Peak Body in May 2024, representing 35 community-controlled organisations. Their Centre of Excellence evidence review found 56% of QLD youth justice children had prior child protection contact — consistent with the QFCC's 72.9% figure using different methodology.

*Sources: Closing the Gap Dashboard; AIHW 2025; QATSICPP Centre of Excellence*

| ACCO vs intermediary outcomes | Cannot compare effectiveness by organisation type | Standardised outcome framework applied equally |
| Disability crossover | ~40% estimate has no QLD-specific data | NDIS-youth justice data linkage |
| Education outcomes | No linked data | Education-justice data linkage |
| Cultural safety metrics | No standardised measure exists | Community-defined measurement framework |
| Kickstarter program outcomes | Zero published evaluations | Independent evaluation of all 41 funded programs |
| Cost-benefit by program | Verified cost data for 484 programs, but no benefit data | Outcome data linked to cost data |

JusticeHub has built the cost and evidence mapping for 484 QLD programs. The missing half is outcome data — which only the department can provide or mandate.

## 14. Recommendations

These recommendations follow directly from the evidence presented in this report.

### 1. Publish program-level outcome data
The QAO recommended this in 2024. No program should receive public funding without documented, published outcome tracking. This is the single highest-impact reform available — it costs nothing and would immediately reveal which of the 484 programs work.

### 2. Mandate intermediary overhead disclosure
Require all funded organisations to publish what percentage of their funding reaches frontline service delivery. The 27:1 intermediary-to-ACCO ratio is indefensible without transparency on how intermediary funding is spent.

### 3. Redirect detention infrastructure spending to community control
Even a 10% shift of the $536M budget ($54M/year) would more than double ACCO contract value from $36M to $90M. The evidence shows community programs at $5K/year outperform detention at $991K/year.

### 4. Address the remand crisis
90% of detained children are unsentenced. This is a bail system problem, not a crime problem. Reform bail laws to reduce remand, not expand breach provisions.

### 5. Invest in the cross-system pipeline
72.9% of children in youth justice were known to child protection. Fund community-controlled family support services (at $3-8K/year) to prevent the $991K/year detention pathway.

### 6. Commission independent Kickstarter evaluation
41 programs funded, zero independently evaluated. Before the next funding round, publish independent evaluations of Round 1 outcomes.

### 7. Raise the age of criminal responsibility
The ACT has done it. Scotland did it decades ago. International evidence and every major human rights body supports it. QLD should follow.

### 8. Respond to the data gap audit
This report identifies 8 specific data gaps that prevent evidence-based policy. The department should publish a response to each gap with a timeline for closure.
