## Key Findings Relevant to the Inquiry

### 1. Government funding bypasses Aboriginal community-controlled organisations

In NSW, $5 billion flows through DCJ/FACS grants to 2,675 organisations. Only **3.2% are Indigenous**. The direct "Breaking the Cycle of Reoffending" grant category ($9.9M) funds **zero Aboriginal community-controlled organisations**.

In QLD, intermediary organisations receive **27 times more** than ACCOs in top contracts ($963M vs $36M).

### 2. Philanthropic funders demonstrate an alternative model

Philanthropic funders (Paul Ramsay Foundation, Dusseldorp Forum, NIAA) invest $115 million in NSW — **70.9% through Indigenous-led organisations**. They fund Australia's only KPMG-validated justice reinvestment model and 16 programs rated Effective.

### 3. Proven alternatives exist and are documented

NSW has 4 programs with Proven evidence (RCT/quasi-experimental) and 16 rated Effective. The cheapest Effective program costs $1,708 per young person per year. Detention costs $939,000. The ratio is 550:1.

Maranguka (Bourke) delivers a 5:1 economic return. Youth crime in Dubbo halved in one year. Youth Koori Court produces 40% less custody with no increase in reoffending.

### 4. The bail crisis is measurable

Since the NSW Bail and Crimes Amendment Act 2024, youth detention has surged 34%. BOCSAR data shows 85-90% of those refused bail under the new laws are First Nations children. 79.8% of Aboriginal youth in detention are on remand.

### 5. The cross-system pipeline is predictable

BOCSAR found 82% of children aged 10-13 in court had prior child protection reports. 53% of their charges were withdrawn. The system processes them, disrupts their lives, and releases them without conviction.
