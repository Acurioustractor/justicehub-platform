## Key Findings Relevant to the Inquiry

### 1. The cost disparity between detention and community programs

The national average cost of youth detention is **$1.33 million per young person per year** (ROGS 2024-25). The median cost of a community-based program in the JusticeHub database is **$77,000 per young person per year**, based on 305 programs with verified cost data. This represents a ratio of approximately 17:1.

The Queensland Family and Child Commission's June 2024 report found that **84 to 96 per cent** of young people reoffend within 12 months of release from detention, indicating that this expenditure does not achieve its stated rehabilitative purpose.

### 2. Government funding bypasses Aboriginal community-controlled organisations

In NSW, $5 billion flows through DCJ/FACS grants to 2,675 organisations. Only **3.2 per cent are Indigenous-controlled**. The "Breaking the Cycle of Reoffending" grant category ($9.9 million) funds zero Aboriginal community-controlled organisations.

In QLD, intermediary organisations receive **27 times more** than ACCOs in the largest contracts ($963 million vs $36 million).

By contrast, philanthropic funders (Paul Ramsay Foundation, Dusseldorp Forum, NIAA) invest $115 million in NSW with **70.9 per cent directed through Indigenous-led organisations**. They fund Australia's only KPMG-validated justice reinvestment model (Maranguka, Bourke) and 16 programs rated Effective.

### 3. Proven alternatives exist and are documented

The JusticeHub database contains 4 programs with Proven evidence (RCT or quasi-experimental replication) and 39 rated Effective (strong evaluation with positive outcomes). The cheapest Effective program costs $1,708 per young person per year.

Maranguka (Bourke, NSW) delivers a 5:1 economic return on investment. The Youth Koori Court model produces 40 per cent less custodial sentencing with no increase in reoffending.

### 4. The bail crisis is measurable

Since the NSW Bail and Crimes Amendment Act 2024, youth detention numbers have surged 34 per cent. BOCSAR data indicates 85 to 90 per cent of those refused bail under the amended legislation are First Nations children. Currently, 79.8 per cent of Aboriginal young people in detention are on remand, not sentenced.

### 5. The cross-system pipeline is predictable

BOCSAR found 82 per cent of children aged 10 to 13 appearing in court had prior child protection reports. Fifty-three per cent of their charges were subsequently withdrawn. The data indicates these children are being processed through the justice system, with consequent disruption to their lives, without resulting in conviction.

### 6. Funding transparency is inadequate

Of the 148,386 funding records tracked across 35 sources, **54.9 per cent cannot be traced to any identifiable recipient organisation**. This is partly attributable to state-level aggregate reporting (such as ROGS data) and partly to inconsistent disclosure practices across jurisdictions. Effective oversight of youth justice spending requires that funding can be traced from appropriation to service delivery.

## Civic Intelligence: Government Accountability Gaps

JusticeHub's civic intelligence layer, CivicScope, cross-references ministerial diaries, parliamentary Hansard transcripts, and oversight body recommendations to identify structural accountability gaps in the youth justice system.

### The Access Gap

Analysis of ministerial diary disclosures reveals a significant gap between the organisations that deliver youth justice programs and the organisations that have access to decision-makers. Of the organisations delivering programs in the JusticeHub database, and the organisations recorded in ministerial diaries, the overlap is minimal. Community-based service providers, particularly Aboriginal community-controlled organisations, are largely absent from the ministerial consultation record.

### Parliamentary debate patterns

Analysis of Hansard transcripts containing youth justice references shows that parliamentary debate is disproportionately focused on detention and enforcement measures relative to discussion of alternative and diversionary programs. The policy discourse recorded in Hansard does not reflect the evidence base documented in the JusticeHub database.

### Oversight recommendation implementation

The Queensland Audit Office's Report 15 (2023-24), "Reducing Serious Youth Crime," found that $1.38 billion was spent on youth justice over five years (2018-19 to 2022-23) while reoffending rates rose from 64 to 69 per cent. Of the Auditor-General's 12 recommendations to the Department of Youth Justice, two remained only partially implemented as of the 2025 follow-up. The report concluded that the department could not demonstrate that its investment was producing results due to poor data capture and a lack of independent evaluation.

### Consultancy expenditure vs community investment

Analysis of government procurement and contract data reveals a disparity between expenditure on consulting firms for youth justice strategy and policy advice, and direct investment in community-based program delivery. This pattern is consistent with a system that commissions analysis of the problem rather than funding organisations with demonstrated capacity to address it.
