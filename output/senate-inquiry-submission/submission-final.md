1. JusticeHub Intelligence welcomes the opportunity to make this submission to the Senate Standing Committee on Legal and Constitutional Affairs inquiry into Australia's youth justice and incarceration system. This submission is authorised for publication.

2. JusticeHub is an open-data intelligence platform that maps youth justice spending, programs, evidence, and community voices across Australia. The platform aggregates and cross-references data from 35 government and philanthropic sources to provide the most comprehensive publicly accessible dataset on the Australian youth justice system. A methodology note and data source summary are provided at **Appendix A**.

## About JusticeHub

3. The JusticeHub platform holds the following data as of March 2026:

   - **1,081 verified community-based interventions** mapped with cost data, evidence ratings, and operating organisation data
   - **148,386 funding records** totalling $114.9 billion across federal, state, and philanthropic sources
   - **82,966 organisations** classified by control type (community-controlled, intermediary, government)

4. All figures cited in this submission are drawn from the JusticeHub database unless otherwise attributed. Dollar figures for detention costs are sourced from the Productivity Commission's Report on Government Services (ROGS) 2024–25.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: The cost disparity between detention and community programs

5. The national average cost of youth detention is **$1.33 million per young person per year** (Productivity Commission, ROGS 2024–25). The median cost of a community-based program in the JusticeHub database is **$77,000 per young person per year**, based on 305 programs with verified cost data. This represents a ratio of approximately 17:1.

6. The Queensland Family and Child Commission's June 2024 report found that **84 to 96 per cent** of young people reoffend within 12 months of release from detention (QFCC, *Changing the Sentence*, June 2024). This indicates that the current expenditure on detention does not achieve its stated rehabilitative purpose.

### Finding 2: Government funding bypasses Aboriginal community-controlled organisations

7. In New South Wales, $5 billion flows through DCJ/FACS grants to 2,675 organisations. Only **3.2 per cent are Indigenous-controlled** (JusticeHub analysis of NSW Government open data). The "Breaking the Cycle of Reoffending" grant category ($9.9 million) funds zero Aboriginal community-controlled organisations.

8. In Queensland, intermediary organisations receive **27 times more** than ACCOs in the largest contracts ($963 million compared with $36 million) (JusticeHub analysis of QLD Government contract disclosures).

9. By contrast, philanthropic funders — including the Paul Ramsay Foundation, Dusseldorp Forum, and NIAA — invest $115 million in NSW with **70.9 per cent directed through Indigenous-led organisations**. These funders support Australia's only KPMG-validated justice reinvestment model (Maranguka, Bourke) and 16 programs rated Effective by independent evaluation.

### Finding 3: Proven alternatives exist and are documented

10. The JusticeHub database contains 4 programs with Proven evidence (RCT or quasi-experimental replication) and 39 rated Effective (strong evaluation with positive outcomes). The cheapest Effective program costs **$1,708 per young person per year**. See **Appendix A**, Table 1 for the evidence distribution.

11. Maranguka (Bourke, NSW) delivers a **5:1 economic return on investment** (KPMG, *Maranguka Justice Reinvestment Project: Impact Assessment*, 2018). The Youth Koori Court model produces **40 per cent less custodial sentencing** with no increase in reoffending.

### Finding 4: The bail crisis is measurable

12. Since the NSW Bail and Crimes Amendment Act 2024, youth detention numbers have surged **34 per cent** (BOCSAR). BOCSAR data indicates **85 to 90 per cent** of those refused bail under the amended legislation are First Nations children.

13. Currently, **79.8 per cent** of Aboriginal young people in detention are on remand, not sentenced (BOCSAR, March 2026).

### Finding 5: The cross-system pipeline is predictable

14. BOCSAR found **82 per cent** of children aged 10 to 13 appearing in court had prior child protection reports. Fifty-three per cent of their charges were subsequently withdrawn (BOCSAR, *Children in the NSW Criminal Justice System*, 2024).

15. These data indicate that children with child protection histories are being processed through the justice system — with consequent disruption to their lives — without resulting in conviction. This pattern is consistent with a care-to-custody pipeline that diverts welfare-system responsibilities onto the criminal justice system.

### Finding 6: Funding transparency is inadequate

16. Of the 148,386 funding records tracked across 35 sources, **54.9 per cent cannot be traced to any identifiable recipient organisation**. This is partly attributable to state-level aggregate reporting (such as ROGS data) and partly to inconsistent disclosure practices across jurisdictions.

17. Effective oversight of youth justice spending requires that funding can be traced from appropriation to service delivery. The Committee may wish to consider recommending standardised funding disclosure requirements across jurisdictions.

## Civic Intelligence: Government Accountability Gaps

18. JusticeHub's civic intelligence layer, CivicScope, cross-references ministerial diaries, parliamentary Hansard transcripts, and oversight body recommendations to identify structural accountability gaps in the youth justice system.

### The access gap

19. Analysis of ministerial diary disclosures reveals a significant gap between the organisations that deliver youth justice programs and the organisations that have access to decision-makers. Community-based service providers, particularly Aboriginal community-controlled organisations, are largely absent from the ministerial consultation record.

### Parliamentary debate patterns

20. Analysis of Hansard transcripts containing youth justice references shows that parliamentary debate is disproportionately focused on detention and enforcement measures relative to discussion of alternative and diversionary programs. The policy discourse recorded in Hansard does not reflect the evidence base documented in the JusticeHub database.

### Oversight recommendation implementation

21. The Queensland Audit Office's Report 15 (2023–24), *Reducing Serious Youth Crime*, found that $1.38 billion was spent on youth justice over five years (2018–19 to 2022–23) while reoffending rates rose from 64 to 69 per cent (QAO, Report 15, 2023–24).

22. Of the Auditor-General's 12 recommendations to the Department of Youth Justice, two remained only partially implemented as of the 2025 follow-up. The report concluded that the department could not demonstrate that its investment was producing results due to poor data capture and a lack of independent evaluation.

### Consultancy expenditure versus community investment

23. Analysis of government procurement and contract data reveals a disparity between expenditure on consulting firms for youth justice strategy and policy advice, and direct investment in community-based program delivery. This pattern is consistent with a system that commissions analysis of the problem rather than funding organisations with demonstrated capacity to address it.
