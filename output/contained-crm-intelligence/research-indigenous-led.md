# CONTAINED — Indigenous-Led & First Nations Campaigns: Cultural Grounding

> Research brief: how the strongest First Nations campaigns won, and the concrete plays CONTAINED runs so it leads WITH community, not about it.
> READ-ONLY planning artefact. Nothing here was executed against GHL / Supabase / Vercel / email. Every external action is documented for Ben to decide (Tier 3).
> Compiled 2026-06-13. Voice: Imagination Architect. Figures: §7 canon only.

---

## 0. Why this brief exists

CONTAINED is Indigenous-justice-centred. The container holds a question that First Nations communities have already answered: the cure exists, it is community-led, and it works. So the campaign cannot borrow the language of First Nations leadership while keeping the wheel in settler hands. The wall in Room 3 is the test. Whose names go on it, who decides, who gets paid, who is filmed, and who holds the story afterward.

The 78 live contacts make this real, not theoretical. The SA service-org wall (Personal outreach, 34 contacts) is the consent gate for Room 3. The young people who build and host the container are co-authors, not props. The funders (Identified, 23) are backing a model whose authority sits with community, not with us. Every play below ties to that base, to the nominate mechanic, and to the Adelaide-then-national-tour shape.

Six anchor cases ground this brief: the Uluru Statement / Voice dialogue process, Maranguka in Bourke, Raise the Age, Michael Long's The Long Walk, Bringing Them Home / Sorry Day, and the OCAP and "nothing about us without us" storytelling-ethics tradition.

## 1. The Uluru Statement from the Heart — authority comes from the process, not the product

**The pattern: legitimacy is manufactured upstream, in who decides.** The Statement did not start as a document. It started as thirteen First Nations Regional Dialogues, each gathering roughly 100 people drawn from traditional owners, community organisations and leaders, feeding into a convention of around 250 delegates. More than 1,200 Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people shaped it over six months. The design was deliberately built to avoid group-think and to give the final words trust in the eyes of the people they spoke for. The Statement was then issued as an invitation to the Australian people, not a submission to government. ([ulurustatement.org](https://ulurustatement.org/history/the-journey-so-far/), [law.unimelb.edu.au case study](https://law.unimelb.edu.au/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/3230377/MF19-Australia-paper.pdf))

**Why it worked.** The authority of the final words was earned before anyone read them. Because the process was First Nations designed and led, no one could say the document spoke over community. The "invitation, not a petition" frame turned a policy ask into a relationship offer, which is why it moved people who had never engaged with constitutional reform.

**The play CONTAINED runs — Room 3 is a dialogue, not a logo wall.**
- Treat the SA wall (the 34 Personal-outreach contacts) the way the Referendum Council treated a regional dialogue. Before any org goes on the wall, hold a small SA-org gathering (in person or one call each) where they shape what Room 3 says about their work, in their words. The wall is co-authored, not curated by us.
- Frame every wall invitation as an invitation, not a feature request. The ask is "help us hold this room," not "can we use your logo." This is the difference between the 34 feeling used and the 34 feeling sovereign over their own panel.
- Carry the "authority upstream" rule into the nominate mechanic too: the people nominating decision-makers should include the community orgs whose young people are most affected, so the pressure comes from the affected community, not from a settler-run list.

**Failure mode to avoid.** The Voice referendum lost at the ballot in part because a process that was beautifully legitimate inside community was, nationally, out-communicated and politicised. The lesson for CONTAINED is not "avoid bold asks." It is: the experience must carry its own legitimacy in the room, so it never depends on winning a contested public argument to land. Thirty minutes alone in the container does what a national debate could not — it converts before the politics start.

## 2. Maranguka, Bourke — community owns the model, the data, and the savings story

**The pattern: an Aboriginal-led backbone with real governance and real data.** Maranguka ("caring for others" in Ngemba language) is the first Aboriginal-led place-based justice reinvestment model in Australia. It is governed by the Bourke Tribal Council, the overarching body representing the clan and family groups of the Bourke Aboriginal community, working through an Aboriginal community-owned multi-disciplinary backbone team. Its strategy, "Growing our kids up safe, smart and strong," is community-authored. KPMG's assessment put 2017 savings at $3.1 million, two-thirds in justice savings. The community runs its own data through the Palimaa platform, guided by Indigenous Data Sovereignty. ([justreinvest.org.au](https://www.justreinvest.org.au/community/bourke-maranguka/), [maranguka.org.au](https://maranguka.org.au/), [seerdata.ai case study](https://seerdata.ai/maranguka-case-study/), [Indigenous Justice Clearinghouse impact assessment](https://www.indigenousjustice.gov.au/resources/maranguka-justice-reinvestment-project-impact-assessment/))

**Why it worked.** Governance came first, then services, then evidence. Because the Tribal Council held the wheel, the police, the schools and the agencies were partners inside a community plan, not the other way around. And because the community held its own data, the savings story belonged to Bourke, so it could not be extracted, spun, or claimed by an outside body.

**The play CONTAINED runs — Room 3 as the Maranguka of the wall, and JusticeHub as Palimaa.**
- Room 3 already changes each stop to feature local community-led orgs. Make that promise explicit and structural: each city's wall is governed by the local community orgs the way Bourke's plan is governed by the Tribal Council. CONTAINED supplies the container and the craft; the community supplies the authority and the names.
- Tie the evidence layer to the Maranguka logic. JusticeHub is the public "what works instead" layer, and Empathy Ledger is the consent, story and community-authority layer. Position these to community partners as their Palimaa: a place where the proof of their model lives under their control, not a place where we hold their data.
- For the 23 Identified funders, the Maranguka frame is the strongest single argument: this is not charity, it is reinvestment in models that already return savings to community. The ask at `/contained/invest` lands harder when it points at a working Australian precedent, not only the Spanish one.

**Failure mode to avoid.** Do not present community-led models as something CONTAINED created or coordinates. Bourke's authority is Bourke's. CONTAINED points at the work; it does not annex it. The moment the campaign implies it runs or ranks the orgs on the wall, it becomes the extractive thing it is critiquing.

## 3. Raise the Age — a coalition with First Nations leadership and a single clear demand

**The pattern: many orgs, one demand, Aboriginal-led evidence at the centre.** Raise the Age is a coalition campaign asking all governments to lift the minimum age of criminal responsibility to at least 14 and fund community alternatives. Its supporting report drew on submissions from more than 90 Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander health, legal and human rights organisations. First Nations children are massively over-represented, which is why First Nations organisations lead the framing. Tasmania and the ACT have moved to 14. ([raisetheage.org.au](https://raisetheage.org.au/), [antar.org.au](https://antar.org.au/campaign/raise-the-age/), [VALS policy brief](https://www.vals.org.au/wp-content/uploads/2022/08/Raising-the-Age-of-Criminal-Responsibility-Policy-Brief-August-2022.pdf))

**Why it worked.** The single demand let dozens of organisations stand together without diluting anyone. The First Nations leadership at the centre of the evidence gave the whole coalition moral and factual weight. The over-representation figure made the injustice impossible to abstract away.

**The play CONTAINED runs — one demand under every nominate.**
- CONTAINED already has its over-representation anchor: roughly 23x Indigenous over-representation (national youth detention statistics, §7 canon). Pair it with the SA detention figure of 3,261 dollars per day to detain one child (Productivity Commission ROGS 2024-25) and the 84% returning to sentenced supervision within 12 months (AIHW 2023-24). Three numbers, one demand: back what works.
- The nominate mechanic is CONTAINED's coalition tool. Raise the Age stacked 90+ organisations behind one ask; CONTAINED stacks witnesses behind one nominated decision-maker. Keep the demand identical across every nomination so the pressure aggregates instead of fragmenting: "walk through this before your next decision about a young person."
- Use the 7 Partner/committed contacts as the visible coalition spine, the way Raise the Age used its named org list. A short, real, named list of who already stands with it is worth more than a long abstract one.

**Failure mode to avoid.** Raise the Age has stalled in most jurisdictions for years. A clear demand is necessary but not sufficient; CONTAINED's edge is that the container converts emotionally in 30 minutes where a policy ask alone fatigues. Do not let CONTAINED become another worthy ask that decision-makers nod at and shelve. The walkthrough is the conversion event; the nominate is the follow-through. Keep both.

## 4. The Long Walk — a moving physical act that lets people join, step by step

**The pattern: one person's embodied act becomes a vehicle anyone can join.** In 2004 Michael Long walked roughly 650 km from Melbourne toward Canberra to put Indigenous lives back on the national agenda. People joined for an hour, a day, or the whole way. It became The Long Walk, an annual reconciliation vehicle that gave non-Indigenous Australians a concrete way to express commitment by literally walking alongside. ([reconciliation.org.au](https://www.reconciliation.org.au/michal-long-is-walking-for-a-voice/), [City of Melbourne](https://www.melbourne.vic.gov.au/news/long-walk-walking-talk-reconciliation))

**Why it worked.** The act was physical, repeatable and joinable at any depth. You did not have to understand the policy to walk a kilometre. The low-threshold entry (walk a bit) opened onto a high-commitment identity (I walk every year). It gave reconciliation a body.

**The play CONTAINED runs — the container is the walk, the tour is the long walk.**
- CONTAINED already has the embodied act: 30 minutes inside, alone, phone and shoes at the door. That is its Long Walk. Lean into the joinable-at-any-depth shape. The escalation ladder (witness → nominate → back → share → host → fund → document) is exactly the Long Walk's "walk for an hour or walk the whole way."
- Make the national tour a visible, single, moving thing. Adelaide is one step on a walk that continues to Perth, Victoria and beyond. Each city that hosts is joining the walk, not buying a franchise. This reframes the host/partner ask (the top of the ladder) as joining something already in motion, which the Identified and In-conversation contacts can feel.
- The lowest rung must be genuinely effortless and genuinely meaningful, the way walking one kilometre was. For CONTAINED that is "witness" — book the 30 minutes, bring one person who would never normally come. Everything else stacks on that first honest step.

**Failure mode to avoid.** A national tour can read as a roadshow that arrives, performs, and leaves — the extractive pattern. The Long Walk worked because it walked WITH communities, not at them. CONTAINED must leave each city with the local wall, the local relationships, and the local young people's work intact and credited, so the tour deposits rather than extracts.

## 5. Bringing Them Home / Sorry Day — testimony, witnessed, with the right to grieve held by the people whose story it is

**The pattern: first-person testimony, nationally witnessed, on the community's terms.** The Bringing Them Home report (1997) gathered the testimony of Stolen Generations survivors. National Sorry Day was established in 1998, one year to the day after the report was tabled, as an annual act of witnessing. The 2008 National Apology was the moment the nation formally listened. The power was in survivors telling their own stories and the country being asked to witness, not to fix. ([2020socialjustice.com](https://2020socialjustice.com/sorry-day/), [Meanjin, a decade on](https://meanjin.com.au/essays/how-we-said-sorry-reflecting-on-the-apology-a-decade-on/))

**Why it worked.** Testimony in the first person is unarguable. You cannot fact-check grief. A formal, repeated day of witnessing gave the nation a structure to hold the story without consuming or resolving it. The survivors kept authorship; the nation took responsibility.

**The play CONTAINED runs — the room is a witnessing structure; consent governs every word.**
- CONTAINED's whole architecture is witnessing: nobody is filmed inside, nothing is asked of you, you witness and you leave changed. That is the Sorry Day move applied to youth justice. Name it that way to community partners — CONTAINED asks the public to witness, not to perform sympathy.
- Where a young person's words, name, image or cell design appear (Room 1 is designed by young people at each stop), they are testimony, and the young person holds authorship. Per the binding calendar gate, no name, image or story without confirmed consent. Treat consent as ongoing and revocable, not a one-time signature. This is workstream 3, and it is gated for good reason.
- Build a small, repeatable act of witnessing into the tour rhythm, the way Sorry Day repeats. The daily Adelaide recap and the comment harvest can become a structured "what people witnessed today," in the community's framing, never a highlight reel of distressed young people.

**Failure mode to avoid.** The opposite of witnessing is spectacle. The instant a young person's pain becomes content — a powerful clip, a fundraising image, a quote pulled out of context — CONTAINED crosses from Sorry Day into extraction. Hold the line: the young people are co-authors, never beneficiaries, never "case studies." If a piece of content would make a young person an object, it does not ship.

## 6. OCAP and "nothing about us without us" — the protocol layer underneath all of it

**The pattern: community owns, controls, accesses and possesses the story and the data.** OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession), developed with First Nations communities, plus the CARE principles from the Global Indigenous Data Alliance, set the standard: communities own their information, control its use at every stage, can access it, and possess it. The companion principle in storytelling is "nothing about us without us" — Indigenous self-representation, creative control, community consultation, cultural protocols, because "we are the only ones who can speak for us." ([fnigc.ca OCAP training](https://fnigc.ca/ocap-training/), [University of Toronto on OCAP](https://mdl.library.utoronto.ca/mdl-blog/first-nations-principles-ownership-control-access-and-possession-ocapr-pathway-data), [Arapahoe Libraries on Indigenous filmmakers](https://arapahoelibraries.org/blogs/post/nothing-about-us-without-us-how-indigenous-filmmakers-are-changing-the-narrative/), [UN, breaking media stereotypes](https://www.un.org/en/academic-impact/we-are-indigenous-breaking-media-stereotypes-indigenous-storytelling))

**Why it worked.** Protocols turn good intentions into structure. Once ownership and control sit with community by design, extraction stops being possible, not just frowned upon. The principle moves the question from "did we mean well" to "who holds the wheel," which is the only question that protects against the white-savior failure mode.

**The play CONTAINED runs — make the protocol layer visible and binding.**
- This is already CONTAINED's architecture: Empathy Ledger is named the "consent, story, and community authority layer." Make that promise concrete to the 34 SA orgs and to young people's orgs. A story enters CONTAINED only with consent, stays under the storyteller's control, and can be withdrawn. Say it plainly and put it in writing.
- Run a "nothing about us without us" check on every public surface before it ships, alongside the existing seven Imagination Architect tests. For any piece featuring a community org or a young person: did the named people see it, shape it, and agree to it? If not, it does not go live.
- Default to real names of real community heroes (Kristy, Tanya, the named orgs), never "Indigenous communities" in the abstract. The language guardrails already require naming the country, language group or organisation. OCAP makes that a sovereignty practice, not just a style rule.

**Failure mode to avoid.** Tokenism reads as Aboriginal art on a settler document while the settler framework runs underneath. For CONTAINED that would look like a beautiful wall of community orgs wrapped around a campaign whose decisions, money and narrative control all sit with us. The wall must reflect real authority, real consent and, where young people work, real pay. Decoration without control is the exact thing First Nations campaigners name and reject.

## 7. Failure modes to avoid (consolidated)

1. **Borrowing the language of community-led while keeping the wheel.** The deepest risk. Maranguka and OCAP both say authority comes first. If CONTAINED implies it runs, ranks, or owns the orgs on the wall, it becomes extractive.
2. **The roadshow that arrives, performs and leaves.** The Long Walk worked because it walked with communities. A tour that takes the story and the photos and departs deposits nothing. Leave each city's wall, relationships and young people's work intact and credited.
3. **Spectacle over witnessing.** The instant a young person's pain becomes a clip or a fundraising image, CONTAINED crosses from Sorry Day into extraction. Co-authors, never case studies.
4. **A worthy ask that gets nodded at and shelved.** Raise the Age has stalled for years. The container's 30-minute conversion plus a single repeated nominate demand is the antidote; do not let either go soft.
5. **Out-communicated when it goes contested.** The Voice lost the national argument. CONTAINED must carry its legitimacy in the room so it never depends on winning a politicised public debate to land.
6. **Tokenism: art on the document, settler framework underneath.** Decoration without control. The wall must reflect real authority, real consent, and real pay where young people work.

## 8. The 3–5 highest-leverage transferable plays

**1. Make Room 3 a community-governed dialogue, not a curated logo wall (Uluru + Maranguka).** Before any SA org goes on the wall, hold a small gathering or one call each (the 34 Personal-outreach contacts) where they author their own panel in their own words. Frame it as an invitation to help hold the room, not a request to use their logo. This is the single act that makes the difference between the 34 feeling used and feeling sovereign, and it is the consent gate Room 3 already depends on. *Highest leverage because it converts the campaign's biggest risk (extraction) into its biggest strength (authentic community authority) at the exact point the launch depends on.*

**2. Run one repeated demand under every nominate, anchored to three canonical numbers (Raise the Age).** Keep the ask identical across all nominations so pressure aggregates: walk through before your next decision about a young person. Anchor it with ~23x Indigenous over-representation, 3,261 dollars/day to detain one child in SA (ROGS 2024-25), and 84% returning to supervision within 12 months (AIHW 2023-24). Use the 7 Partner/committed contacts as the visible, named coalition spine.

**3. Make the national tour one visible moving thing anyone can join at any depth (The Long Walk).** Adelaide is one step, not a launch you repeat. Each city joining the walk, not buying a franchise. Keep the lowest rung (witness: book 30 minutes, bring one person who would never come) genuinely effortless and genuinely meaningful, so it opens onto the higher rungs the Identified and In-conversation contacts can climb.

**4. Position the experience as witnessing, governed by binding consent (Sorry Day + OCAP).** Name CONTAINED publicly as asking people to witness, not to perform sympathy. Where a young person's words, name or cell design appear, they hold authorship: ongoing, revocable consent, never spectacle. Make Empathy Ledger's "consent, story and community authority" promise concrete and in writing to the SA orgs and young people's orgs. Run a "nothing about us without us" check on every surface before it ships.

**5. Tell the savings story as reinvestment in working community models, with the proof under community control (Maranguka).** For the 23 Identified funders, the strongest argument is the Australian precedent: Bourke returned millions through a community-owned model. CONTAINED points at that work; it does not annex it. JusticeHub and Empathy Ledger are the community's evidence layer, not ours.
