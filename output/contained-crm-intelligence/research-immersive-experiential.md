## The frame: an experience is not the campaign, it is the ignition

Every campaign below proved one thing and then had to prove a second. The first proof is easy to feel: a room can move a person more than a report ever will. The second proof is the hard one: a moved person does nothing unless the room hands them the next thing to do before the feeling fades.

CONTAINED already understands the first proof. One container. Three rooms. The cure already exists. A fifteen-year-old's reality, then what works instead, then the organisations already doing it. Thirty minutes, alone, phone and shoes at the door. That is the strongest emotional front door in Australian youth justice right now.

What this research is for is the second proof. The graveyard of immersive advocacy is full of experiences that moved everyone and changed nothing, because the exit was a door and not a doorway. The campaigns that changed minds AND outcomes all did the same three things at the threshold of leaving: they handed over a single concrete action at peak emotion, they captured the person so the relationship could continue, and they gave the person a way to carry the room to someone else. Witness, then capture, then ripple. That is the spine of this whole document, and CONTAINED's escalation ladder already names it: witness, nominate, stand with it, host or partner.

The rest of this is how the best in the world did each rung, what it cost the ones who skipped it, and the play CONTAINED runs in Adelaide on 23 June and on the national tour after.

## Part 1 — The campaigns, by archetype

### A. Memorial as activism: the EJI Legacy Museum and the National Memorial for Peace and Justice

In Montgomery, Alabama, the Equal Justice Initiative under Bryan Stevenson built two sites within walking distance of each other. The Legacy Museum tells the story from enslavement to mass incarceration through first-person narratives, data-rich exhibits, and art. The National Memorial for Peace and Justice is an open-air structure of 800 weathered-steel columns, one for each county where a racial-terror lynching happened, each engraved with the names of the dead. As a visitor walks through, the floor descends, and by the end the columns hang overhead, placing the visitor in the position of the spectators in old lynching photographs. EJI documented more than 4,000 racial-terror lynchings across twelve Southern states between 1870 and World War II ([EJI](https://eji.org/news/museum-and-memorial-opening-april-26-2018/); [NYU Law](https://www.law.nyu.edu/news/ideas/bryan-stevenson-national-memorial-for-peace-and-justice-legacy-museum-from-enslavement-to-mass-incarceration)).

Why it changed minds: the architecture does the argument. Nobody is lectured. The descending floor IS the thesis. The visitor reaches the conclusion in their own body, which is the only conclusion that survives contact with the car park.

The mechanic that matters most for CONTAINED is not the museum. It is what EJI built around it: the **Community Remembrance Project**. Communities collect soil from the sites of lynchings into labelled jars, which become an exhibit inside the Legacy Museum, and they install narrative historical markers in the actual public places where the violence happened. Since 2015 the project has supported more than 80 historical markers, soil collected from roughly 700 sites, and a racial-justice essay contest for over 900 high-school students ([EJI Community Remembrance Project](https://eji.org/projects/community-remembrance-project/)). The visit to Montgomery is the ignition. The soil jar in your own county, dug by your own hands, is the ripple. The museum gave the country a feeling. The remembrance project gave each town a job.

This is the single most transferable structure in the whole field for CONTAINED, because Room 3 already wants to be it. Room 3 changes every stop to feature the local host orgs. The EJI model says: do not just feature them on a wall, give the visitor a piece of the local work to carry home and finish.

### B. The literal shipping container: Shared Studios "Portals"

In 2014 Amar Bakshi placed a gold-painted shipping container in a public park, fitted it with immersive audio and video, and connected it live to an identical container thousands of miles away. You step in, and you are full-body, eye-to-eye with a stranger in Tehran, Gaza, Kigali, or a refugee camp, having a private twenty-minute conversation, both of you answering the same prompt: "What would make today a good day for you?" Portals have connected more than 60,000 people across twenty-plus countries, from refugee camps to corporate boardrooms ([Shared Studios / Design Indaba](https://www.designindaba.com/articles/creative-work/portals-project-shared-studios-brings-together-strangers-across-world); [Wikipedia: Portals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portals_(initiative))).

This is CONTAINED's literal architectural twin: a shipping container in a public square, a private timed experience inside, a single shared prompt. The lessons are precise.

The prompt is everything. "What would make today a good day for you?" is not abstract and not political. It is human, answerable by anyone, and it produces intimacy in twenty minutes because it lowers the stakes of the first sentence. CONTAINED needs an equivalent threshold question, one that turns the visitor from spectator into participant the moment they exit. The campaign already has a candidate, and it is better than Portals' because it is an ask, not just an opener: "Who needs to walk through this before they make the next decision about a young person?"

The timed, private, full-attention container creates a scarce, almost sacred social object. People queue. People talk about having been inside. The gold paint made Portals legible as one thing in every city, an identity that travelled. CONTAINED's container is its own gold paint: the same object, recognisable from Mount Druitt to Kaurna Yarta to Boorloo (Perth), is what makes a national tour feel like one movement rather than ten unrelated events.

### C. VR empathy, and the one hard conversion number: "Clouds Over Sidra"

In January 2015, at the World Economic Forum in Davos, the United Nations premiered "Clouds Over Sidra", a virtual-reality film by Gabo Arora and Chris Milk following a twelve-year-old girl through a day in the Za'atari refugee camp in Jordan. UNICEF then took it to the streets in 40 countries with Google Cardboard and Samsung Gear headsets, in a program called Translating Empathy to Action ([MIT Docubase](https://docubase.mit.edu/project/clouds-over-sidra/); [Atlas Corps](https://atlascorps.org/virtual-reality-refugees/)).

Here is the number that should govern CONTAINED's whole capture strategy. UNICEF reported that the VR experience **doubled the face-to-face donation rate, from 1 in 12 people without VR to 1 in 6 with it, and lifted the average gift by about 10%** ([Atlas Corps](https://atlascorps.org/virtual-reality-refugees/)).

Read that twice. The experience did not just make people feel more. It roughly doubled the conversion to a concrete commitment, measured at the moment of the ask. The lesson is not "immersion works". The lesson is "immersion works AT THE MOMENT OF THE ASK, with a fundraiser standing right there". The donation happened on the street, headset still warm, a human present, an envelope open. Not a follow-up email three days later. CONTAINED's equivalent of the open envelope is a host at the exit, a nomination card in hand, and a QR that takes ten seconds, all while the visitor is still inside the feeling.

### D. Sensory recreation of confinement: the Guardian's "6x9" and the physical replica cells

The Guardian's "6x9", built by Francesca Panetta in 2016 over nine months, put viewers inside a virtual composite of real segregation cells to convey the sensory and psychological deprivation of solitary confinement, drawing on the testimony of former prisoners and psychologists. It opened with the fact that an estimated 80,000 to 100,000 people in US prisons are held in solitary, around 23 hours a day with no human contact ([The Drum](https://www.thedrum.com/news/2016/06/03/could-you-survive-solitary-behind-scenes-guardians-6x9-vr-experience)).

The physical-world cousins are even closer to CONTAINED's Room 1. The ACLU of Nebraska installed a 6x9-foot replica solitary cell at the State Capitol with a bed pallet and a combined toilet-sink, tied to a specific policy ask: limit isolation to no more than 15 consecutive days, the UN Nelson Mandela Rules standard ([ACLU Nebraska](https://www.aclunebraska.org/press-releases/replica-solitary-confinement-cell-installed-at-capitol/)). Yale Law's Liman Program and a coalition ran "Inside the Box", a three-week replica-cell display in the New Haven public library and on campus, surrounded by community discussions, film screenings, expert panels, and a talk by the Connecticut Corrections Commissioner ([Yale Law](https://law.yale.edu/yls-today/news/replica-solitary-confinement-cell-coming-new-haven)). Talib Akbar, a former prisoner, tours a replica cell around Wisconsin with an audio track of inmates howling from nearby cells ([Yahoo News](https://news.yahoo.com/traveling-solitary-confinement-cell-replica-100307483.html)).

Two transferable moves here. First, the replica cell that lives at the seat of power, the Nebraska Capitol, put the experience in front of the exact decision-makers it was trying to move, on their own ground. CONTAINED's Priority A walkthroughs (courts, MPs, budget leads) are the same instinct: do not wait for the magistrate to come to the container, schedule the magistrate's private slot. Second, the cell was never just a cell. It was a three-week hub with panels, screenings, and the Corrections Commissioner himself in the room. The object draws the crowd; the programming converts the crowd. CONTAINED beside the Reintegration Puzzle Conference, 24 to 25 June, is exactly this, and the conference is the programming that converts the proximity.

### E. The behavioral evidence, and the honest critique

The strongest experiment-grade evidence that an immersive experience changes ACTION, not just feeling, comes from Stanford's "Becoming Homeless" study (Jeremy Bailenson's Virtual Human Interaction Lab, PLOS ONE, 2018). People who went through the VR experience of losing their job and home signed a real petition supporting affordable housing at much higher rates: **85% of the VR group signed, against 63% who read the same story as text and 66% who saw a 2D version** ([Stanford Report](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2018/10/virtual-reality-can-help-make-people-empathetic); [PLOS ONE](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0204494)). The effect persisted over weeks, not minutes.

But the field has an honest critique CONTAINED must respect, or it will overclaim and get caught. Researchers warn there is limited long-term evidence that immersive empathy reliably converts into sustained pro-social behaviour, that the "empathy machine" framing can become "identity tourism" that turns marginalised people into objects of feeling for the privileged, and that a short experience cannot represent a long lived reality ([Aeon](https://aeon.co/ideas/its-dangerous-to-think-virtual-reality-is-an-empathy-machine); [Disrupting the empathy machine, NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9549362/)). The critique sharpens CONTAINED's design rather than threatening it. CONTAINED already refuses identity tourism by design: nobody is filmed inside, young people are co-authors who build the rooms and host them for pay, and Room 3 hands the visitor toward the real organisations doing the work rather than leaving them marinating in the suffering of Room 1. The defence against "you made me feel bad and then left" is the campaign's own rule: never start and end inside detention, always arrive at the alternative. Hold that line and the critique becomes a credential.

### F. The cautionary tale: Kony 2012

Invisible Children's "Kony 2012" video reached more than 100 million views in days, the fastest-spreading advocacy video of its era. Then it evaporated. The campaign's offline action day, "Cover the Night" on 20 April 2012, was the test of whether the feeling had become a movement. In Sydney, of roughly 19,000 people who registered online to attend, **about 25 actually turned up** ([Nonprofit Quarterly](https://nonprofitquarterly.org/why-did-kony-2012-fizzle-out/); [factually.co](https://factually.co/fact-checks/society/kony-2012-failure-reasons-8aaca7)). The organisation collapsed soon after, amid questions about oversimplification, accountability, and financial transparency.

The diagnosis is one sentence, and it should be pinned above every CONTAINED planning meeting: **virality without structure is spectacle.** The feeling was real and enormous. The infrastructure to catch it, hold it, and walk each person to a meaningful next step did not exist. People clicked, felt, shared, and then had nothing to do that mattered, so they did nothing. CONTAINED's risk profile is the inverse and the safer one: a small, deliberate, high-intimacy experience with a real evidence layer (JusticeHub) underneath it. The danger for CONTAINED is not too little reach. It is reaching the launch with the booking funnel empty and the warmest fourteen inbound contacts never told the container is happening. The Sydney 25 is what an empty capture layer looks like at scale. CONTAINED's defence is its 78 named people and a relationship owner for each, but only if the capture and follow-up actually run.

## Part 2 — Winning patterns, and the concrete play CONTAINED runs

Each pattern below names the move, says why it worked in the wild, and then writes the specific thing CONTAINED does with its container, its 78-contact base, its nominate mechanic, its Adelaide-then-national shape, and its Indigenous-justice grounding.

### Pattern 1 — The ask lives at the threshold, not in the inbox three days later

Why it worked: Clouds Over Sidra doubled donations because the ask happened with the headset still warm and a human present (1 in 6, not 1 in 12). Becoming Homeless got 85% petition signatures because the petition was right there at the end of the experience. The conversion window is measured in seconds, not days. Kony 2012 proved the inverse: defer the ask to "later" and "later" becomes "never".

The play CONTAINED runs: position a trained young host at the container exit holding two physical objects, a printed nomination card and a single-question reflection card, and frame the ask as the locked door-opener: "Who needs to walk through this before their next decision about a young person? Write their name." The exit QR (idea bank #15) takes the visitor to `/contained` to complete the nomination digitally in ten seconds. This is CONTAINED's open envelope. Do not rely on the post-experience cron email alone, which only fires off a reflection that contains an email and only if EMAIL_ENABLED is confirmed true in prod (currently flagged unverified in the idea bank). The human at the threshold is the conversion engine. The email is the backup, not the front line.

### Pattern 2 — One named action, chosen for the person in front of you, never a menu

Why it worked: every campaign that converted offered ONE next thing at the moment of peak feeling. EJI: collect the soil from your county. ACLU Nebraska: cap isolation at 15 days. Clouds Over Sidra: give now. A menu of five asks at peak emotion produces paralysis and a polite nod. CONTAINED's audience map already segments the single ask per audience, and that discipline is the asset.

The play CONTAINED runs: pre-assign each booked visitor a single rung before they arrive, owned by their relationship owner. A magistrate or MP gets nominate-and-cite (witness privately, then who else needs to walk through). A funder gets back-the-next-city, the $30K-a-stop frame and a twenty-minute conversation at `/contained/invest`. A service org gets stand-with-it, get your organisation onto the next city's Room 3 wall. The warm public gets witness-and-bring-one, book and bring one person who would never normally come. The container is constant. The single ask is tailored, decided in the CRM before the door opens, not improvised at the exit.

### Pattern 3 — The take-home object that makes the visitor finish the work

Why it worked: EJI's Community Remembrance Project is the masterclass. The Montgomery visit is the ignition; the soil jar dug in your own county, the historical marker installed in your own town, the essay your own students write, those are the ripple. More than 80 markers and 700 soil sites came from handing local people a tangible piece of the work to complete at home ([EJI](https://eji.org/projects/community-remembrance-project/)). The experience did not end at the exit. It started a job.

The play CONTAINED runs: Room 3 already changes every stop to feature local organisations. Extend it from a wall the visitor looks at into a thing the visitor carries. The take-home object is the nomination, made physical and trackable: a card naming the decision-maker the visitor thought of inside, that becomes a real CRM record through the nominee bridge (idea bank #4, the nightly hand-built path to the named decision-maker). The visitor leaves Adelaide having started a job, getting the decision-maker they named into the same room. The Indigenous-justice grounding deepens this: in each city the local Aboriginal-led and community-controlled organisations on the Room 3 wall (in Adelaide, KWY, Tjindu, ALRM, SAACCON, Tiraapendi Wodli, Iwiri, NPY) are the heroes the visitor is being pointed toward, with consent confirmed by a named human at each org before the wall is built. The take-home is not a souvenir of suffering. It is a thread connecting the visitor to the real local work and a named person they have moved one rung.

### Pattern 4 — Bring the room to the seat of power, do not wait for power to wander in

Why it worked: the ACLU's replica solitary cell stood inside the Nebraska State Capitol, putting the experience directly in front of the legislators it was built to move, on their own ground, attached to one precise ask. Yale's "Inside the Box" pulled the Connecticut Corrections Commissioner into the room. You do not change a decision-maker's mind by hoping they buy a ticket. You schedule them a private slot.

The play CONTAINED runs: this is the Priority A walkthrough, and it is already in the strategy. Personal, never bulk. The 23 Identified contacts and the nominated decision-makers each get a hand-written private invite carrying the nominator's warrant softly: "someone who works alongside you asked us to make sure you were invited to walk through this before your next decision." Adelaide sits beside the Reintegration Puzzle Conference precisely so the practitioners and decision-makers are already on the ground. The play is to treat the conference delegates as the Nebraska Capitol: the power is already standing next to the container, and Hannah March at JRI is the one person who unblocks that whole audience (idea bank #7). Book the slots into their conference breaks before they arrive.

### Pattern 5 — One recognisable object that travels makes ten events into one movement

Why it worked: Portals' gold paint made a container in Kigali and a container in New York legibly the same thing, one global initiative, not scattered local art. The visual identity travelled, so the story compounded across cities instead of resetting. Conversely, the campaigns that read as a different thing in each place never accumulated into a movement.

The play CONTAINED runs: the container itself is the gold paint. The same object, the same three rooms, the same thirty minutes, recognisable from Mount Druitt (stop one) to Kaurna Yarta (stop two, Adelaide) to Boorloo (Perth), Mparntwe (Alice Springs), Meanjin (Brisbane), Warrane (Sydney), Ngambri/Ngunnawal (Canberra), Naarm (Melbourne), nipaluna (Hobart), and the Northern Rivers. What must stay constant is the object and the spine. What must change is Room 3, the local wall. The next-city trigger pack (idea bank #23) is how Adelaide becomes the template a future-city host or funder can pick up: the $30K-a-stop maths, the org-wall model, the host-recruitment model, the consent protocol, bottled into one forwardable page. The tour's power is that stop ten still looks like stop one, so a funder backing Perth knows exactly what they are buying.

### Pattern 6 — Capture is the campaign, the experience is just the reason to capture

Why it worked: this is the hard lesson under all of it. Clouds Over Sidra worked because UNICEF stood there with an open ask in 40 countries and measured the 1-in-6 conversion. Kony 2012 failed because 19,000 Sydney registrations had no path to the 25 who showed; the capture layer was a registration form with nothing behind it. The experience is the front door. The CRM is the house.

The play CONTAINED runs: the empty Adelaide booking pipeline is the single highest-leverage problem in the entire campaign, and it is the Kony failure mode in miniature, a great experience ten days out with nobody booked. Three moves close it, all read off roster.json and the live pipeline stage, never a `project:contained` tag query. First, the EOI link rides in every personal reply, every LinkedIn post, the container QR, and the email signature (idea bank #2). Second, the fourteen inbound contacts in "In conversation" who asked about the container and were never told it is happening get a personal human reply this week (idea bank #1); an inbound lead going cold because nobody replied is the most expensive miss in the book. Third, every walk-through is captured at the threshold (Pattern 1) so footfall becomes a follow-up path rather than anonymous attendance. The escalation ladder is the house behind the door: witness, nominate, stand with it, host or partner, and each contact moves exactly one rung per touch. That is Ben's Tier-3 call to action; this document just maps the rungs.

## Part 3 — Failure modes to avoid (what sank the others)

1. **The Kony cliff: virality with no capture layer.** 19,000 Sydney registrations, 25 attendees. The feeling was real and the infrastructure to catch it did not exist. CONTAINED's guard: the 78 named contacts each have a relationship owner and a single next rung, and the booking funnel gets filled by hand this week, not left to organic demand. The experience without the CRM is a Sydney rally of 25.

2. **Defer the ask and lose it.** Conversion happens in seconds at the threshold, not in an email days later. CONTAINED's guard: a human host at the exit with a physical nomination card, not sole reliance on the post-experience cron (especially while EMAIL_ENABLED is unverified in prod). The open envelope beats the follow-up every time.

3. **Identity tourism: making people the object of feeling.** The empathy-machine critique is that a short immersion can turn a marginalised person into a privileged visitor's emotional souvenir. CONTAINED's guard is built in: nobody filmed inside, young people are paid co-authors who build and host the rooms, Room 3 points toward real Aboriginal-led and community-controlled organisations, and the campaign never ends inside detention. Hold the "always arrive at the alternative" rule and the critique becomes a credential. Break it and CONTAINED becomes the thing the critics warn about.

4. **Overclaiming the evidence.** The honest research says long-term behaviour change from immersion is real but modest and not guaranteed. CONTAINED's guard: let JusticeHub carry the evidence claims with sourced figures (brand-guide §7, or link `/contained/brief` and quote nothing), and let the container carry only the feeling. Never claim the room changes policy. Claim the room opens the door and the evidence layer closes it. Diagrama's numbers stay behind the wording-approval gate until the partner signs off; lead qualitative until then.

5. **A menu of asks at peak emotion.** Five options produce paralysis. CONTAINED's guard: one named rung per visitor, pre-assigned in the CRM by relationship owner, decided before the door opens.

6. **Letting each city read as a different thing.** Without one recognisable object and one constant spine, ten events never compound into a movement and each city's funder starts from zero. CONTAINED's guard: the container and the three-room spine stay identical; only Room 3's local wall changes; the next-city trigger pack makes Adelaide the reusable template.

## Part 4 — The 3 to 5 highest-leverage transferable plays

These are the moves that decide whether CONTAINED becomes EJI's Community Remembrance Project (a feeling that built 80 markers) or Kony's Cover the Night (a feeling that produced 25 people in a park).

1. **Convert at the threshold, in human hands.** Trained young host at the exit with a physical nomination card and the locked door-opener question, plus a ten-second exit QR. This is Clouds Over Sidra's open envelope (1 in 6, not 1 in 12). The post-experience email is the backup, never the front line, while EMAIL_ENABLED is unverified.

2. **One named rung per visitor, pre-assigned in the CRM.** No menu at peak emotion. Magistrate gets nominate-and-cite, funder gets back-the-next-city, service org gets onto-the-next-wall, warm public gets witness-and-bring-one. The container is constant; the single ask is tailored before the door opens, owned by the relationship owner across the 78.

3. **Make the nomination the take-home that finishes the work.** Borrow EJI's soil-jar logic: the visitor leaves having named a real decision-maker, and the nightly nominee bridge turns that name into a tracked record and a personal invite carrying the nominator's warrant. The visitor's job is to get the person they named into the same room.

4. **Fill the booking funnel by hand this week, off roster.json, before relying on any organic demand.** The empty Adelaide pipeline is the Kony failure mode in miniature. EOI link everywhere, personal replies to the fourteen who asked and were never told, threshold capture of every walk-through. Read the honest 78, never the polluted tag query.

5. **Keep the container and the spine identical across cities; change only Room 3 and bottle Adelaide as the template.** The container is CONTAINED's gold paint, the thing that makes a national tour one movement. The next-city trigger pack turns stop two into the reusable model a Perth or Brisbane host can pick up, with the Indigenous-led local organisations as the heroes of each city's wall.
