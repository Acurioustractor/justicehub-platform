# Research: Movement-Building and Community Organizing Playbooks for CONTAINED

> What this is: a study of the campaigns that turned warm sympathy into organised, self-propagating power, read for one purpose. CONTAINED has 78 warm contacts, a container that moves people in thirty minutes, a nominate mechanic, and ten days to Adelaide. This file names the winning patterns, says why each worked, and writes the exact play CONTAINED runs, tied to its own shape. It ends with the five highest-leverage transfers.

## The frame before the patterns

A campaign and a movement are not the same shape. A campaign broadcasts and counts who responds. A movement hands each responder a job that recruits the next person, so the base grows while the founder sleeps. CONTAINED is a campaign with a movement's raw material: an experience so physical it changes the person who walks through, and a question, "who needs to walk through this before they make the next decision," that asks the visitor to act before they leave.

The six bodies of practice below all solve the same problem CONTAINED has right now. Seventy-eight people are warm. Most have never been asked to do anything. The container can move thousands, and then what. The pattern that wins is always the same underneath: turn the moment of feeling into a job, turn the job into a relationship, turn the relationship into a structure that outlives the moment.

Three things shape every play here.

The container is the conversion engine no other campaign had. Marriage Equality had a kitchen table and a story. CONTAINED has thirty minutes, alone, phone and shoes at the door, that does what an hour of argument cannot. The walk-through is the strongest "story of us" device in this whole study. Build the ladder so the door is always rung one.

The 78 are not an audience. They are the first snowflake. Twenty-three in the funder lane, thirty-four on the SA service-org wall, fourteen inbound and hot, seven already committed. A broadcast list of 78 is small. A structure where each of the 78 carries one relationship outward is a base of hundreds before Adelaide opens.

Adelaide-then-national-tour is the snowflake's natural shape. A touring container that changes Room 3 in every city is, structurally, a hub model: one method, local hosts, local heroes, central spine. The campaigns below that scaled did it exactly this way. The ones that broke did it without the spine.

## Pattern 1: Public Narrative, the story of self / us / now (Marshall Ganz)

**Name it.** Marshall Ganz, the United Farm Workers organiser turned Harvard teacher, codified the practice that motivates people to act through three linked stories. A *story of self* says who I am and why I do this. A *story of us* says who we are and what we share. A *story of now* turns the present into a moment of challenge, hope, and choice that demands action today. The roots are Rabbi Hillel: if I am not for myself who will be, if I am for myself alone what am I, if not now when.

**Why it worked.** Ganz's insight is that people do not act on data, they act on a story that moves them through fear to hope to a choice. Self earns the right to ask. Us makes the listener a member, not a spectator. Now makes inaction itself a decision. It built the farm-worker movement, it became Camp Obama's leadership template, and it is the spine under almost every campaign below. The trap it avoids is the campaign that leads with the problem's size, which produces despair, not action. Ganz leads with a person, then a we, then a choosable now.

**The CONTAINED play.** CONTAINED already holds all three stories. The discipline is to order them the way Ganz does and never lead with the statistic.

- *Story of self.* Ben's locked email voice is already a story of self: honest, specific, "here is what isn't figured out yet." Keep it as the opener on every personal touch to the 78. The funder fourteen and the inbound fourteen get Ben's why, not the campaign's pitch.
- *Story of us.* The container is the story of us made physical. Room 1 is the shared reality. Room 3, the wall of SA organisations, is the "we" the visitor joins. When the SA service orgs see themselves on that wall, the wall says "you are us." That is why the consent calls to the 34 are not admin, they are the story-of-us being built one yes at a time.
- *Story of now.* "Doors open Tuesday 23 June" is the now. The nominate mechanic is the choice inside the now: "who needs to walk through this before their next decision about a young person." The figure that carries the now is the SA cost frame, used once, sourced on the surface: $3,261 a day to hold one child in South Australia (Productivity Commission ROGS 2024-25), against $75 a day for what works (Community Services Benchmark Study 2024). One number, inside a story, never as the opener.

**Concrete:** rebuild the funder-fourteen invite and the inbound-fourteen reply on the self/us/now spine. Self (Ben's why, two sentences). Us (Mount Druitt happened, Adelaide is stop two, the orgs on the wall, you belong in this). Now (doors 23 June, here is your thirty minutes, and the one question to carry in). The ladder's rung-one ask is always "witness," because the walk-through is the strongest story-of-us CONTAINED can deliver.

**Failure mode to avoid.** Leading with the despair number. A story that opens "$1.33 million a year to lock up one child" and stops there recruits no one. It confirms the problem and closes the wallet. Ganz's order is self, then us, then now, with the figure as the hinge inside "now," never the headline. CONTAINED's own rule already says this: never start and end inside detention, always arrive at the alternative.

## Pattern 2: The kitchen-table conversation and the crowdsourced story (Australian Marriage Equality, 2017)

**Name it.** The campaign that won the 2017 postal survey ran on two engines: brave couples telling their own stories publicly, and ordinary supporters having one-to-one conversations with people they already knew. Its own framing: "a campaign about conversations, with someone you know, about someone you know." It crowdsourced authentic story at scale (more than 150 hours of footage in five days), trained supporters with an opener ("I'm voting Yes, can I tell you why?") instead of a fact sheet, seeded local groups across electorates, and built a coalition of more than 2,000 organisations and 15,000 volunteers. It made every supporter "the expert" and "the best person to speak in their community."

**Why it worked.** The moveable middle does not move for a national ad. It moves when someone it trusts says "this matters to me, here is why." The campaign turned its base from an audience into messengers, each working their own network, each a local voice no head office could fake. Story did the emotional work; the relationship did the persuasion. The one named gap: converting clicktivists into offline organisers took deliberate effort beyond the social feed.

**The CONTAINED play.** CONTAINED's container is a better kitchen table than the kitchen table. The play is to make every walk-through end with the visitor recruiting one person.

- *The "bring one person" multiplier* is the marriage-equality conversation built into the booking. Every personal invite and every booking confirmation carries one line: bring one person who would never normally come. The witness becomes a messenger at the moment of highest intent. This is already idea 14 in the bank; this study says it is not a nice-to-have, it is the core conversion mechanic the winning campaign ran.
- *Crowdsource the story, on consent.* Marriage Equality's footage was its fuel. CONTAINED's equivalent is the visitor reflection, captured at the exit on consent, fed through Empathy Ledger into JusticeHub. Real people, real reactions, their words. This is the renewable story supply for the national tour. Held entirely behind consent on their terms; no young person's name, image, or story until confirmed.
- *Local messengers, not a head-office voice.* In Room 3, the SA organisations are the local messengers. On the national tour, each city's host org becomes the trusted local voice the way each electorate had its own. CONTAINED never imports a spokesperson into a city; it hands the room to the people already doing the work there. "For them, not about them."

**Concrete:** add the "bring one person" line to every personal invite to the 78 now, and to every booking confirmation. Brief the 34 SA orgs not just as wall-features but as the local voices who tell their own networks the container is coming. The ask to an SA org is not "consent to appear," it is "you are the local messenger for your community, here is the container that points people to you."

**Failure mode to avoid.** The clicktivist gap. A LinkedIn comment is not an organiser. Marriage Equality learned that turning a "like" into a doorknock took a deliberate next ask. CONTAINED's daily-comment-harvest (idea 19) is the bridge: every warm commenter gets a same-day human reply and a concrete next rung, not a thank-you. A base that only ever clicks is not a base.

## Pattern 3: The snowflake and the hub structure (Sunrise Movement)

**Name it.** Sunrise grew fast on the *snowflake model*, Ganz's structure for distributed leadership: every leader's job is to develop the leadership of others, and the relationships between them are the glue. No one person holds the power. Sunrise paired it with a *hub structure*: mostly geographic hubs with real autonomy, interconnected, each feeding the movement's overall strategy. Paid staff spent their time enabling and training volunteer organisers rather than mobilising supporters directly.

**Why it worked.** The snowflake multiplies a tiny staff. One organiser who recruits and develops ten team-leaders, each of whom develops ten more, builds a base no broadcast list can match, and it keeps functioning when the organiser steps away. The hub structure let Sunrise be one movement in a hundred places at once, each place owned locally.

**The CONTAINED play.** This is the single most important structural pattern for CONTAINED, because CONTAINED is already a touring hub model waiting to be named as one.

- *The 78 are the first ring of the snowflake.* Do not treat them as a list to broadcast to. Treat the seven Partner/committed and the warmest of the fourteen inbound as the first team-leaders. Each one's job is not to attend; it is to carry CONTAINED to one corner of their world. Hannah March (JRI) already is this, unblocking an entire conference audience through one relationship. That is the snowflake working. Find the other six.
- *Each tour city is a hub.* Room 3 changing in every city is the hub structure in physical form: one method (three rooms, the question, the ladder), local autonomy (the city's own orgs on the wall), central spine (CONTAINED, JusticeHub, the consent protocol, the figures). The next-city trigger pack (idea 23) is the hub starter-kit: here is how Adelaide happened, now run your own.
- *Staff enable, they do not deliver.* The lesson for a two-person founder team is brutal and freeing: Ben and Nic cannot personally carry every city. The job in Adelaide is to build the first repeatable hub-kit so that Perth or Melbourne can be run by a local host with the spine handed to them, not by Ben flying in.

**Concrete:** name the snowflake explicitly. Pick the first ring from the 78 (the seven committed plus the hottest inbound), and give each a single carry-job tied to their world, not a generic "please share." Build the Adelaide hub-kit as you go so the tour is a structure, not a sequence of heroics.

**Failure mode to avoid.** Sunrise's own documented shortcoming: distributed organising grows fast and shallow, and leaves nowhere to go when the fast growth stops, because there was no sustained method of deep local organising underneath. For CONTAINED this means: a container that visits a city and leaves no local structure behind is a spike, not a hub. Every stop must leave a named local relationship and a path for that city's orgs to stay on the wall (idea 25), or the tour is a tour of spikes. The spine is what stops the snowflake melting.

## Pattern 4: Distributed and relational organizing (Bernie 2020, the BERN app)

**Name it.** Bernie 2020 ran the largest distributed days of action a presidential campaign had attempted, built on *relational organizing*: asking supporters to reach the people they already know. The BERN app let any supporter log the people in their own life, the campaign matched each to a voter record and noted their support and priority issue, and supporters ran their own house parties and canvasses without a field office watching. Volunteers organised more than 1,600 house parties in under a minute of a launch. The conceit: a campaign amplifies a small staff by orders of magnitude when it empowers motivated volunteers to run their own contact, their own events, their own follow-up.

**Why it worked.** A message from a stranger persuades almost no one; a message from a friend persuades. Relational organising harvests the most valuable asset a campaign has and never owns: the trust already inside each supporter's relationships. Distributed structure removed the bottleneck of "you may only act under supervision," which is the thing that stalls most campaigns at the size of their paid team.

**The CONTAINED play.** CONTAINED's nominate mechanic is relational organising in disguise, and it is currently half-built.

- *Nominate is relational organising.* "Who needs to walk through this before their next decision" asks a supporter to name a specific decision-maker from their own world, the way BERN asked supporters to name the specific people in theirs. The nominator's relationship is the warrant. This is the campaign's most powerful relational move and the bank already flags that it breaks at its most important rung: the nominee never gets reached.
- *Close the loop the way BERN did.* BERN's power was that a logged name became a real, reachable record. CONTAINED's nominee bridge (idea 4) is the same move done by hand for now: every nomination becomes a personal invite to the named decision-maker, carrying the nominator's warrant softly ("someone who works alongside you asked us to make sure you saw this before your next decision"). Without that loop, nominate is a feeling, not an organising act.
- *Let the 78 run their own contact.* The distributed lesson is to stop being the only sender. Where a contact in the 78 has a relationship CONTAINED does not, the play is to equip them to make the ask themselves: hand the seven committed and the warm inbound a forwardable invite and a one-line script, so they invite their own decision-maker, their own funder, their own city. CONTAINED amplifies by handing the ask outward, not by sending more itself.

**Concrete:** run the nominee bridge nightly through launch week so the headline mechanic actually reaches a human. Build a one-paragraph forwardable invite that any of the 78 can send into their own network with their own name on it. The leaderboard (idea 11) makes the relational act visible: every nomination is one supporter using one relationship.

**Failure mode to avoid.** A logged action that goes nowhere kills trust. If a supporter nominates a decision-maker and nothing ever happens, the mechanic teaches people that their action is theatre. The nominee bridge is not optional polish; it is what makes nominate a real organising act rather than a button. Equally, never automate the touch to a nominee: relational means a human carries it, every time.

## Pattern 5: The ladder of engagement, designed rung by rung

**Name it.** The ladder of engagement is the base-building framework that moves a person from observing to leading through deliberately sized steps. Each rung is a slightly bigger ask. Organisers match the ask to the person ("let's talk about what you're up for"), support them through each step, and never skip rungs. The illustrating story: a group went from learning to calculate energy use, to monitoring together, to testifying at a national hearing, a progression that would have been impossible as a first ask. The top rung turns supporters into people who recruit and lead others.

**Why it worked.** Commitment is built, not requested. Ask too much too soon and the person flees; ask nothing and they stay a spectator. The ladder formalises the path so the organisation can see where each person is and offer the right next rung. It is the connective tissue under every pattern above: the snowflake, the relational ask, the kitchen-table conversation are all rungs.

**The CONTAINED play.** CONTAINED already has its ladder named (Beautiful Trouble): Witness → Nominate → Back → Share → Host → Fund → Document. This study sharpens how to run it.

- *Every surface offers the next rung, never a leap.* The ladder's discipline is one step up, not the summit. A first-time LinkedIn commenter is asked to witness (book a slot), not to fund a $30K stop. The funder who has walked through is asked to fund, because they are already high on the ladder. Match the rung to where the person is, which is exactly what the segment matrix already encodes: the inbound fourteen get "witness," the funder fourteen get "fund," the SA orgs get "stand with it / host the wall."
- *The walk-through is the rung that moves people fastest.* In most campaigns the early rungs are small (sign, share). CONTAINED has a rung-one ask that does a rung-five amount of emotional work: thirty minutes inside the container. Design every path so the door is the first real rung, because no other campaign in this study had a single ask that converts a spectator into a believer in thirty minutes.
- *Build the rung above each person now.* The activation-ladder email (idea 21) is the ladder made automatic: a visitor who walked through gets the next rung within 24 hours (nominate the person you thought of inside, share the question, back the next stop). Hand-build the same for the 78: each of them has a known current rung, so each has a known next ask. The seven committed are ready for "host / fund / recruit." The fourteen inbound are ready for "witness, then bring one person." Nobody in the 78 should receive a generic ask.

**Concrete:** map each of the 78 to a current rung and write the single next rung for each segment, not each person (the dossiers already hold the per-person detail). Inbound fourteen: witness + bring one. Funder fourteen: witness then fund the next city. SA orgs thirty-four: host the wall + be the local messenger. Committed seven: recruit one team-leader each (the snowflake's second ring).

**Failure mode to avoid.** The skipped rung. The ladder literature's sharpest warning: people would not have testified as their first step. CONTAINED's version of skipping a rung is asking a cold or barely-warm contact to fund a stop, or sending the funder-brief with a money ask before the person has felt anything. The ground rule: no money ask before a walk-through or a real human exchange. Witness first, always. The other failure is abandoning people mid-ladder: a visitor who walks through and never hears again has been left on a rung with no next step, which is why the exit reflection QR and the activation email exist.

## Pattern 6: The spine, the coalition, and the long foundation (cross-cutting, Marriage Equality + Sunrise)

**Name it.** Two patterns that sit under all the others. First, the *coalition spine*: Marriage Equality won as a coalition of 2,000+ organisations and 15,000 volunteers with shared infrastructure, not a single org shouting. Second, the *long foundation*: the 2017 win rested on fifteen years of prior community activism, seeded local groups, and unlikely allies built one relationship at a time. Sunrise's hubs needed a central strategy spine or they drifted and could be co-opted.

**Why it worked.** A movement needs both distribution and a centre. Pure top-down cannot scale past the staff; pure bottom-up has nowhere to go and gets misrepresented or co-opted. The winning shape is a strong, light spine (shared story, shared figures, shared protocol) with maximum local ownership hanging off it.

**The CONTAINED play.** CONTAINED's spine already exists and is unusually disciplined: the locked figures (§7), the consent protocol, the Imagination Architect voice, the three-room method, the platform stack (CONTAINED → JusticeHub → Empathy Ledger → Australian Living Map of Alternatives). That spine is what lets a local host run a city without the message drifting. The coalition is the 34 SA orgs plus the committed seven plus JRI; the Indigenous-led and community-controlled orgs are not features on a wall, they are the coalition's moral centre and must be routed through named relationships, never a cold inbox. "For them, not about them" is the coalition discipline.

**Concrete:** treat the spine as a deliverable, not an accident. The next-city kit is the spine handed to a host: figures, consent protocol, voice rules, the three-room method, the ladder. Treat the Indigenous-led orgs as the coalition's centre of gravity, which the brand rules already require.

**Failure mode to avoid.** Two opposite failures. A spine so tight it leaves no local ownership produces a tour that feels imposed, not hosted. A spine so loose that figures drift, consent slips, or a young person's story is used without their terms destroys the trust the whole thing runs on. CONTAINED's locked figures and consent gates are the spine doing its job; the rule is hold the spine on facts and protocol, hand everything else to the local host.

## The five highest-leverage transferable plays

Ranked by how much organised, self-propagating power they add per unit of effort in the ten days to Adelaide and across the national tour.

**1. Order every personal touch as self / us / now, with the walk-through as rung one.** (Ganz.) Rebuild the funder-fourteen invite and the inbound-fourteen reply on the spine: Ben's why, then "you belong in this we," then "doors 23 June, here is your thirty minutes and the one question to carry in." Lead with a person, never the despair figure; the SA cost frame ($3,261/day SA, ROGS 2024-25) lives once, inside the "now." This is the lowest-effort, highest-return change to assets that already exist.

**2. Build "bring one person" into every booking, and crowdsource the story on consent.** (Marriage Equality.) The walk-through is a better kitchen table than the kitchen table. One line on every invite and confirmation turns each witness into a messenger at peak intent. Exit reflections, on consent through Empathy Ledger, become the renewable story supply for the national tour. This is the core conversion mechanic the winning Australian campaign actually ran.

**3. Name the snowflake: turn the 78 into a first ring of team-leaders, each city into a hub.** (Sunrise + Bernie.) Stop broadcasting to the 78; pick the first ring (the seven committed plus the hottest inbound) and give each a single carry-job tied to their world, the way Hannah March unblocks a whole conference through one relationship. Build the Adelaide hub-kit as you go so the tour is a repeatable structure, not a sequence of founder heroics. This is the difference between a campaign and a movement.

**4. Close the nominate loop nightly, and make the ask forwardable.** (Bernie / relational.) Run the nominee bridge every night through launch week so the headline mechanic reaches a real human carrying the nominator's warrant. Hand the 78 a one-paragraph forwardable invite with their own name on it, so they make the ask into their own networks. A logged action that goes nowhere kills trust; a relational ask carried by a friend is the most persuasive move CONTAINED owns.

**5. Map each of the 78 to a rung, offer only the next one, never skip.** (Ladder of engagement.) Inbound fourteen: witness + bring one. Funder fourteen: witness then fund the next city. SA orgs thirty-four: host the wall + be the local messenger. Committed seven: recruit one team-leader each. No money ask before a walk-through or a real human exchange; witness first, always. The activation-ladder email automates the next rung for every visitor within 24 hours so nobody is left standing on a rung with no step above it.
