# Research — Criminal-justice & youth-justice reform campaigns that won

> What the greatest justice-reform campaigns did, and the concrete play CONTAINED runs because of it.
> Compiled 2026-06-13. READ-ONLY planning artefact. Nothing here was executed against GHL / Supabase / Vercel.
> Every CRM move below is documented for Ben to action by hand. None has been performed. The campaign population is the 78 contacts in the CONTAINED Engagement pipeline (`roster.json`), never a wider `project:contained` tag query.

## 0. Why study these, and the lens we are reading them through

CONTAINED is not selling tickets. It is a movement with a physical front door: one shipping container, three rooms, thirty minutes, in public space on Kaurna Yarta (Adelaide) on Tuesday 23 June 2026, then a national tour. The container moves people emotionally. JusticeHub carries the evidence. The escalation ladder moves each person one rung: witness → nominate a decision-maker → stand with it → host it in your city.

So we read every campaign below for four things:
1. **Coalition structure** — who carried the message, and who was kept at the centre.
2. **The evidence-to-emotion bridge** — how a settled fact became a felt thing, then a demand.
3. **Decision-maker pressure mechanics** — how a named human with power got moved.
4. **Safe storytelling of named young people** — how real lives were used without extracting from them.

The campaigns: Equal Justice Initiative (Bryan Stevenson — the Legacy Museum, the National Memorial for Peace and Justice, the Community Remembrance Project); the US "Raise the Age" wave and the Campaign for Youth Justice; #cut50 / Dream Corps and the First Step Act; the Missouri Model advocacy carried by the Annie E. Casey Foundation; the Australian Raise the Age coalition; and Change the Record / Maranguka justice reinvestment in Bourke. Plus the failures: Kony 2012, and the Ban the Box backfire.

## 1. Equal Justice Initiative — proximity as the whole strategy

**What happened.** Bryan Stevenson built two physical places in Montgomery, Alabama: the Legacy Museum (inside a building that once warehoused enslaved people) and the National Memorial for Peace and Justice. Both opened in 2018. They now draw over half a million visitors a year. Stevenson's stated thesis is one sentence: *"Proximity is the key to our capacity to make a difference."* The museum does not argue you into a position. It stands you on the site, tells you what the site was, and lets the floor under your feet do the work. EJI then extended the building outward through the Community Remembrance Project: since 2015, local volunteers across the US have collected soil from roughly 700 lynching sites into glass jars etched with each victim's name, and installed more than 80 historical markers. One jar stays local; a matching jar travels to the Montgomery museum and sits in a wall of hundreds.

**Why it worked.**
- **Proximity beats persuasion.** A person who has stood inside the space arrives at the policy conversation already changed. You are not starting from zero; you are starting from a body memory.
- **The ritual is participatory, not spectated.** Soil is scooped slowly while a litany asks attendees to "say his name." The visitor is not an audience. They do a thing with their hands. That act binds them.
- **Local jar + Montgomery jar = a federated movement with one heart.** Each community keeps its own grief and also contributes to a single national wall. The structure scales without diluting, because the centre holds the story while the edges multiply.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED.**
- The container IS the Legacy Museum move, at 1:400th the cost. The single highest-leverage thing CONTAINED already does right is proximity: phone and shoes at the door, alone, thirty minutes, nobody filmed. Protect that fiercely. Do not let the launch turn the container into a backdrop for a panel. The walkthrough is the argument.
- **Run the "soil jar" mechanic as a Room 3 artefact.** In Room 3 (the SA organisations already doing the work), give every visitor one physical token they fill or place with their own hands before they leave: a card they write one name on (the young person, or the decision-maker, they are walking out to act for), dropped into a clear vessel that stays at the container and grows visibly across the four days. One identical token travels with the tour to the next city's Room 3. Adelaide's vessel and Perth's vessel are the same movement. This is the federated-jar structure: local grief held locally, one wall growing nationally.
- For the 7 **Partner/committed** (Diagrama team, Hannah March, Nicole Mekler) and the 34 **Personal outreach** SA orgs, the ask is never "promote our event." It is "who needs to *stand inside* this before their next decision?" Proximity is the product. Their job is to walk a specific human through the door.

## 2. The US "Raise the Age" wave + Campaign for Youth Justice — the parent at the front

**What happened.** The Campaign for Youth Justice was founded in 2005 by a parent whose own son was transferred into the adult criminal system. It became the national clearing house for removing under-18s from adult courts. Over the campaign's life the number of US states that automatically prosecute 16- and 17-year-olds as adults was cut from 14 to 7. CFYJ's operating principle: *the people most impacted by the law must lead the campaign to change it* — directly-impacted families are not case studies, they are spokespeople.

**Why it worked.**
- **The messenger was the mother, not the think tank.** A policy paper says recidivism. A parent says "my son." The same fact lands in a different organ of the body. Legislators who could dismiss an NGO could not dismiss a constituent parent in the room.
- **One concrete, legible ask.** Not "reform youth justice." A single number: raise the age of adult-court jurisdiction. A bill a legislator could actually vote yes on. Movements that ask for everything get nothing on the statute book.
- **A clearing house.** CFYJ did not run every state campaign. It armed state coalitions with shared evidence, framing, and the family-led model, then let each state win its own fight.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED.**
- **JusticeHub is the clearing house.** The campaign-bible framing is already right: CONTAINED is the emotional front door, JusticeHub is the public evidence layer that every host city plugs into. The tour structure (Adelaide first, then Perth, Mparntwe, Brisbane, Sydney, Canberra, Melbourne, Hobart, Northern Rivers) is the CFYJ "arm each state coalition" model. Each city wins its own version; the centre holds the evidence and the standard. Point hosts to `justicehub.com.au/contained/brief` rather than re-arguing the numbers city by city.
- **Put the directly-impacted person at the front, on their terms, never extracted.** This is where CONTAINED must be more careful than CFYJ ever had to be, because the lives here are Aboriginal children and the rule (campaign-bible §9 gates) is absolute: no name, image, or story without confirmed consent on their terms; young people only ever via their organisations. So the "parent at the front" play becomes: the community organisation, the Elder, the youth worker who *holds* the story carries it. Room 3 already does this structurally by featuring the local org. The messenger is the org, not the child.
- **One legible ask per audience, already mapped.** Courts/MPs: book one private slot before your next decision about a young person. Funders: a 20-minute conversation about the next city. Services: get your org on the wall, with approval. Keep these single and concrete. The 23 **Identified** funder/target contacts each need exactly one rung-specific ask, not a brochure.

## 3. #cut50 / Dream Corps — humanisation plus a bill, across the aisle

**What happened.** Van Jones, Jessica Jackson and Matt Haney founded #cut50 in 2015. In 2018 they led the winning campaign for the federal First Step Act, which the New York Times called the most substantial criminal-justice breakthrough in a generation. Their method had two named halves: **legislation and humanisation.** Every campaign was led by people directly impacted by the system, and they deliberately built a bipartisan coalition, putting conservative and progressive champions on the same stage.

**Why it worked.**
- **Humanisation made the legislation thinkable; the legislation gave the humanisation somewhere to go.** Emotion without a bill is catharsis. A bill without emotion is a committee. #cut50 always carried both at once, in that order: feel, then act.
- **Bipartisan by design.** They refused to let the issue calcify into left-versus-right. They found the conservative case (cost, redemption, family) and the progressive case (racial justice, dignity) and let each champion speak to their own people. That doubled the coalition and de-risked it from any single election.
- **Directly-impacted people held the mic.** Not advocates speaking for. People speaking as.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED.**
- **CONTAINED already has the two halves: the container humanises, JusticeHub legislates.** Make the order explicit at every touch. The walkthrough first (feel), then the JusticeHub brief and the nominate-your-decision-maker mechanic (act). Never end a person's journey inside Room 1's detention reality; always arrive at Room 3 and the action. "Never start and end inside detention — always arrive at the alternative" is the campaign-bible rule and it is the #cut50 sequence exactly.
- **Build the bipartisan room physically.** The container is politically disarming because it asks nothing of you inside and nobody films you. That is the asset that lets a conservative MP and a community organiser both walk through without losing face. The Adelaide list already spans government (Lewina Schrale, DECYP Tasmania), philanthropy (Paul Ramsay Foundation, Minderoo, Dusseldorp, Tim Fairfax), and community-controlled orgs. Sequence private walkthroughs so a decision-maker who would never attend a "rally" will attend a thirty-minute private experience. The privacy IS the bipartisan invitation.
- **Find the cost frame for the fiscal conservative.** The settled figures do the cross-aisle work: $1.33M national / $3,261 a day in SA to detain one child (Productivity Commission ROGS 2024-25), against community alternatives at a fraction. The fiscal-conservative case and the dignity case are the same container. Lead the funder/government segment with the cost legibility; lead the community segment with dignity. Same walkthrough, two doors in.

## 4. The Missouri Model — the existence proof that travels

**What happened.** Missouri closed its youth training schools roughly three decades ago and rebuilt around small group homes, relationships, and direct supervision by trained youth specialists. The result: recidivism around 9% within three years, no in-facility suicide since the change, and billions saved by not building prisons the state no longer needed. The Annie E. Casey Foundation made the Missouri Model the spine of a national "close youth prisons" advocacy push: a working, costed, real-world existence proof that the better thing is not theoretical.

**Why it worked.**
- **It is a *proof*, not a *proposal*.** The hardest objection in justice reform is "that's naive, it can't work safely." Missouri answers with a place that already runs. You cannot argue a building out of existence.
- **It paired the proof with reinvestment maths.** Casey's framework is explicit: close the prison, reinvest the savings into community alternatives. The money is not lost, it moves. That reframes "soft on crime" into "smart with public money."
- **But "widely cited and seldom replicated."** Casey's own honest finding: a perfect proof does not replicate itself. Replication needs a committed local champion and hands-on support, not just admiration. This is a failure-mode warning as much as a win.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED.**
- **Room 2 IS the existence proof. Make it Australia's Missouri.** Room 2 is built on the visit to Diagrama's youth justice centres in Spain: education, therapy, connection instead of concrete, 13.6% reoffending, a social return cited at €5.64 per €1. That is CONTAINED's Missouri Model. Note the gate: Diagrama numbers sit behind a partner wording-approval gate for Adelaide. Until cleared, lead Room 2 with the qualitative ("education, therapy, connection instead of concrete") and hold the percentages. The proof can be felt before it is quantified.
- **Always pair the proof with the reinvestment line.** $3,261 a day to contain one SA child versus community alternatives. The money is not lost, it moves. This is the Casey reinvestment frame and it directly mirrors the Australian justice-reinvestment story below (Bourke). For the funder segment, this is the whole argument.
- **Heed the replication warning: do not ship a proof without a local champion.** "Seldom replicated" is the trap. CONTAINED's defence is structural and already in the roster: Room 3 changes every city to feature local orgs, and the tour only opens where there is a host who can hold it ("if we cannot host it, we do not open it"). Every future-city contact (Baressa Frazer / Cape York, Michael Haji-Ali / Perth, Rhian Miller / Brisbane, Katherine Hayes & Shannon Cant / YAC Queensland) is a potential local champion. Do not tour to a city without one. The Missouri lesson is that the proof alone is not enough; the named local host is the mechanism.

## 5. Australian Raise the Age — the coalition breadth and the trusted messenger

**What happened.** Launched in 2020, the Australian Raise the Age coalition grew to over 100 organisations across every state and territory: National Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Legal Services, Change the Record, Human Rights Law Centre, Amnesty International Australia, the Law Council, and a bloc of 32 health and medical organisations including the Australian Medical Association and the Royal Australasian College of Physicians. The medical bloc carried a specific argument: children's brains are not fully developed, many have already survived trauma and disability, and incarceration further damages them. Wins so far: ACT raised the age to 14 (1 July 2025), the Northern Territory was first to move 10→12 (August 2023), Tasmania committed, Victoria moved. Losses: NSW unmoved; Queensland's government reversed its raise in 2024 and returned the age to 10.

**Why it worked (and where it stalled).**
- **Breadth across sectors made the position un-fringe-able.** When doctors, lawyers, Aboriginal legal services and human-rights bodies all say the same thing, "tough on crime" politicians cannot paint it as activist noise. The medical voice in particular moved it from a values argument to a child-development fact.
- **It localised the win.** Each jurisdiction was a separate fight with a separate champion. The federated structure let ACT and NT win even while NSW stalled.
- **The reversal in Queensland is the cautionary half.** A policy win that rides only on the government of the day is reversible the moment the politics turn. Norm change is more durable than a statute that the next election can unwind. CONTAINED's job is norm change, the thing under the statute.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED.**
- **CONTAINED is the emotional engine the Raise the Age coalition never had.** That coalition has the breadth, the evidence, the open letters. What it lacks is a felt thing the public can stand inside. CONTAINED supplies the proximity; the coalition supplies the policy demand. The roster already touches this world: Hannah March (Justice Reform Initiative), Nicole Mekler (Just Reinvest). Position CONTAINED to *feed* the existing coalition, not to compete with it. Walk coalition members through the container so they can point their own networks at a visceral experience instead of a PDF.
- **Recruit the trusted-messenger blocs the same way.** The single most transferable tactic here is the 32-organisation medical bloc: the trusted profession says the unarguable thing. For CONTAINED in SA, the equivalent trusted messengers are Aboriginal-controlled orgs (Aboriginal Legal Rights Movement / Chris Larkin, KWY / Craig Rigney, Nunkuwarrin Yunti / Vicki Holmes, Aboriginal Family Support Services / Sharron Williams, SAACCON) and the research/clinical voices (Rohan Lulham / Sydney, Hayley Passmore / UWA). When they walk through and then say "this is real," the message stops being CONTAINED's and becomes theirs. That is the 34 Personal-outreach SA orgs' true role: not promoters, trusted witnesses.
- **Build for norm change, not just a policy moment.** The Queensland reversal is the warning. Do not let CONTAINED's success be measured only in a bookable-MP count. Measure it in how many people walked out and changed how they talk at their own dinner tables, in their own boardrooms. The escalation ladder (witness → nominate → stand → host) is itself the durability mechanism: a person who hosts is harder to un-convince than a person who merely attended.

## 6. Change the Record / Maranguka (Bourke) — Aboriginal authority at the centre, and the maths

**What happened.** Change the Record is the Aboriginal-led coalition for First Nations justice, steered by leading Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander, human-rights and community organisations. Its flagship proof is Maranguka in Bourke, north-west NSW. From 2013 the Bourke community, through the Bourke Tribal Council, partnered with Just Reinvest NSW to build Australia's justice-reinvestment proof of concept. Maranguka means "caring for others" in Ngemba language. A KPMG analysis found it saving government over $3 million by reducing justice-system contact, including a 72% reduction in under-25s arrested for driving without a licence. The structure that made it work: the Bourke Tribal Council asserting cultural authority across the whole system, not a program delivered *to* the community.

**Why it worked.**
- **Community held the authority, not just the participation.** Maranguka is Indigenous self-governance, guided by the Tribal Council. The community was not consulted; it decided. That is the difference between a program and a movement.
- **The maths gave decision-makers cover.** $3 million saved, 72% fewer arrests in one concrete category. A premier can defend reinvestment with a KPMG number in a way they cannot defend a feeling. The emotion opens the door; the maths lets them walk through it publicly.
- **It is the Australian Missouri / Casey reinvestment story, told on Country.** Close the harm, move the money to community, measure the saving.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED.**
- **Indigenous authority at the centre is already CONTAINED's spine — keep it structural, not decorative.** Room 3 in Adelaide is a wall of SA organisations, weighted to Aboriginal-controlled orgs. Room 1 cells are "designed by young people at each stop." The campaign-bible rule is that community orgs are heroes and young people are co-authors, never beneficiaries. This is the Maranguka principle: authority, not participation. The greeter-candidate orgs in the roster (KWY / Craig Rigney, Tjindu Foundation / Kellie Graves, Iwiri, MYSA / Helena de Anstiss, Youth Inc., Youth Options) are the people who should hold the door, set the consent terms, and shape Room 3. Document them as Room 3 co-authors, not as a vendor list.
- **Carry the reinvestment maths into the funder and government conversation.** Maranguka's $3M-saved / 72%-fewer-arrests is the proof that the money moves rather than vanishes. Pair it with the SA cost figure ($3,261/day) for the 23 Identified funder/target contacts. The structure to say out loud: *money flows inward, evidence flows outward, story stays sovereign at the centre.*
- **Let the proof be Australian and on Country.** CONTAINED does not have to import the whole argument from Spain or Missouri. Bourke is the home-grown existence proof. Room 2's Diagrama and the Maranguka story are the same shape: the better thing already runs. Where the Diagrama numbers are gated, the Bourke numbers (public, KPMG-sourced) can carry the reinvestment point without waiting on partner sign-off.

## 7. Failure modes to avoid (and how CONTAINED is already built against them)

**A. Kony 2012 — viral reach with no rung to climb, and a fragile single hero.**
100 million views in six days; Kony still free in 2013. It collapsed because (1) the ask was vague and far away (awareness, not a local action a viewer could take), (2) it oversimplified to the point that experts disowned it, (3) it rode one charismatic individual who then imploded publicly, and (4) virality burned out faster than it could be converted. *CONTAINED's defence:* the escalation ladder gives every witness a next rung that is local and concrete (nominate someone you know, host it in your city). The campaign is structural (rings, anchors, layers) not personality-dependent. And the honest population is 78 real relationships in the pipeline, not a vanity reach number. **The active risk:** treating the Day-1 LinkedIn return-post reach as the win. Reach is the top of the ladder, not the prize. Measure rung-movement (witness → nominate → host), not impressions. Never widen the campaign to the polluted ~272 `project:contained` tag query chasing scale; the 78 are the real movement.

**B. Ban the Box — the well-meant intervention that backfired through a mechanism nobody modelled.**
Removing the criminal-record checkbox to help ex-offenders led, in audit studies, to employers statistically discriminating instead: the Black/white callback gap ballooned from 7% to 45% after adoption, and employment for young low-skilled Black men *fell*. The lesson is not "don't reform." It is *model the second-order effect on the very people you mean to help, and measure it.* *CONTAINED's defence:* the named consent gates are exactly this discipline — no young person's name, image, story, or cell design without confirmed consent on their terms; young people only via their orgs. **The active risk:** a Room 1 detention recreation so visceral it re-traumatises the young people who built it, or a media moment that exposes a child the campaign meant to protect. Watch the second-order effect on system-impacted young people at every step. The "nobody filmed inside" rule is a Ban-the-Box-grade safeguard; hold it.

**C. The Queensland reversal — a win that rode the government of the day.**
Covered above. Statute without norm change is reversible at the next election. Build the norm under the law.

**D. Missouri's "seldom replicated" — a perfect proof does not travel without a local champion.**
Covered above. Never tour to a city without a named host who can hold it.

## 8. The 3–5 highest-leverage transferable plays

1. **Proximity is the product (EJI).** Protect the thirty-minute solo walkthrough like the asset it is. Never let the launch convert the container into a panel backdrop. The person who stood inside arrives at the policy conversation already changed. Every ask to the 78 is "who needs to *stand inside* this before their next decision?", not "promote our event."

2. **Feel, then act — never end inside the cell (#cut50 + the escalation ladder).** Sequence every journey: container humanises → JusticeHub legislates → nominate / stand / host. Always arrive at Room 3 and the action. The walkthrough without a rung is Kony 2012; the rung without the walkthrough is a committee. Carry both, in that order.

3. **The org is the messenger, the young person is never extracted (CFYJ + Raise the Age + Maranguka).** Put directly-impacted authority at the front *through* the community orgs that hold the story. The 34 SA Personal-outreach orgs are trusted witnesses, not promoters: when Aboriginal Legal Rights Movement, KWY, Nunkuwarrin Yunti walk through and say "this is real," the message becomes theirs. Indigenous authority at the centre is structural (Room 3 co-authorship), not decorative.

4. **Carry the existence proof plus the reinvestment maths (Missouri/Casey + Bourke + Diagrama).** Lead the funder/government segment with the proof that the better thing already runs (Room 2 / Diagrama, Maranguka / Bourke) and the maths that the money moves rather than vanishes ($3,261/day SA; Maranguka's $3M saved, 72% fewer arrests). Prefer linking `justicehub.com.au/contained/brief` over quoting numbers; hold gated Diagrama percentages until partner sign-off.

5. **Build the federated movement: one heart, many cities, a named host in each (EJI soil jars + CFYJ clearing house + Missouri replication warning).** JusticeHub is the clearing house that holds the evidence and the standard; each tour city wins its own fight with its own local champion. Run the soil-jar mechanic in Room 3 — a hand-made token each visitor leaves, one identical token travelling to the next city — so Adelaide and Perth are visibly the same movement. Never open a city without a host who can hold it.
