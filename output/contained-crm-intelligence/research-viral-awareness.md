# Research — Viral awareness & participation campaigns (and how they failed)

> What the great mass-participation campaigns actually did, the share-loop that made them spread, the failure that made some of them collapse, and the concrete play CONTAINED runs because of each one.
> Compiled 2026-06-13. READ-ONLY planning artefact. Nothing here was executed against GHL / Supabase / Vercel. Every CRM move below is documented for Ben to action by hand, none has been performed. The campaign population is the 78 contacts in the CONTAINED Engagement pipeline (`roster.json`), never a wider `project:contained` tag query.

## 0. Why study these, and the lens we are reading them through

CONTAINED has a nominate-a-decision-maker mechanic and generated share tiles. That is the same family of machinery that powered the biggest social-good campaigns of the last fifteen years. So we read each one for the parts CONTAINED can transplant, and the parts that killed them.

Four questions per campaign:
1. **The participation mechanic** — what, exactly, did a person DO with their hands or their phone.
2. **The share-loop** — how one participant manufactured the next one.
3. **The nomination/tagging dynamic** — how a named person got pulled in by another named person (CONTAINED's exact mechanic).
4. **The failure mode** — what oversimplified, what extracted, what collapsed when the spike ended.

CONTAINED is not a 24-hour stunt. It is a physical container, three rooms, thirty minutes, on Kaurna Yarta (Adelaide), Tuesday 23 June 2026, then a national tour: Perth, Mparntwe, Brisbane, Sydney, Canberra, Melbourne, Hobart, Northern Rivers. The escalation ladder moves each person one rung: witness → nominate a decision-maker → stand with it → host it in your city. Every play below is built to feed that ladder, and to protect the thing that makes CONTAINED different from a hashtag: you have to stand inside it.

The campaigns studied: the ALS Ice Bucket Challenge, Movember, It Gets Better, charity:water's "donate your birthday", Dumb Ways to Die, and the cautionary collapse of Kony 2012.

## 1. ALS Ice Bucket Challenge — the nomination chain, and the trap after the spike

**What happened.** Mid-2014. You film yourself pouring ice water over your head, post it with `#IceBucketChallenge`, and publicly name two or three specific people who now have 24 hours to do the same or donate to ALS. It raised roughly **$115 million for the ALS Association in six weeks** (over $220 million across ALS organisations globally). Mark Zuckerberg, Bill Gates, Oprah and LeBron James all did it on camera. Between 1 June and 13 August, people shared more than **1.2 million videos on Facebook**.

**The participation mechanic.** A tiny, physical, filmable act with a built-in 24-hour deadline. Cheap, silly, repeatable, and visually unmistakable as a member of the same movement.

**The share-loop.** The act WAS the share. There was no second step. You did the thing on camera; the video was the recruitment poster.

**The nomination/tagging dynamic — read this twice, it is CONTAINED's mechanic.** The genius was not the ice. It was that you could not complete the challenge without **publicly naming specific people**. A nomination from your actual friend, with your name attached, carried social weight a brand ask never could. Each completion manufactured two to three new obligated participants. That is exponential by construction, and the obligation was interpersonal, not institutional.

**The failure mode — the one CONTAINED must design against.** Three traps.
- **Slacktivism gap.** One analysis found only **one in five videos even mentioned the disease**. People performed belonging without absorbing the cause. Between June and August, 1.2M videos produced donations from only about **107,000 new donors** — most challengers never gave.
- **One-time donors.** Average gift was about $40, mostly first-timers who never came back. A spike is not a base. The ALS Association had to work for years to convert a viral moment into durable funding.
- **The mechanic outran the meaning.** The water was so fun it detached from ALS entirely; for many it became a generic dare.

**Why it ultimately mattered anyway — the durable-structure lesson.** The reason Ice Bucket is remembered as a win and not a fad is what the money was wired into: a **biorepository (Project MinE)** that existed before the spike and could absorb the cash. That funding helped discover the **NEK1 gene** (one of five new ALS genes validated since), and **$2.2M of it funded the trial behind Relyvrio**, an FDA-approved drug. The campaign worked because the spike landed on a structure that could turn money into outcomes. Kony 2012 (section 6) had no such structure, and that is the whole difference.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED.**
- **CONTAINED's nominate-a-decision-maker mechanic IS the ice-bucket nomination, pointed at power instead of friends.** Ice Bucket named your mates; CONTAINED names the magistrate, the minister, the CEO, the funder who decides a young person's path. Keep the interpersonal warrant exactly as Ice Bucket had it: the receipt and share tile must say *a named person who respects you asked us to make sure you walked through this*, not *the campaign invites you*. That warrant is the whole engine.
- **Design AGAINST the slacktivism gap from day one.** Ice Bucket's failure was performance-without-comprehension. CONTAINED's defence is structural: you cannot "do" CONTAINED from your couch. The act of participation is physically standing in the container for thirty minutes. The share tile is the *receipt* of an embodied act, not a substitute for it. Never let the tile become the whole transaction. The tile should always point one rung up the ladder (witness → nominate → stand → host), never close the loop.
- **Wire the spike into a structure before you chase the spike.** The ALS lesson is the biorepository. CONTAINED's biorepository is **JusticeHub** (the evidence layer) plus the **Adelaide-then-tour shape**. Before driving any volume, confirm the thing that catches it exists: a working EOI/booking path, a nominee follow-up that does not silently fail, a "bring it to my city" capture. Right now Pipeline B (the Adelaide booking funnel) holds **zero real demand** and the Future-city stage has **no capture asset** (per the segment matrix). Fix the catcher before you open the tap, or CONTAINED gets the 1.2M-videos / 107K-donors gap.
- **Convert the one-timers, the explicit weakness Ice Bucket never solved.** The escalation ladder is precisely the anti-one-time-donor instrument. Every person who witnesses gets one named next rung within 48 hours (nominate someone), not a generic "stay in touch." For the **14 inbound "In conversation" leads** (Row A4: Anita Pahor, Katherine Hayes, Rohan Lulham, Romina Reyftmann and the rest), the personal reply should already name their rung, so they never become a $40-and-gone.

## 2. Movember — the ritual that turns a body into a billboard, every single year

**What happened.** Every November, men ("Mo Bros") start clean-shaven and grow a moustache for the month; women and non-growers ("Mo Sisters") walk/run 60 miles or host a "Mo-ment". It raised **$137.2M globally in FY2024** and funds 1,250+ men's-health projects. It has run annually since 2003 and become a recognised season.

**The participation mechanic.** Your own face becomes a slow-release, unavoidable conversation-starter for thirty days. The awkward stubble *is* the ask: every colleague who says "what's with the mo?" triggers the men's-health conversation. The cost of participation is social discomfort, which is the point, because the discomfort is the message (men don't talk about their health).

**The share-loop.** Peer-to-peer fundraising pages ("Mo Space") plus the physical face. The moustache is a thirty-day billboard you can't take off, so the loop runs in real life, not just online.

**The nomination/tagging dynamic.** Softer than Ice Bucket: it's recruitment-by-visible-belonging plus team formation (workplaces, mates form teams and pull each other in). The driver is the annual ritual and the peer team, not a 24-hour tag.

**Why it sustained where Ice Bucket spiked.** Three structural choices. (1) **A fixed calendar slot** people anticipate and re-enter yearly. (2) **A wide on-ramp**: grow, move, host, or "Mo Your Own Way", so non-moustache-growers aren't excluded. (3) **An owner**: a real organisation that funds projects all year, so the November spike feeds a permanent body.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED.**
- **Make CONTAINED a recurring marker, not a one-off launch.** Adelaide on 23 June is the first instance of something that should return. The national tour is already this shape; make the *repeat* explicit and dated so funders and partners can anticipate "the next one". Movember's strength is that you can predict it. CONTAINED's tour stops should be announced as a season, not a surprise, so each city's organisers can prepare their Room 3 wall in advance.
- **Build the wide on-ramp Movember has and Ice Bucket lacked.** Not everyone can host a container. The ladder already does this (witness / nominate / stand / host) but make each rung a real, low-friction "you can do this from where you are" action. The person who can't get to Adelaide can still nominate a decision-maker and share the tile. Don't let the container's scarcity exclude the willing.
- **The moustache lesson for the container: leave people carrying a visible marker out the door.** Movember works because the participant becomes the medium for a month. CONTAINED's equivalent: every walk-through leaves with one physical token (a card with one name on it, the decision-maker they're walking out to move) that prompts the "what's that?" conversation. The visitor becomes the billboard, exactly as the moustache does.

## 3. It Gets Better — the user-generated story archive, and where it plateaus

**What happened.** 2010. Dan Savage and Terry Miller posted one video telling bullied LGBTQ+ youth "it gets better". The format was so simple it became a template: **50,000+ videos, 50M+ collective views, 70,000+ people sharing their own stories**. It grew into a registered project with toolkits, youth leadership programs and grants.

**The participation mechanic.** Record yourself telling your own story to camera, in the same simple frame. The contribution is your testimony. Anyone with a webcam and a story qualifies, no skill, no money.

**The share-loop.** Each video is both a contribution and an invitation: "I did mine, here's the format, do yours." The replicable template is the loop, the form is so plain that watching one tells you exactly how to make yours.

**The nomination/tagging dynamic.** Weaker than Ice Bucket, there is no forced naming. It spread by identification ("that's me, I should add mine") rather than obligation. That made the spread slower but the contributions far more meaningful, each one was a real story, not a dare.

**The failure mode / ceiling.** Critiques from within LGBTQ communities flagged that the message risked being passive ("just wait, it'll improve") rather than demanding structural change, and that it could flatten very different lives into one comforting frame. The lesson: **a story archive that only consoles, without converting to a demand, plateaus.** Inspiration is not yet pressure.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED.**
- **CONTAINED's Room 3 IS an It Gets Better archive, made physical and pointed at a demand.** Room 3 is the SA organisations already doing the work that works, the proof that the cure exists. Treat the wall as a living archive of real practitioners, each with consent, each a "this already works here" story. But unlike It Gets Better, every story on the wall must end in a demand, not consolation: *here is what works, here is the decision-maker who could fund it, walk out and name them.*
- **Replicable template, not bespoke production.** It Gets Better scaled because the format was copy-able. CONTAINED's Room 3 wall should be a template each tour city fills with its own local orgs, so Perth's wall, Mparntwe's wall, Brisbane's wall are visibly the same form holding different local truth. Federated, like Movember teams, with one frame.
- **Avoid the consolation plateau.** Do not let CONTAINED become "look how sad this is, but people are doing good work, isn't that nice." Every room must turn the felt thing into a named next action. The container moves you; the ladder spends that movement. If a visitor leaves only moved and not assigned a rung, the campaign just consoled them, which is the It Gets Better ceiling.

## 4. charity:water "donate your birthday" — proof-of-impact as the engine of return

**What happened.** Scott Harrison's model: instead of presents, ask people to donate their birthday (raise $X, where roughly $40 funds clean water for one person). About **17,000 people have donated their birthday, raising nearly $9M through birthdays alone.** Two structural moves made it durable: the **100% model** (private donors cover operating costs so every public dollar goes to the field) and **proof of impact** (donors get back **GPS coordinates and photos** of the specific well their money built, with the line "You did this").

**The participation mechanic.** Reframe an existing personal occasion (your birthday) into a fundraising trigger. Zero new behaviour invented, an event you already have is repurposed. Very low friction.

**The share-loop.** Your birthday is already a moment you broadcast to your whole network. The campaign hijacks an existing high-attention personal event. Everyone you'd have invited to the party becomes a prospect, on a day they already expect to hear from you.

**The proof loop, this is the part CONTAINED needs most.** charity:water solved the trust gap that sinks most giving: *where did my money actually go?* By sending back GPS + photos of a real, located, dated outcome, they closed the loop and manufactured repeat givers. "You did this" converts a donor into a believer. This is the durable-structure answer to the Ice Bucket one-time-donor problem.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED.**
- **Close the loop with proof, or the spike dies like Ice Bucket's.** When a CONTAINED supporter nominates a decision-maker and that decision-maker actually walks through, the nominator should hear about it: *the person you named stood inside it on [date], here is what they saw.* That is CONTAINED's "You did this". It converts a one-time nominator into someone who nominates again. Right now the nominee never becomes a tracked record (per the segment matrix, Row C2), so this loop is broken, document the fix: a lightweight nominee-tracking row so the nominator can be told the outcome.
- **The container produces charity:water's photo automatically — use it.** Every walk-through is a located, dated, real event. The proof-of-impact asset writes itself: a real photo of a real person standing where a young person's future gets decided. (Real photos only, per brand rule, the container in public space, built by young people, is the image.) Send proof back to the people who made it happen, especially the **14 KEEP funders** (Row A1) who back the tour, "your backing put 40 decision-makers inside it in Adelaide" beats any projection deck.
- **Hijack an existing moment, the way birthdays do.** CONTAINED has a built-in high-attention moment most campaigns lack: the **Reintegration Puzzle Conference, 24-25 June**, with the container standing literally beside it. That is CONTAINED's "birthday", an event the audience already shows up to. The play is to make the container the unmissable thing next to a gathering that's already happening, not to manufacture attention from scratch (Row C1/A7, unblock via Hannah March / JRI).
- **Adopt a 100%-style transparency frame for funders.** charity:water's 100% model is really a trust device. CONTAINED's equivalent is JusticeHub's open evidence layer: every claim links to source. Lead funders to `justicehub.com.au/contained/brief` rather than quoting numbers, the verifiable structure behind the emotion is what converts a sceptical funder.

## 5. Dumb Ways to Die — entertainment as the trojan horse, ending in a pledge

**What happened.** Metro Trains Melbourne, November 2012. Facing rising unsafe behaviour by young people who tuned out fear-based safety ads, McCann made a cute, darkly funny animated song. **2.7M views in 48 hours, 28M in two weeks**, then a mobile game franchise (190M+ downloads). The campaign claims a **~21% reduction in station incidents** (one source cites ~30% fewer near-misses). It is Australian, made for a young, lecture-averse audience, the same audience youth-justice reform has to reach.

**The participation mechanic.** First it's just watching/singing/sharing a song. Then, critically, the game and site end in a **rail-safety pledge** ("I promise to be safe around trains"). The campaign deliberately did not stop at awareness, it built a path from entertainment to a small committed act.

**The share-loop.** Pure entertainment shareability, the song was so catchy people shared it for the joy, and the cause rode along. It spawned 85 parodies in two weeks, each one free reach.

**The failure mode it consciously avoided.** Fear-based, authoritarian messaging, which its young audience had already learned to ignore. Its lesson is the inverse of a failure: it refused to lecture. But note the open question raised by critics, did it change behaviour or just entertain? Metro's answer was the pledge: convert the laugh into a commitment, so the campaign has a behavioural endpoint, not just a view count.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED.**
- **The container is CONTAINED's "don't lecture" device.** Youth justice reform usually arrives as a lecture full of fear and statistics, exactly what Dumb Ways's audience tunes out. CONTAINED's answer is the same as Metro's: don't argue, let them *experience*. The walk-through is the catchy song, it gets in before the defences go up. Protect this, do not let the launch turn the container into a panel with slides.
- **Always end on the pledge.** Dumb Ways's discipline was converting the laugh into a button-press commitment. CONTAINED's pledge is the nomination: every walk-through ends with a named, small, committed act, *who needs to stand inside this before their next decision?* The container moves you; the nomination is the pledge that banks the movement. Never end on "wasn't that powerful", end on a name written down.
- **Lead with the figure as the hook the way "dumb ways" did, sparingly and shocking.** Dumb Ways made the morbid approachable. CONTAINED's equivalent shock-but-true hook is the cost: **$3,261 a day to detain one child in SA** (Productivity Commission ROGS 2024-25), against $75/day for alternatives. One stark, sourced, local number that makes someone stop, the way "dumb ways to die" made you click. Use the §7 canon figure with source, or link `justicehub.com.au/contained/brief`, never the retired numbers.

## 6. Kony 2012 — the collapse CONTAINED is built to avoid

**What happened.** March 2012. Invisible Children released a 30-minute film to make warlord Joseph Kony famous enough to be arrested. It hit roughly **100 million views in about six days**, the fastest-spreading video of its time. Within two weeks the reputation crashed. The planned offline action ("Cover the Night", 20 April) drew weak turnout. The campaign is now the textbook case of viral awareness that converted to nothing durable.

**Why it exploded, and then why it imploded.**
- **It exploded** on a clean villain, a simple ask ("make him famous"), an emotional child-centred narrative, slick production, and a tangible action kit (posters, bracelets). The participation mechanic was real: share the film, buy the action kit, plaster your city on one night.
- **It imploded** on four faults CONTAINED must treat as a checklist:
  1. **Oversimplification.** The film flattened a complex regional conflict and, critically, was **factually behind reality**, Kony and the LRA had been pushed out of Uganda years earlier. When the simplification met experts, the credibility collapsed.
  2. **White-saviour framing.** Critics named the "Africans are helpless, must be saved by young Americans" narrative as the rot at the centre. The people most affected were the *subject* of the film, not its authors or its centre.
  3. **No durable structure.** "Cover the Night" was a one-night spectacle with nothing on the other side. Awareness peaked and had nowhere to convert. There was no biorepository (cf. ALS), no archive (cf. It Gets Better), no field outcome (cf. charity:water).
  4. **Financial-transparency backlash.** Scrutiny of how Invisible Children spent money compounded the loss of trust once the narrative cracked.

**The concrete play for CONTAINED — design against every fault, by name.**
- **Against oversimplification: never claim more than the evidence holds, and link the evidence.** CONTAINED's "the cure already exists" is a strong simple line, the Kony lesson is that a simple line must sit on verifiable ground or it shatters on contact with an expert. This is exactly why JusticeHub exists underneath the container, and why the §7 figure discipline (source + year + jurisdiction, retired numbers banned) is not pedantry, it is the anti-Kony immune system. The **Diagrama figures must hold their wording gate** (no percentage public without partner sign-off, Row A5) for precisely this reason, a wrong stat from a partner is a Kony-style credibility crack.
- **Against white-saviour framing: keep the affected people at the centre, as authors not subjects.** This is the single most important thing CONTAINED does differently. The work is **Indigenous-led**; the SA Room 3 wall features **community-controlled organisations** (KWY, Tjindu, ALRM, SAACCON, Tiraapendi Wodli, Iwiri, NPY) with explicit consent (Row A3); young people are paid hosts who consent on their own terms (Row C4), not props. The container is "for them, not about them". Kony made Ugandans the backdrop to American action; CONTAINED makes the affected communities the authors and the centre. Protect this fiercely, the moment CONTAINED becomes white reformers saving Indigenous kids on camera, it has become Kony 2012. Route every community-controlled org through a named relationship, never a cold inbox blast.
- **Against no-durable-structure: the tour and JusticeHub ARE the structure Kony lacked.** Kony's "Cover the Night" had nothing behind it. CONTAINED's equivalent of Cover the Night is the launch, but behind it sits a national tour (a repeated structure, like Movember's calendar), an evidence platform (like ALS's biorepository), and an escalation ladder (a conversion path, like Dumb Ways's pledge). The lesson: **never let the spike be the whole thing.** If the Adelaide launch peaks and there is no booked next city, no captured "bring it here" leads, and no nominee follow-up, CONTAINED has built Cover the Night. The Future-city capture gap and the empty Pipeline B (segment matrix, standing hazards 4) are the live Kony risks, fix the catcher before the spike.
- **Against transparency backlash: be radically clear about money and method up front.** charity:water's 100% model and CONTAINED's open JusticeHub evidence are the same defence. Publish the cost structure ($30K/stop, $50-70K/container) plainly; lead funders to the brief, not to a number you'd have to defend later.

## 8. The 3-5 highest-leverage transferable plays

1. **Run the nomination as Ice Bucket pointed at power, with the friend-warrant intact.** CONTAINED's nominate-a-decision-maker is the exact engine that raised $115M for ALS, redirected from naming friends to naming magistrates, ministers and funders. The non-negotiable is the interpersonal warrant: the receipt and share tile must read *a named person who respects you asked us to make sure you walked through this*, never *the campaign invites you*. Documented action: in the 14 inbound "In conversation" personal replies (Row A4) and every nominee touch (Row C2), lead with the named human, not the campaign.

2. **Fix the catcher before opening the tap — the anti-Kony, anti-slacktivism move.** ALS worked because money landed on a biorepository; Kony failed because awareness landed on nothing. CONTAINED's live risk is the same: Pipeline B (Adelaide booking) holds zero real demand and the Future-city stage has no capture asset (segment matrix, hazards 4). Documented action: before driving volume, confirm the EOI/booking path works, build the nominee-tracking row, and add a "bring it to my city" capture, so the spike converts instead of evaporating.

3. **Close the proof loop, charity:water style — "You did this".** The thing that turns a one-time participant (Ice Bucket's fatal weakness) into a repeat one is hearing the outcome. Documented action: when a nominated decision-maker actually walks through, tell the nominator; when funders' backing fills the container, send them the real photo of real people inside it (real photos only). This is the single highest-leverage retention mechanic, and it is currently broken because the nominee never becomes a tracked record.

4. **Always end on the pledge, never on the feeling — Dumb Ways's discipline.** The container moves people (the catchy-song effect); the campaign fails if it stops there (the It Gets Better consolation plateau). Documented action: every walk-through, every email, every tile ends on one named next rung of the ladder, not "wasn't that powerful". A name written down beats a feeling felt.

5. **Keep the affected communities as authors, not subjects — the one rule that separates CONTAINED from Kony 2012.** Indigenous-led, community-controlled orgs consented and centred on the Room 3 wall (Row A3), young people paid and consenting as hosts (Row C4), "for them, not about them". The moment CONTAINED looks like reformers saving Indigenous kids on camera, it has become the campaign that imploded in two weeks. Documented action: route every community-controlled org through a named relationship, never a cold inbox; hold the Diagrama wording gate; lead with sourced §7 figures or the brief link, never a number you'd have to walk back.
