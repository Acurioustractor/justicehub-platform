## The Products

### 1. CivicScope — "The Earth"
**What it is:** Grant intelligence + procurement readiness + relationship mapping.

**Who pays:** Any org that applies for grants, bids on tenders, or needs to understand the funding landscape. NFPs, consultancies, social enterprises, Indigenous orgs, local councils, grant writers.

**What they get:**
- Search 672K government contracts by keyword, sector, value, location
- See who wins tenders in your space (and how much they win)
- Track 156K+ grant/funding records — who funds what, where, how much
- Relationship mapping: which orgs connect to which funders, which board members overlap
- Procurement readiness scoring: "here's what you need to be competitive for this tender"
- Political donation overlay: see if your competitors donate to the parties awarding contracts

**Revenue model:** SaaS subscription.
- **Starter** ($49/mo): Search grants, basic filters, saved searches
- **Pro** ($149/mo): Relationship mapping, tender alerts, procurement scoring
- **Enterprise** ($499/mo): API access, custom reports, political donation overlay, board network analysis

**Why it sells:** Grant writing is a $2B industry in Australia. Every NFP spends weeks finding and applying for grants. CivicScope turns weeks into minutes. The procurement angle is even bigger — $500B+ in government procurement annually, and most small orgs don't know where to start.

**Current state:** 672K contracts, 312K donations, 156K funding records, civic intelligence scrapers running daily. The data exists. Needs productisation: auth, billing, search UI, saved searches, alerts.

**Compound effect:** Every org that signs up for CivicScope becomes visible in the ecosystem. Their funding profile enriches JusticeHub. Their grant applications feed back into success rate data. Their procurement wins become case studies for the ALMA network.

### 2. JusticeHub / ALMA Network — "The Mission"
**What it is:** Community justice evidence platform + decentralised network of Basecamps.

**Who pays:**
- **Funders** pay for intelligence: portfolio analysis, evidence mapping, impact comparison, landscape reports. (Teya, Lucy, PRF are the first three.)
- **Government** pays for evidence: "show us what works in our state" reports. Senate inquiry submissions. Policy briefings backed by data.
- **Research institutions** pay for data access: 98K orgs, 1,146 programs, $120B funding, evidence ratings.
- **Community orgs** pay nothing (or pay-what-you-can). They're the network. They contribute data and stories.

**Revenue model:** Intelligence-as-a-service.
- **Funder reports** ($2K-$10K per report): Custom portfolio analysis, landscape comparison, evidence mapping
- **Government briefings** ($5K-$20K): State-level evidence reviews, inquiry submissions, policy recommendations
- **Research data licences** ($500-$2K/yr): API access to ALMA dataset for universities
- **ALMA Network membership** (free for community, $500/yr for large orgs): Access to Basecamp network, shared evidence, consortium bidding

**Current state:** 3 funder profiles configured. Regional + national pages live. 6 agents running. The product works — it needs customers.

**Compound effect:** Every funder report generates insights that improve CivicScope's grant data. Every community org in the ALMA network is a potential CivicScope customer. Every government briefing creates relationships for procurement work.

### 3. Empathy Ledger — "The Voice"
**What it is:** OCAP-compliant storytelling platform. Ethical impact media for organisations.

**Who pays:** Any org that needs to tell their impact story properly — which is every org. But specifically:
- **Indigenous organisations** who need OCAP-compliant media management (this is a legal/cultural requirement that nobody else serves)
- **NFPs** who have photos, stories, testimonials scattered across drives and phones
- **Funders** who want verified impact stories from their portfolio orgs
- **Government departments** who need community voice in their reports but can't navigate consent/sovereignty

**What they get:**
- OCAP-compliant media library (photos, video, audio, stories)
- Consent management: who gave permission, for what use, with what expiry
- Story builder: turn raw community input (voice notes, photos) into publishable impact stories
- API for embedding stories in websites, reports, grant applications
- Storyteller profiles: community members own their narrative

**Revenue model:** SaaS + setup.
- **Setup** ($2K-$5K): Import existing media, configure consent framework, train staff
- **Platform** ($99/mo per org): Media library, consent management, basic story builder
- **Pro** ($249/mo): API access, branded embeds, multi-project, advanced consent workflows
- **Enterprise** ($499/mo): White-label, custom workflows, portfolio-level reporting

**Why it sells:** OCAP compliance is becoming non-negotiable for any org working with Indigenous communities. The National Agreement on Closing the Gap requires it. Nobody has a tool for it. Empathy Ledger is the only product in market.

**Current state:** 261 photos, 12 stories, 51 storytellers, v2 API live, OCAP framework implemented. Needs: multi-tenant auth, billing, onboarding flow, self-serve setup.

**Compound effect:** Every org on EL has stories that can appear in JusticeHub regional reports. Every photo becomes available for CONTAINED campaigns. The consent framework becomes a model that CivicScope can reference for data governance.

### 4. ACT Farm / The Harvest — "The Place"
**What it is:** Black Cockatoo Valley — 150 acres, Jinibara lands. R&D residencies, workshops, June's Patch (healthcare worker wellbeing), conservation.

**Who pays:**
- **Residents** ($300-$500/night): R&D residencies for innovators, researchers, social entrepreneurs
- **Workshop participants**: Species observation, ethical AI, garden design, weed management
- **June's Patch partners**: USC, Wishlist — healthcare worker wellbeing program
- **Harvest events**: Gatherings, working bees, showcase events

**Revenue model:** Direct (accommodation + events) + indirect (relationships → consulting → deals).

**Compound effect:** The Farm is where relationships deepen. A funder who reads a JusticeHub report might visit for a residency. An Indigenous org in the ALMA network might host an on-country workshop. The Harvest events bring the ecosystem together physically. The Farm is ACT's "third place" — not an office, not a Zoom call, but shared ground.

**What it is:** White goods and bedding to remote communities.

**Revenue model:** Not a revenue product. It's the anchor. It's what makes everything else credible.

**Compound effect:** Every Goods delivery is a data point that connects to a JusticeHub org profile, a CivicScope funding trail, an Empathy Ledger story. It's the thing that makes the abstract concrete. "We track $120B and this kid needs a bed" is the line that opens every door.

## The Compound Loop

Here's how they feed each other:

```
CivicScope (sells data)
    → org signs up, finds grants
    → their funding profile enriches JusticeHub
    → JusticeHub uses it in funder reports (sells intelligence)
    → funder wants impact stories (buys EL setup)
    → org uses EL to tell their story (pays monthly)
    → story appears in regional report (more JH value)
    → org wins tender (CivicScope success metric)
    → org visits Farm for residency (direct revenue)
    → Goods delivery documented through full stack (credibility)
    → media picks it up (CONTAINED campaign)
    → more orgs sign up for CivicScope
```

**Every product makes every other product more valuable.** CivicScope is the entry point (widest market). JusticeHub is the mission (deepest impact). Empathy Ledger is the differentiator (nobody else has OCAP). The Farm is the relationship builder. The Goods Project is the credibility anchor.

2. **Cultural authority** (OCAP compliance, Indigenous relationships, community trust) — can't be bought

The defensibility isn't in any one product. It's in the compound. A competitor could build a grant search tool. They can't build the grant search tool that's connected to the evidence base that's connected to the stories that's connected to the parliamentary record that's connected to the governance network that's connected to a farm in Black Cockatoo Valley where the people behind all of it actually gather.
