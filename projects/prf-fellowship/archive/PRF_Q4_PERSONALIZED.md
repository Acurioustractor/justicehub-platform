# QUESTION 4: Why Is This Important to You? (250 words)

## Ben's Personalized Response

This fellowship emerges from a journey that began in Bolivian prisons, where I witnessed resilience in the most unlikely places. As a prison visitor, then later facilitating offending programs for 300+ participants in Queensland Corrective Services, I saw that people trapped in systems often held the clearest vision of what needed to change. Yet their knowledge rarely shaped the systems themselves.

At Orange Sky, photographing people experiencing homelessness across Australia, I learned that every person has a story worth telling. But I also learned something harder: telling stories isn't enough if power remains unchanged. At AIME, working with Indigenous communities, I encountered 60,000 years of systems thinking that modern policy continues to ignore. Communities don't need us to solve their problems—they need infrastructure to implement their own solutions.

This fellowship matters because I've spent two decades working **in** systems—youth refuges, corrections, homelessness services, youth justice—and I've seen the pattern: we fund programs that work, then dismantle them when funding ends. We commission research that extracts community knowledge, then shelve reports. We celebrate individual success stories while systems remain unchanged.

The question that drives this work is fundamental: What if communities owned the platforms that tell their stories? What if grassroots workers supporting young people could translate their wisdom into policy influence? What if the infrastructure itself—digital, physical, economic—belonged to communities?

If this fellowship succeeds, we demonstrate that the path to systems change isn't better research **about** communities, but better infrastructure **for** communities to lead change themselves. That possibility—communities as architects, not subjects—is what keeps me working toward it.

## Option C: More Focus on Infrastructure as Power

I've spent twenty years working at the intersection of community knowledge and institutional power, and I've learned a hard truth: good programs die when communities don't own the infrastructure that sustains them.

In Queensland Corrective Services, I created the "Positive Futures Program" for people leaving prison. It worked. Participants thrived. Then funding shifted, institutional priorities changed, and the program disappeared. The knowledge remained with participants and communities—but they lacked power to sustain it.

At Orange Sky, I photographed Pete, who experienced homelessness while volunteering with us. His story shifted hearts when we shared it. But Pete still experienced homelessness. Stories raised awareness, but ownership of platforms, resources, and decision-making remained with organizations, not communities.

At AIME, collaborating with Aboriginal communities, I encountered a different paradigm: 60,000 years of systems thinking where communities don't wait for permission to implement solutions. Working with Jack Manning Bancroft to build IMAGI-NATION digital platform, I saw what becomes possible when communities own infrastructure—not just stories, but the platforms that amplify them.

This fellowship asks the question I should have asked twenty years ago: Instead of building better programs that communities depend on, what if we build infrastructure communities own? What if JusticeHub and Contained aren't **my** projects serving communities, but community-owned platforms I helped build, then stepped back from?

The possibility that communities could shift systems themselves—not by convincing policymakers, but by owning the evidence infrastructure—drives everything. That's the systems change we've never funded.
