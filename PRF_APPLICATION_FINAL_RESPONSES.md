## QUESTION 6: Approach and Methodology (500 words)

This fellowship uses **participatory action research** methodology, centering communities as co-researchers investigating systems change together. The approach integrates community partnership, creative practice, digital ethnography, and systems analysis across four phases. **If needed, we will prioritize depth over breadthâ€”focusing on 3-5 communities for deeper analysis rather than maximum numbers at the expense of rigorous learning.**

**Phase 1: Establish Baseline (Months 1-3)**

Launch Contained installation in three communities targeting areas with high youth detention rates. Document visitor responses through surveys (n=100+ per site) and semi-structured interviews (n=20-30 per site); track policy-maker attendance, media coverage, and community reactions using media monitoring tools. Simultaneously, onboard ten grassroots organizations to JusticeHub platform, training communities in AI-assisted story extraction and establishing ownership/revenue-sharing frameworks.

**Critical integration measurement:** Track whether Contained visitors engage with JusticeHub stories (via QR codes, unique URLs); monitor whether digital story readers seek physical installation experience. Map the youth justice policy ecosystem: decision-makers, funders, influencers, and how evidence currently moves through this system.

**Phase 2: Deep Community Engagement (Months 4-9)**

Partner with five communities hosting Contained as co-researchers. Conduct story-gathering workshops with young people, families, and community workers affected by youth justice system. Use JusticeHub's AI (simple, established transcription and summarization tools, not experimental models) to extract themes, quotes, and case studies from interview transcripts, with the **critical ethical step** that communities review, edit, and approve all AI-extracted content before publication.

**Analytical approach:** We will use mixed-method analysis including qualitative coding of interview transcripts and stories using thematic analysis, basic descriptive statistics of engagement metrics (visitor numbers, story views, funding applications), and simple network mapping of how stories travel between community, media, and policy actors.

Document: How does AI-assisted extraction compare to traditional research in honoring lived experience? How does community editing affect AI outputs? What does "consent" mean in AI-assisted storytelling? Map how stories move through the ecosystem: community â†’ media â†’ policy. Implement revenue-sharing when stories are republished or cited, documenting whether economic participation affects community engagement, trust, and agency.

**Phase 3: Test Systems Change Hypotheses (Months 10-12)**

Communities present findings to state/federal policymakers, youth justice departments, and funding bodies. Document: How do policymakers respond to community-generated evidence versus traditional research? What evidence formats influence which decisions?

Measure hearts and minds shift through: media content analysis (coding for framing shifts from "punishment" to "support"), public discourse tracking via social media sentiment analysis, interviews with journalists and advocates. Track narrative change indicators.

Monitor resource flow: Does evidence contribute to funding shifts from detention to community programs? Do organizations using JusticeHub gain new funding opportunities? What evidence convinces funders to reallocate? (Track via funder interviews and grant outcome data.)

**Phase 4: Synthesis and Dissemination (Months 13-18)**

Analyze findings across research questions using triangulation of qualitative and quantitative data. Identify: What conditions enabled systems change? What prevented it? How did physical and digital integration work? What role did community ownership play?

Create outputs for multiple audiences:
- **Communities:** Open-source playbook enabling replication
- **Funders:** Investment guide for community knowledge infrastructure
- **Policymakers:** Evidence brief on community-led research effectiveness
- **Sector:** Methodology paper on digital platforms as sites of self-determination

**Ethical Framework:**
All research follows principles of community consent (ongoing, not one-time), economic reciprocity, knowledge sovereignty (communities own data), and dual accountability (serves community goals AND contributes to knowledge).
