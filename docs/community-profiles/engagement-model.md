# The community engagement and support model

**Date:** 2026-06-10
**Companions:** `org-profile-spec.md` (the mechanism), `output/proposals/community-justice-strategy-2026-06.md` (the strategy), `output/justice-reinvestment/jr-discourse-and-support-analysis.md` (the evidence base for every rule below)

## The two sentences this model is built on

Halls Creek, in their own words: *"The traditional judicial system has failed us and continues to let us down. We must manage this problem ourselves. The Aboriginal way."*

Maranguka, on what data is for: *"operationalising self-determination by levelling the playing field with the use of data and ensuring attributable sovereignty."*

The communities are not asking to be empowered. They are governing already, and they are asking for the playing field to be levelled. The platform's job is leverage, not permission.

## What a community gets, from day one

1. **Their profile, under their hand.** Built with them in the room, published only when they say so, sections visible only as they choose. The organisation is the editor of record.
2. **Their data, returned in their shape.** Not a government dashboard about them. Their programs, their outcomes, their funding history, exportable and reusable in their own grant applications and board reports. The reporting burden runs in reverse: the platform does the assembling, the community does the deciding.
3. **The cost case, pre-built.** Their program spend beside the ROGS detention benchmark, per young person, sourced. The argument they have been making for years, rendered in the numbers funders and ministers respond to.
4. **Stories on their terms.** Empathy Ledger consent levels per story, enforced in the database. A story can be shared for the campaign and withheld from the funder, or the reverse.
5. **Sight of each other.** The network map. Lajamanu can see what Cherbourg ran. Moree can see Halls Creek's youth engagement night patrol numbers. The support between sites that the chairperson described is a view, not a conference.

## What the platform never does

Derived from the discourse analysis (section 4 of the companion memo):

1. Never publishes community data without the community's confirmation. Unclaimed stubs say so, plainly.
2. Never renders deficit framing. Profiles lead with what the community runs and achieves, not statistics about their young people.
3. Never becomes the funder's audit surface. Funders see what communities choose to publish, nothing else. No back door.
4. Never extracts. Data goes in under consent, comes back out at the community's request, and deletion requests are honoured at the row level.
5. Never lets a government or grantee define success. The top rung of the verification ladder is community-verified: the community confirms whether it is working. That rung outranks every other signal on the platform.

## The engagement sequence

**1. Invitation through relationship.** No cold outreach. Entry is through the anchor communities, the justice reinvestment network, the trip, and George's and the network leadership's introductions. The first contact is a person, not a platform.

**2. In the room, on Country.** The trip circuit is the engagement mechanism: profile co-created with the organisation in one sitting, on their terms, offline-tolerant capture staged to the review queue. They see it before anyone else does.

**3. Org-confirmed.** The organisation reviews and publishes. This is the claim flow. From this moment they hold the pen.

**4. Community-verified.** Outcomes confirmed with the community's own evidence. The badge that means something because the community awarded it.

**5. The network meets itself.** Gatherings grow from the map: events where sites compare playbooks, hosted by basecamps. Oonchiumpa in Mparntwe is the first basecamp; spokes radiate from the trip.

## Support offering behind the sequence

- Capture tooling that works in the room (the spoke-circuit flow in the profile spec build order)
- Story support through Empathy Ledger, consent-first
- Data assembly: funding histories from the national dataset, evidence from the Australian Living Map of Alternatives, returned to the community pre-organised
- Grant intelligence: matched opportunities from the funding dataset, offered to the community rather than gatekept
- Campaign machinery when communities want it: the issue-page, follow-loop, and digest infrastructure proven on the Justice Matrix, pointed at the reform questions communities choose

## The line to George and the network leadership

The national delivery organisation tender is open now. Whoever wins it will need exactly this: a way for sites to hold their own data, see each other, and accumulate the evidence base for reform without surrendering sovereignty to a department dashboard. JusticeHub is that infrastructure, already running, with governance designed from Maiam nayri Wingara, OCAP, and CARE rather than retrofitted. The ask is alignment and a pilot, not procurement: walk the founding profiles, name the first spoke sites, and let the network test whether the platform deserves them.
