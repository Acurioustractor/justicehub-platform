# Aunty Corrine Extended Interview - Core Analysis
## Case Studies, Systems Failures, and Community-Led Model Evidence

---

## Executive Summary

This interview provides **extraordinary documentation** of:

1. **25+ young people** Aunty Corrine "took on" over 20+ years (unpaid)
2. **Specific case studies** showing community support vs system failure
3. **Service critique** from someone who knows every service in town
4. **Economic analysis** in her own words: "tick and flick funding," "big black monies," "dollar signs"
5. **What Aunties actually need** (not what services think they need)
6. **Community-led model in action** - relationship-based, 24/7, crisis-responsive, culturally grounded

**Most Powerful Insight:**
> "I don't care if I get myself in trouble. A lot of these services got here, it's just all a big competition. We don't need that many services. We should make do with what we got. Don't keep bringing in new services because it's all about dollar signs. **Tick flick funding keep rolling in for what? Because we still got a lot of bad children on the streets.**"

This is community expertise challenging the entire service delivery model.

## PART 1: CASE STUDIES - Young People Aunty Corrine Supported

### **Case Study 1: "Bubbles" - The Boy with No Lunch**

**How it started:**
> "I took on a young fella that his mom died and supported him. Only reason why I end up with that kid, because he was going to school no lunch. My six children, we used to make lunch here for, drop it off every day. And they said to me one day, 'Oh mum, can you make one extra one for Bubbles?' That was a young fellow. So they used to take the lunch from here, but **he ended up coming here. He dropped the anchor here. Never went back to the family that he was living with.**"

**What Aunty provided:**
- Daily lunch (originally)
- Full-time accommodation
- Family belonging
- Stability

**What services provided:**
- Nothing documented

**Duration:**
- Long-term (until he was stable)

**Key insight:**
Community support started with **noticing** (no lunch at school) → **relationship** (kids bringing him lunch) → **trust** (he chose to stay) → **family** (dropped anchor)

**Services don't work this way.** They work intake → assessment → program placement.

---

### **Case Study 2: Steven - Cleveland to Mount Isa Pipeline**

**Background:**
> "This young fella here [Steven], his mother couldn't handle him. We supported his mom by phone, plus my daughter Jocelyn, when he was in Cleveland. **When he came outta Cleveland, he told them mob to send him straight back to Mount Isa. So we took this young fella on too, from Cleveland. Got him into school, got him off the streets. He had two page full of criminal history in Townsville.**"

**Age:** Just turned 21 (at time of interview)

**What Aunty provided:**
- Phone support to mother while he was in Cleveland
- Stable accommodation when released
- School enrollment
- "Got him off the streets"
- Family connection
- **Result:** Now living independently, no recent criminal charges

**What services provided:**
- Cleveland detention (made things worse)
- Youth Justice (minimal support post-release)

**What's missing:**
> Benjamin: "What do you think other services did with him?"
> Aunty: "Only just Youth Justice. But I'm gonna tell you about Youth Justice. When I was first dealing with Youth Justice, they used to have a lot of Aboriginal workers. **There's hardly anyone there now. It's only one old lady still there, Janet.**"

**Key insight:**
Cleveland released him with no support plan. He had to ASK to be sent to Mount Isa (where family was). Services didn't think about this. **Aunty provided the transition support that Youth Justice should have funded.**

### **Case Study 3: Terrance - Hiding from Child Protection**

**Background:**
> "Young Terrance, lived at my house. He hid from his mother 'cause she moved town. He didn't wanna follow her. He ended up in my house. **Then I had young Terrence, he was under child safety. He came here first.** They were just living down the road somewhere, but he dropped the anchor. Then his big brother was trying to make him go home. And I just said to him, 'Leave him. Don't force him to go, just leave him.' Made him cry and everything. So **he ended up dropping the anchor.**"

**What Aunty provided:**
- Safe place to stay when he didn't want to follow mother
- No pressure, no force
- Respected his choice
- Advocated to brother to leave him alone

**What child safety did:**
- Presumably knew he was under their care
- No indication they checked in or provided alternative placement
- He stayed with Aunty (unpaid kinship care)

**Key insight:**
Young people vote with their feet. They go where they feel safe. Terrance **chose** Aunty's house over forced placement. Child safety didn't seem to have alternatives that felt safe to him.

### **Case Study 4: Lockie Boot - Recent Placement**

**Background:**
> "I took on a little young fellow named Lockie Boot just recently. But he's back with his mum now. **Mother never ever supported me. She only brought a bottle of Coke and a packet of chips, and then he didn't wanna share with my children.** Which was wrong. I said, 'You know, whatever I buy or get and put in the house, you eat it too, so you shouldn't be nasty with these little people.' Because **we all learned to share in the house.**"

**What Aunty provided:**
- Full-time care
- Food, accommodation
- Teaching about sharing and family values
- Reunification with mother when safe

**What mother provided:**
- One bottle of Coke and one packet of chips (entire time)

**What services provided:**
> "Service would've helped me with the kids would've been Edmund Rice [Flexi School]."

**Key insight:**
Aunty not only cares for young people—she **teaches values** (sharing, respect) while caring. And she **works toward reunification** with family when possible. This is what trauma-informed, family-centered care actually looks like.

### **Case Study 5: The 25 "M Valley Boys" (MBBs)**

**Background:**
> "I took on all these young boys because of my son Norton. They used to call themselves the M Valley boys 'cause we live in Mornington Valley. All these boys kept coming here...I took on 25 young fellas. They used to run amok here. They were called the MBBs."

**What Aunty provided:**
- Went out at 3-4am to get kids off streets
- Brought them home
- Attended court with ALL of them
- "I never gave up on these children regardless of whatever they did wrong"
- "All they wanted was just someone to show 'em a lot of love and attention"

**What services provided:**
- Police harassed and arrested them
- Court processed them
- Various services "dealing with" them but not coordinating

**Key story - The food theft:**
> "My daughter said, 'Mom, guess what? The church gave all the boys food.' I checked that fridge and I looked at the food. I said, 'Nah, this wasn't provided by the church.' I walked up where all the boys were sleeping. I said, 'Where did all that food come from?' They broke into the Healy State School. Took all the food that they provide for our Aboriginal children to go to school. **So they all got in trouble over that. They all went to court over that.**"

**Aunty's response:**
- Confronted them immediately
- Made them accountable
- Went to court WITH them
- Continued supporting them
- Taught consequences AND love

**Key insight:**
Aunty provided **accountability WITH relationship**. She didn't abandon them when they did wrong. She made sure they faced consequences BUT stayed connected. **This is what "therapeutic" and "relational" actually means.**

Services provide consequences WITHOUT relationship. Or relationship WITHOUT accountability. Aunty provides both.

### **Case Study 6: Norton - Army Pathway (Success Story)**

**Background:**
> "My son Norton, when Sandra found out about the Army and the Navy came to town...Norton was the one that stepped up. He stopped the drugs. He did everything himself just to go into the army. **It took me two years to work with just Norton, but he got there eventually. He did go away.**"

**What Aunty provided:**
- Two years of sustained support
- Connection to Army opportunity (Sandra helped)
- Advocacy when systems tried to block access
- Family support while away

**What happened:**
- Norton joined Army
- Nephew fell sick with motor neuron disease
- Norton came home for funeral
- Never went back to Army
- **Then had major motorbike accident**

**What services did:**
- Army/Navy came to town (recruitment event)
- But held it at school, not community
- Limited accessibility

**Aunty's critique:**
> "They invited me to go there and I said, 'You need to come in here with your uniform on. Why aren't you advertising in the community? Don't talk to certain people, talk to everybody, regardless whether you're black, white or brindle.'"

**Key insight:**
It took **2 years** of family support for Norton to be ready. Services want quick outcomes. Aunty invested years. And when he came back (family crisis, then accident), she didn't see it as "failure"—she saw it as **life**. He made the choice, got the experience, came home when family needed him.

**This is long-term, culturally grounded support.** Not program completion rates.

### **Case Study 7: The Boy with Army Reserves Uniform Who Burnt It**

**Background:**
> "Another young person, we bought him the uniform and everything. He was in the Army Reserve. **You know when he wore his uniform, he got teased that much that he went and burnt that uniform and never went back.** Young Ashton, he got that much teased, you know, 'Big-noting yourself, who you trying to be, what you trying to make out?'"

**What Aunty provided:**
- Helped him join Army Reserves
- Bought uniform
- Support and encouragement

**What community did:**
- Lateral violence ("big-noting yourself")
- Teased him for trying to do well

**What services did:**
- Reserves program didn't protect him from lateral violence
- No cultural safety planning
- No family/community engagement to prevent this

**Key insight:**
**Lateral violence is real.** Services don't address it because they don't understand community dynamics. Aunty knows this happens but couldn't prevent it alone. This is why **collective community work** is needed—not just individual support.

To help young people succeed, you need to address:
1. Individual young person (Army Reserves did this)
2. Family support (Aunty did this)
3. **Community culture that tears down success** (NO ONE addressed this)

### **Case Study 8: Voice of Peace (VOP) Program - What Worked Until Funding Cut**

**Background:**
> "Amy the Peace Officer and young Lana (bless her soul) came here, knocked on my door. I told 'em to come back next day. **I pulled 15 boys outta my house. Out of the 15, they ended up with 6.** That's still pretty good numbers."

**What happened:**
- VOP program working well
- 6 boys actively participating
- **One boy took his own life** (devastating for community)
- Then: "When I rang up about the program VOP, **they lost the funding and it never happened again.**"

**Aunty's analysis:**
> "That VOP program should have been continuing still today, 2025. But because they've lost the funding, or government didn't want to fund it again. **Because you know when something start looking really good and deadly, you get the funding taken away. They don't want things to look great and good out here in Mount Isa.**"

**Key insight:**
This is **systems analysis** from someone who's seen it repeatedly:
- Community program works
- Young people engage
- Evidence of success
- **Funding gets cut**

**Why?** Aunty's theory: "They don't want things to look great and good."

Alternative theory: Short-term grant funding model ensures programs die just when they're working.

**Either way: Criminal waste of community investment.**

### **Case Study 9: The Boy Who Got Punched at McDonald's**

**Background:**
> "I had a young fellow, he got attacked at McDonald's. An uncle put him in a headlock and punched his head in at the age of 15. All these boys that were with him, they all ran home and told me. I was sitting there worrying about this kid because they took him back to his house. But then they decided to have a big barbecue. So the kids snuck away. **Who bangs on the gate? The kid that got punched in the head and put in a headlock ended up here. I made him stay in my house. I hid him in my house, got onto child safety and to the police station. And the kid never got sent to Cleveland, even though he was one of those naughty kids.**"

**What Aunty did:**
- Boys trusted her enough to run and tell her
- She worried (even though not "her" kid)
- Kid trusted her enough to come to her house
- She hid him (kept him safe from violent uncle)
- Contacted child safety AND police
- Advocated so he DIDN'T go to Cleveland

**What child safety did:**
- Called Aunty to ask about mother's history
- Used Aunty's knowledge to assess situation
- **Extracted Aunty's expertise, didn't compensate her**

**Key insight:**
Child safety NEEDED Aunty's knowledge to make good decision. They called HER to understand family. But she's not employed by them. They extract community knowledge without compensating expertise.

**This happens constantly.**

### **Case Study 10: The Boy Who Sat for 4 Days Silent**

**Background:**
> "I had another young fella come in. **He sat in my house for four days. I never questioned him. I just kept walking past him.** And then I said to him, 'Are you okay?' Then he told me that his uncle wanted to stab him. I said, 'Right, well, I'm gonna ring the police.' Got the police involved, and guess what, the kid's doing okay now."

**What Aunty did:**
- Let him sit silently for 4 days
- No pressure, no interrogation
- Just presence
- When ready, he told her
- She took action (police, protection)

**What this demonstrates:**
- **Trauma-informed practice**: Don't force disclosure
- **Patience**: 4 days of just being present
- **Safety**: Kid knew he could be there without explaining
- **Action when needed**: Once she knew, she acted

**This is professional-level crisis intervention.** Counselors are taught this in university. Aunty does it intuitively because **she understands trauma and relationship.**

## PART 2: SYSTEMS FAILURES - Documented in Interview

### **Failure 1: Youth Justice Losing Aboriginal Staff**

> "When I was first dealing with Youth Justice, they used to have a lot of Aboriginal workers. **There's hardly anyone there now. It's only one old lady still there, Janet.**"

**Impact:**
- Cultural knowledge lost
- Community relationships severed
- Young people don't see themselves in staff

---

### **Failure 2: Services Not Coordinating**

> "You had five services dealing with one child. **Would never come to the table, the five services, to sit down at the table and talk about where they are with this one child. It was like a big competition here.** This service better than that service."

**Impact:**
- Duplication of effort
- Gaps in support
- Young person confused about who to trust
- Services competing instead of collaborating

---

### **Failure 3: Services Don't Know the Community**

> "The saddest part about Mount Isa, we got a lot of people here in Mount Isa. **Why do we have to have everybody coming from out of town doing all the jobs here? Because all these people gotta learn about our little black children. About their families. When we got people in the community, give them a go, give them a job.** Maybe there wouldn't be so much problems in the community."

**Impact:**
- Staff don't know families, culture, history
- Community members who have knowledge can't get jobs
- "As soon as they see the color of your skin, you don't even have a chance"

---

### **Failure 4: Co-Responders (Aunty's Strongest Critique)**

> "The co-responders, I don't care what anyone say, **that is one service we don't need in this community.**"

**Why?** (From earlier conversation):
- Don't arrive before police (should be first)
- Walk through houses without listening to community
- Paid close to $60/hour
- "At the end of the day, we give our kids a paycheck"

**Benjamin's observation:**
> "After I spoke to you yesterday, we had a meeting with them. There was a lead person who walked in and immediately almost had this front of wanting to challenge Brody on what he's doing. It was a very obvious power play."

**Key insight:**
Co-responders positioned as competition/threat to community programs, not support. **Power dynamics, not partnership.**

---

### **Failure 5: No After-Hours Support**

> "I know they all here to try and support and help our kids. The saddest part about it, **a lot of things happen at night. It starts from five in the afternoon. Kids start walking on the streets five o'clock in the afternoon. We've got more services working during the day. We need 'em at night.**"

**Impact:**
- Aunty and community dealing with crises 5pm-5am
- Services work 9-5
- Young people need support when services are closed
- Aunty goes out at 3-4am to get kids off streets (unpaid)

---

### **Failure 6: Tick-and-Flick Funding**

> "A lot of these services got here, it's just all a big competition. We don't need that many services. **Don't keep bringing in new services because it's all about dollar signs. Tick flick funding keep rolling in for what? Because we still got a lot of bad children on the streets. We still got a lot of bad children not even in school.**"

**What "tick and flick" means:**
- Service sees kid once
- Counts them as "served"
- Reports numbers to funder
- Gets next year's funding
- Kid still on streets

**Key insight:**
Aunty sees the economic model clearly. Services profit from high numbers, not from success. **Perverse incentive.**

---

### **Failure 7: No Infrastructure for Young People**

> "We don't have really a park for our kids. The town pool is too expensive. There's no activities after hours for kids to go to. **Like I said, you gotta join services to for your kids to go to these places, which is really sad.**"

**Aunty's advocacy:**
> "I went into the police station one day and said, 'Where am I supposed to take all these kids? Put 'em all on a dog leash and take 'em to the dog park? **Because there's more dog parks in Mount Isa than any other parks.**'"

**Key insight:**
Mount Isa has invested in dog infrastructure, not kid infrastructure. Then criminalizes kids for being on streets. **Set up to fail.**

---

### **Failure 8: Cleveland Youth Detention Centre**

**Documented in earlier conversation:**
- Family visitation denied (grandson said he "didn't want to visit" but he was never asked)
- Mental health crises not addressed (boy walking around naked, suicidal, sent back to Cleveland after 5 minutes at hospital)
- Young people come out worse than they went in

**Aunty's role:**
- Phone support to families while kids in Cleveland
- Receives kids when released (like Steven)
- Advocates for young people to NOT be sent there

**Key insight:**
Aunty sees Cleveland as place to AVOID, not "service." She tries to keep kids out. When they go in, she's the one providing family connection that Cleveland blocks.

---

### **Failure 9: When Things Work, Funding Gets Cut**

**Pattern Aunty has observed:**
- VOP program working → funding cut
- Good staff at Youth Justice → "pushed down" when doing well
- CASH rehab working (Aboriginal-run) → Salvation Army takes over, fails
- Flexi school working → changed policies, waiting lists

**Aunty's theory:**
> "You know when something start looking really good and deadly, **you get the funding taken away. They don't want things to look great and good out here in Mount Isa.**"

**Key insight:**
Whether intentional or not, short-term funding model ensures programs die when they're most effective. **Community learns: Don't trust government investment.**

---

### **Failure 10: Parents Not Supported**

> "Government pay a lot of our people to make sure their kids get to school, get education, everything. **Too much drugs, too much alcohol, and too much gambling.** Sometime I go uptown, kids sitting on the outside waiting for their mothers to come out. They wanna gamble all the money. They got no money to buy food. **So these kids gotta survive themselves, that's why they do a lot of breaking in and be naughty.**"

**Aunty's solution (rejected by government):**
> "We suggested when the kids get in trouble, **stop their Centrelink.** And they said, well, if you stop their Centrelink, the kids still miss out. And that is true what they're saying. Then we said **when the kids get in trouble, the parents should get in trouble too. They should get chucked in jail too with the kid** to make them wake up and realize."

**Key insight:**
Aunty sees root cause (parental substance abuse, neglect) but has no power to intervene. Services work with kids individually, don't address family systems. **Merry-go-round.**

## PART 3: THE COMMUNITY-LED MODEL (What Aunty Actually Does)

### **Element 1: Relationship-Based, Not Program-Based**

**How young people arrive:**
- They come on their own ("dropped anchor")
- Other kids bring them
- They run to Aunty in crisis
- Parents/family send them

**No intake forms. No assessments. No eligibility criteria.**

Just: **"Are you okay?"**

---

### **Element 2: 24/7, Crisis-Responsive**

> "Me and the old man used to get in our car, go get 'em off the street at 3-4am. Discipline them 'cause they could have did a lot of damage."

**Compare to services:**
- 9-5 operations
- After-hours crisis = police or nothing
- No flexibility for when young people actually need support

---

### **Element 3: Accountability WITH Relationship**

**Example: The food theft from Healy State School**
- Aunty investigated when story didn't add up
- Made boys admit what they did
- They went to court
- She went WITH them to court
- Continued supporting them after

**Not either/or. Both/and.**

Services tend to be:
- Accountability without relationship (police, courts), OR
- Relationship without accountability (some youth services)

Aunty: **Both.**

---

### **Element 4: Teaching Values While Caring**

> "I said to Lockie Boot, 'Whatever I buy or get and put in the house, you eat it too, so you shouldn't be nasty with these little people.' **Because we all learned to share in the house.**"

**Not just accommodation. Not just food. Cultural transmission:**
- Sharing
- Respect
- Family
- Responsibility

---

### **Element 5: Long-Term (Years, Not Weeks)**

> "It took me two years to work with just Norton."

**Services measure:**
- 6-week programs
- 12-month support plans
- "Program completion"

**Aunty measures:**
- However long it takes
- Still there when they come back
- Lifelong connection

---

### **Element 6: Trauma-Informed (Intuitively)**

**Example: Boy who sat silently for 4 days**
- No interrogation
- Just presence
- Waited until he was ready
- Then acted

**This is textbook trauma-informed practice.** Aunty does it because **she understands people.**

---

### **Element 7: Advocacy in Systems**

Aunty navigates:
- Police
- Courts
- Child safety
- Schools
- Mental health
- Housing

**For every kid. Unpaid.**

When services need community knowledge, they call Aunty. When young people need someone to fight for them, they come to Aunty.

---

### **Element 8: Cultural Authority**

When Aunty speaks, people listen. Not because she has credentials. Because she has:
- **Credibility** (25+ young people supported)
- **Knowledge** (knows every family, every service, every failure)
- **Courage** ("I don't care if I get myself in trouble")
- **Results** (kids doing okay, didn't go to Cleveland, got into school)

**Services rent Aboriginal consultants. Community has Aunties.**

## PART 4: WHAT AUNTIES NEED (In Their Own Words)

### **Not More Services**

> "I don't care if I get myself in trouble. A lot of these services got here, it's just all a big competition. **We don't need that many services. We should make do with what we got.** Don't keep bringing in new services because it's all about dollar signs."

---

### **Infrastructure, Not Programs**

> Benjamin: "So more less services and more facilities and infrastructure?"
>
> Aunty: **"Yes. That's a good point, I reckon. Anyway, me personally talking."**

**What that means:**
- Parks (not dog parks)
- Affordable pool access
- After-hours activities
- Public transport so kids can get to school/activities

---

### **Voices Behind Her**

> "And I tell a lot of people, I will support you, but **I need voices behind me to make things work in this community.**"

**This is the "I got the voice but I got no backup" theme.**

Aunty doesn't need:
- ❌ More training
- ❌ Consultation opportunities
- ❌ Advisory roles

She needs:
- ✅ Other people speaking up too
- ✅ Collective power
- ✅ Organized advocacy
- ✅ Platform to amplify her voice

**This is what JusticeHub infrastructure provides.**

---

### **Compensation for Expertise**

Not stated explicitly in this interview, but clear from context:

- She does professional-level work (crisis intervention, case coordination, advocacy)
- Services get paid hundreds of thousands
- She gets $0

**Implied need:** Pay her what the work is worth.

---

### **Services to Actually Work at Night**

> "We've got more services working during the day. **We need 'em at night.** Kids start walking on the streets five o'clock in the afternoon."

**Practical need:** After-hours crisis response that's not just police.

---

### **Local People in Jobs**

> "Why do we have to have everybody coming from out of town doing all the jobs here? **When we got people in the community, give them a go, give them a job.** Maybe there wouldn't be so much problems in the community."

**System change needed:**
- Hire local Aboriginal people
- Not fly-in/fly-out workers
- Stop discrimination: "As soon as they see the color of your skin, you don't even have a chance"

---

### **Long-Term Funding for What Works**

> "That VOP program should have been continuing still today, 2025."

**Implied need:**
- Stop cutting funding when programs work

- Stability for community to plan
