# Last Tuesday on Country: Fifty-Five Judges, Six Postcards, One Frame

Last Tuesday, fifty-five judges and magistrates sat on Country at Atnarpa, Loves Creek Station, on Eastern Arrernte land an hour and a half east of Mparntwe. They were welcomed onto Country by Elders. They walked the homestead. They held six postcards that fit in a chamber pocket. They made a decision under the same constraint the system gives a sentencing officer: limited time, incomplete information, real consequences.

It was not a tour. It was an argument, built carefully, in the only voice entitled to make it.

The voice belonged to Kristy Bloomfield and Tanya Turner. Kristy is a Central Arrernte, Eastern Arrernte and Alyawarra Traditional Owner. Her grandfather was Stolen Generations, taken to the Bungalow in Alice Springs. Tanya is an Eastern Arrernte woman who studied law at UWA, worked as an associate at the Supreme Court of Victoria, and came home. Together they built Oonchiumpa from cultural authority first, legal knowledge second. The order matters.

Before the day began, every judge received one story. Eight minutes long. Tanya's voice. One sentence inside it the day would hang on:

> Our young people are just collateral in a bigger issue. The issue doesn't sit with them.

Read that sentence once and a courtroom stops being neutral.

## What the day was designed to do

The activity ran thirty minutes. Seven phases. The bench was asked to do something they almost never do: hold a young person's words in their hand before they hold the case file.

Phase one, grounding from Elders. No facilitator script there. The land speaks first. Phase two, every judge received one postcard. Six postcards in total, six voices. Phase three, a decision under incomplete information. Phase four, reveal facts one at a time. The kind of facts that live under the fold of a court file. He hasn't been home in three weeks. There is no transport. He trusts one person who is not in the file. Phase five, community authority speaks to what justice looks like from this place. Not culture explained. Justice reframed. Phase six, the alternative becomes visible. Phase seven, the bench leaves Country unsettled, postcards in hand.

The rule the day was built on: cultural authority leads, it does not decorate. Anywhere a facilitator script could go, Kristy or Tanya's words go instead.

## The six postcards

The postcards are the architecture. Six cards. Two cultural authorities. Four young voices. One trust story. Each card a frame given priority over the file.

**Card one. Kristy and Tanya.** The founders. The sentence. The reason the rest of the cards exist.

**Card two. Jackquann and Nigel, together.** Both fourteen. Both from Mparntwe. Both have been to detention. Asked what would help, neither boy asked for punishment. Jackquann said one word, *programs*. Nigel said he wants to go to school every day so he can be a footy player. Two boys. Two answers. Neither answer the system collects.

**Card three. Jackquann.** Lives at Upper Camp with his grandfather. Has been to the Alice Springs Detention Centre. Described it plainly: *"At six o'clock you get locked down. You wait till tomorrow."* Asked what would stop him getting in trouble, he said *"Looking after my family."* He wants a job. He wants to start his own family one day. The card the bench holds when the conversation turns to deterrence.

**Card four. Nigel.** Lives at a station in Double Camp. Has been to Don Dale, in Darwin. Talking to the judge, he panics. Asked what it feels like being away from family, he said *"Bad. Like, going away from my family and stuff."* Oonchiumpa picks him up, takes him to school, gives him consistency. That consistency is the intervention.

**Card five. Laquisha.** Sixteen. Goes to St Joseph's. Has been to Darwin youth detention, fifteen hundred kilometres from home. Twelve-minute phone calls. Two-hour waits between calls. Lockdown at six-thirty every night. Asked why young people get in trouble, she answered in one word: *"Oppression."* Asked what court feels like, she said court is scary because you don't know whether you are getting out, and your family is sitting there watching you.

**Card six. Xavier, told by Fred Campbell.** Xavier was one of Oonchiumpa's first clients. Other services distanced when the relationship got hard. Oonchiumpa did not. Fred says: *"He trusts us. We earned that trust."* Xavier was released from youth detention at the start of the year and has not been back. He helped build Stretch Beds from recycled plastic. He bought his first pair of shoes with his pay and ran home to show his family. Fred says he is quite capable of building on his own and sharing that with other kids.

That is the deck. Six cards. No abstraction. No category. No file.

## What the bench was asked to carry

Three things, named in the pre-trip letter sent to every judge a week before the flight to Mparntwe.

An open question they were already holding about the young people coming before their bench. A willingness to be uncomfortable. The sentence, already read.

Three things to take home with them.

The six postcards as a deck. The ALMA search for their own local government area, programs that already exist near them. A direct line back to Kristy and Tanya, if they want one.

This is what the day was designed to put in chambers. Not a souvenir. A field kit.

## What changes when sentencing officers read first, then decide

There is a long argument in youth justice about *what works*. The argument is mostly held in evidence reviews and Productivity Commission tables. They are useful. They are also distant from the courtroom on the morning a fourteen-year-old appears before a magistrate who has eight minutes for the matter.

Last Tuesday's frame was simple. Move the bench, for one day, into the place the file describes. Put the young person's own words in their hand before the file. Show them the alternative that already exists in postcode 0870. Then hand them a search, and ask them to look at their own jurisdiction the same way.

The reflection instrument follows. Three questions, sent thirty days after the trip. Mirrored to three questions sent before. Open text, not Likert scales. Withdrawal in fourteen days, no reason required. No HREC pathway, because this is not research. It is reflection. Held by JusticeHub and Oonchiumpa, reviewed by Kristy or Tanya before any line is published. A second cohort sits on Country in mid-September. The instrument runs again then.

We will not claim the bench was changed. We will report what they said, in the words they offered, with their consent, in the time it takes them to write it.

## What last Tuesday extends

Oonchiumpa is one of four anchor communities JusticeHub holds in active relationship. Mparntwe / Oonchiumpa, Palm Island Community Company, BG Fit, Minjerribah Moorgumpin Elders-in-Council. Four places. Four forms of community-controlled practice. One platform that holds them, and many more after them.

The pattern is the same in each. Money flows inward. Evidence flows outward. Story stays sovereign at the centre. Last Tuesday was the proof, not the program. The program is the four anchors, and the dozens of other community-controlled organisations across the country already doing the work that detention has been mistaken for.

The bench has the cards now. The cards point back to Atnarpa, and forward to whatever local search the judge runs in their own jurisdiction next week.

Kristy's grandfather was taken to the Bungalow because his Country was a colony's blind spot. Last Tuesday, fifty-five judges and magistrates stood on that same Country, listening. The cards in their pockets were drawn by young people who have not yet been listened to by the system that decides their futures.

That is the work. The work has begun.
