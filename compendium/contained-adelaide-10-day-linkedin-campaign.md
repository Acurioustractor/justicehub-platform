## What the original post teaches us

The launch post (2,193 reactions, 164 comments, 218 reposts) worked because of five mechanics, not luck:

1. **A physical impossibility made real.** "We bought a shipping container" is a sentence nobody scrolls past. The audacity IS the hook.
2. **The two-room image.** One photo did more than the whole text: floral bedspread next to concrete platform. People reacted to the picture, then read.
3. **Named humans.** Isaiah scratching the walls. Maria crying for kids she'd never meet. Comments quoted these back verbatim months later.
4. **A who-needs-to-walk-through list.** "Premiers who celebrate arrests. Treasurers funding failure." People tagged those exact people in the comments.
5. **The comments became the distribution.** "Bring it to Perth", "Bring it to Tassie", Jonty Bush MP asking "where in Queensland?". The audience told us the next ten posts.

The campaign is those five mechanics, run in sequence, with the scarcity turned up.

## The frame

We are not selling tickets. There are no tickets. The container holds 30 minutes at a time and most of Adelaide will never get inside. The campaign sells three doors, in this order of prominence:

- **Nominate** someone who needs to walk through it (a magistrate, an MP, a CEO, a journalist, an educator, in SA or any state). This is the hero CTA. It makes the audience the recruiters.
- **EOI** for one of the limited walkthrough slots. Hard to get. Worded that way.
- **Stand with it** (funding, hosting the next city, partnering, spreading). The wide-open door. Everyone qualifies.

All three land on the one form, route to Engagement → Identified, and we triage by role tags from there. Scarcity at the experience, abundance at the relationship.
