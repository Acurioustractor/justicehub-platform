## International Best Practices

### 1. New Zealand - Oranga Tamariki

<div className="grid grid-cols-3 gap-4 my-6">
  <StatCard value="60%" label="Reduction in MƒÅori youth custody" color="green" size="sm" trend="down" />
  <StatCard value="2010-2020" label="Timeframe" color="blue" size="sm" />
  <StatCard value="$250M" label="Annual investment" color="purple" size="sm" />
</div>

**Key lessons:**
- MƒÅori-led family group conferencing
- Cultural healing integrated into justice responses
- National Indigenous data sovereignty framework
- Community-controlled decision-making
- Sustained government investment over 10+ years

### 2. Canada - Gladue Principles

**Key innovation:** Sentencing framework considering Indigenous background, trauma, and systemic factors

**Impact:**
- Alternative sentencing options for Indigenous youth
- Community-based healing programs
- Reduced incarceration rates in pilot communities

### 3. USA - Tribal Youth Program

**Approach:** Tribal communities designing and delivering their own youth justice programs

**Results:**
- 40-60% reduction in recidivism
- Culturally-grounded interventions
- Youth engaged in cultural activities, language, land

### 4. Colombia - Indigenous Justice Systems

**Recognition:** Indigenous communities' authority to administer justice according to their own laws

**Outcomes:**
- Lower recidivism than state system
- Community healing and restoration prioritized
- Cultural preservation and sovereignty strengthened

### 5. Norway - Youth Care Homes

**Model:** Small, home-like environments with therapeutic support

**Results:**
- 20% recidivism rate (vs 60-70% in prison-like facilities)
- Focus on rehabilitation, education, mental health
- Young people maintain family connections

## Australian Success Stories

### Bourke Justice Reinvestment (NSW)

<Callout type="success" title="10-Year Results">
  The most comprehensive justice reinvestment project in Australia with exceptional outcomes.
</Callout>

| Metric | 2013 (Baseline) | 2023 (10 Years) | Change | Source |
|--------|----------------|----------------|--------|---------|
| Youth in detention (average) | 5.6 | 1.2 | **-79%** | NSW Bureau of Crime Statistics |
| Youth court appearances | 234/year | 98/year | **-58%** | NSW Courts |
| School attendance rate | 62% | 80% | **+29%** | NSW Education Dept |
| Cost of detention | $5.1M/year | $1.1M/year | **-$4M saved annually** | Productivity Commission |

**What they did:**
- Diverted $1.8M from detention costs to community programs
- Created 15 local jobs in youth support roles
- Built Maranguka Justice Reinvestment Project
- Community-led decision-making and program design
- Cultural connection and healing prioritized

**ROI: $2.22 saved for every $1 invested**

### Oonchiumpa Consultancy (Alice Springs, NT)

**Focus:** Cultural camps and youth diversion programs for Central Australian Aboriginal young people

**Impact:**
- 50+ at-risk youth engaged annually
- Cultural connection strengthened through on-Country programs
- Alternative to detention pathways created
- Featured in $250M Central Australia Blueprint

**Model:** Community-designed programs with proper compensation for Indigenous expertise

### MMEIC - Minjerribah Moorgumpin Elders-in-Council (North Stradbroke Island, QLD)

<Callout type="success" title="2017 NAIDOC Award Winner">
  Over 20 years of community-led programs with documented impact on youth justice outcomes.
</Callout>

**Programs:**
- Cultural mentoring and Elders' guidance
- On-Country healing camps
- Traditional knowledge transmission
- Community justice initiatives

**Recognition:** Minjerribah Award for community work during NAIDOC Week 2017

### Palm Island Community Company (QLD)

**Bwgcolman Youth Program:**
- Intensive support for high-risk youth
- Cultural activities and mentoring
- Alternative education pathways
- Family support and case management

**Recent funding:** $235K from Queensland Government (2023)

### Mounty Yarns (Mount Druitt, NSW)

**Approach:** Youth-led storytelling and justice advocacy

**Innovation:** Young people creating their own narratives and solutions

**Partnerships:** Just Reinvest NSW collaboration

## Evidence on Grant Writing Burden

<Callout type="warning" title="The Hidden Cost">
  Practitioners spend **30-40% of their time** writing unsuccessful grant applications, diverting resources from service delivery.
</Callout>

**Academic sources:**
- Nonprofit Quarterly (2022): "The Grant Writing Trap" - 35% of time on average
- Stanford Social Innovation Review (2021): Overhead costs including grant writing 25-40%
- Australian Council of Social Service (2020): 30% average for small community organizations

**What this means:**
- $300K salary = $90-120K lost to grant writing
- Time not spent working with young people
- Perpetual insecurity and short-term planning
- Extractive relationships with funders

**JusticeHub solution:** Revenue-generating platform reduces dependency on grants

## Data Sovereignty Framework

<Callout type="info" title="Indigenous Data Sovereignty">
  Indigenous peoples' right to control collection, ownership, and application of data about their communities, lands, and resources.
</Callout>

**CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance:**
- **C**ollective Benefit - Data used for collective Indigenous benefit
- **A**uthority to Control - Indigenous control over data ecosystems
- **R**esponsibility - How data reflects relationships and obligations
- **E**thics - Minimizing harm, maximizing benefit

**JusticeHub Implementation:**
- Layered consent system (individual, family, community, nation)
- Community-controlled data access
- Cultural protocols embedded in platform
- Revenue sharing from data use
